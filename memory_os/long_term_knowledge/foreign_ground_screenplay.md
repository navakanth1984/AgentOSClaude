## FADE IN:

EXT. EDISON, NEW JERSEY-SUBURBAN STREET-EARLY MORNING

A quiet, tree-lined street in an upper-middle-class Indian-American neighborhood. Manicured lawns. Toyota Camrys and Honda Odysseys in driveways. A HINDU TEMPLE visible at the end of the block, its gopuram rising incongruously above the American rooftops.

## SUPER: "Edison, New Jersey"

Dawn light catches the windows of a handsome two-story colonial. American flag on the porch. Rangoli pattern on the doorstep. The mailbox reads: REDDY.

A SPRINKLER system kicks on, catching the first light. The gentle hiss of water on grass. America at its most pristine.

INT. REDDY HOUSE-ARJUN'S BEDROOM-CONTINUOUS

A teenage boy's room that maps the coordinates of a dual identity. Cricket posters — Sachin Tendulkar, Virat Kohli — hang alongside LeBron James and Tom Brady. A PlayStation 5 glows in standby. Stacks of Marvel graphic novels. A half-built LEGO Millennium Falcon on the desk. An iPhone charging on the nightstand reads 7:42 AM.

ARJUN REDDY (14) sleeps with the covers pulled completely over his head, a cocoon against the world. He is lanky, fair-skinned, with a mop of unruly black hair. A boy balanced perfectly between two cultures, aware of neither's full weight.

His mother's voice floats up from downstairs — warm as ghee, insistent as a temple bell.

PRIYA (O.S.) (warm, lilting Telugu accent) Arjun, baby, your father is leaving for work. Come eat something. At least one dosa.

No response. His mother's footsteps recede. Arjun smiles under the covers — the satisfied grin of a boy who knows exactly how far he can push the boundaries of maternal love. He has won this small battle. Again. As he does every morning of his comfortable, unshakeable, taken-for-granted life.

## INT. HOSPITAL-ROOM-NIGHT

A curtain pulled back. Two beds. Two forms beneath white sheets. The room is cold and humming with machines that have been turned off.

Arjun stands between the beds. He reaches out and touches his mother's hand beneath the sheet. It is cold. He recoils — not from horror but from the disconnect between what his hand feels and what his brain knows. This hand made him dosas this morning. This hand waved from the doorway. This hand is not supposed to be cold.

He doesn't touch his father. He stands at Harish's bedside and just looks at the shape beneath the sheet — the outline of a man who was, twelve hours ago, alive and making jokes about New Jersey.

ARJUN (whispering) I should have come down for breakfast, Dad. You wanted to say goodbye and I was playing a stupid game.

He backs away. Pushes through the curtain. In the corridor, he slides down the wall until he's sitting on the cold floor, knees drawn to his chest, making himself as small as possible. The social worker approaches, but the police officer holds her back — a gesture that says: give the boy a minute.

Arjun presses his face into his knees and silently shakes. He makes no sound. The grief is too enormous for sound.

Arjun takes out his mother's passport from under his pillow. Opens it. Stares at her photo. Then he takes out his phone — charged from the car battery rig — and opens the voice memo app. He scrolls. There: a recording from six months ago. His mother's voice, recorded accidentally when Arjun pocket-dialed her during cricket practice.

He puts in one AirPod. Presses play.

PRIYA (V.O.) (distant, tinny, mixed with crowd noise) Arjun! ARJUN! Beautiful shot, baby! Did you see that, Harish? Our boy just hit a four!

* * *

HARISH (V.O.) (laughing) I saw it! Left-handed cover drive — he gets that from me, you know.

PRIYA (V.O.) From you? You were a bowler! He gets his batting from MY side. My father was a—

The recording cuts out. Forty-three seconds of his parents being alive.

Arjun listens to it again. And again. And again. Tears run silently down his face and onto the cotton mattress. He doesn't make a sound. He has learned to grieve quietly, in small doses, in the dark, where no one can see the boy behind the strength.

A small hand reaches across the mattress. Raju — not asleep after all — finds Arjun's hand in the dark and holds it. Says nothing. Just holds it.

EXT. IPPATTLA-THATHA'S GARDEN-MORNING

Behind Srinivas's compound, there's a small garden — a few mango trees, a neem, some flowering jasmine. Thatha's domain. He comes here every morning, pushed in his wheelchair by whoever is available.

Arjun arrives. He's been coming here every third morning, without being asked, without telling Krishna. He wheels Thatha to his favorite spot under the mango tree.

THATHA (in Telugu) You come again.

ARJUN (in Telugu, improving daily) Of course, Thatha. Who else will listen to your stories?

Thatha chuckles. Pats the ground beside his wheelchair. Arjun sits.

* * *

THATHA (in Telugu, Arjun understanding most) Your father. When he was your age. He sat here. Same tree. Same spot. He said to me: "Nanna, I don't want to be a farmer. I want to build things. Machines. Computers." I told him: "Harish, in this family, we grow what the earth gives us. That's enough." But it wasn't enough for him. He wanted more. And I... I was too proud to understand that wanting more is not the same as wanting to leave us.

He pauses. A long pause, weighted with decades.

THATHA (CONT'D) (in Telugu) I made a mistake. I chose Srinivas over Harish because Srinivas stayed and Harish left. But staying in one place is not loyalty. And leaving is not betrayal. I learned this too late. Your father died thinking I chose his brother over him. That... that is my sin. And it will follow me.

Arjun doesn't fully understand every word, but he understands the weight. He puts his hand on his grandfather's knee.

ARJUN (in Telugu) Thatha. Dad always talked about coming back. Every year he said: "This Diwali, we'll go." He didn't hate you. He loved you. He was just... afraid you wouldn't love him back.

Thatha is still for a long time. Then his hand covers Arjun's. Weathered skin over young skin. Seventy-eight

* * *

years touching fourteen years. The circle, closing.

EXT. IPPATTLA-VILLAGE WELL-DAY

Arjun encounters KAVITHA at the village well, filling water pots. She's the only person in Srinivas's family who's shown him kindness.

KAVITHA (in English) You're getting better at Telugu. Thatha says you visited him yesterday and told him a joke. He laughed for twenty minutes.

ARJUN Raju taught me the joke. Something about a farmer, a crow, and a politician. I'm not sure I got the punchline right, but Thatha didn't seem to care.

KAVITHA (laughing) He doesn't care about punchlines. He cares that you come. You know, he makes Suresh wheel him to the cricket ground every evening now. Just to watch you practice. Suresh hates it. Which makes Thatha enjoy it even more.

They share a laugh. Then Kavitha's expression grows serious.

KAVITHA (CONT'D) Arjun, I want you to know — what my mother did when you arrived, turning you away... I'm sorry. I should have said something. I didn't because... in this house, women don't speak against the mother-in-law. It's not an excuse. It's just the truth.

ARJUN

* * *

It's okay, Kavitha. I ended up exactly where I was supposed to be.

KAVITHA (with admiration) You did. And you should know — my father is not the same man he was when you arrived. He watches your cricket matches. He doesn't cheer. But he watches. That's something, for him.

A loaded beat. Then she picks up her water pot, balances it on her head with effortless grace, and walks away. Arjun watches her go — a young woman navigating the strictures of a traditional household with quiet intelligence.

EXT. IPPATTLA CRICKET GROUND-TRAINING-EVENING

The team practices intensely for the tournament. Krishna runs drills. Arjun works with Raju on his bowling — helping him add an arm ball to his repertoire.

ARJUN Okay, same action, same everything, but this time keep your wrist straight instead of flicking it. The ball will go straight on instead of turning. They won't know the difference from your run-up.

Raju tries. The ball sails over the batsman's head. Both boys wince.

RAJU Maybe I keep to leg spin only, anna.

ARJUN Try again. YouTube Shane Warne didn't get the flipper right on the first try either.

* * *

Raju grins. Tries again. Better. The ball pitches on a length and goes straight. The batsman, expecting spin, plays for the turn and misses. Clean bowled.

RAJU (screaming) DID YOU SEE THAT? ANNA! DID YOU SEE?

Arjun grabs him in a headlock. They roughhouse, laughing. Krishna watches from the boundary, arms folded, a quiet smile on his face.

## INT. KRISHNA'S HOUSE-LATE NIGHT

Everyone is asleep. Arjun sits on the veranda, looking at the stars. Krishna emerges with two tumblers of warm milk.

KRISHNA Can't sleep?

ARJUN Sometimes I think about them at night. During the day, it's easier. There's cricket, and Raju won't shut up, and Lakshmi aunty keeps putting food in front of me. But at night... it gets quiet. And that's when I hear them.

KRISHNA Hear them?

ARJUN Not literally. It's like... echoes. My mom's voice calling me for breakfast. My dad's car pulling out of the driveway. I hear the life we had. And then I hear the silence where it used to be.

Krishna sits. Sips his milk. He doesn't rush to fill the silence with platitudes. He respects Arjun enough to let the grief breathe.

* * *

KRISHNA When Harish left for America, I used to hear his voice too. At night. His laugh. He had a very specific laugh — big, loud, like thunder. I would hear it in the wind sometimes. And I thought I was going crazy. But my mother told me: "The people we love never leave us. They just change the form of their presence." I didn't understand it then. I was fifteen. But now I think I do.

ARJUN What form do they take?

KRISHNA (thinking) Everything. The way you hold a bat — that's Harish. The way you make Chinna laugh — that's your mother, I think, even though I never met her. The way you fight without fighting — that's both of them. They are in you, Arjun. Not as ghosts. As ingredients.

Arjun absorbs this. It's the most beautiful thing anyone has said to him since his parents died. He doesn't cry. He smiles. A small, midnight smile that Krishna catches and returns.

ARJUN Krishna uncle? Thank you. For... everything. But especially for talking about them like they're still real. In America, everyone talks about them in the past tense. Like they've been filed away somewhere. But you talk about Dad like he's just in the next room.

KRISHNA In this village, the dead are always in the next room. We put

* * *

garlands on their photos every day. We cook their favorite food on their birthdays. We tell their stories to their grandchildren. In your America, I think people try to move on from death. Here, we move WITH it.

They sit in comfortable silence, two people sharing a night and a loss, the milk growing cold in their hands, the stars turning slowly above them.

EXT. IPPATTLA-VILLAGE MARKET-DAY

A weekly market day. Vendors set up stalls along the main road. Vegetables, spices, plastic toys, fabric, tools. The village buzzes with activity.

Arjun helps Lakshmi carry groceries. He haggles with a vegetable vendor in halting Telugu — getting ripped off, but enjoying the attempt. The vendor, charmed, throws in extra tomatoes for free.

LAKSHMI (in Telugu, impressed) You haggled! In Telugu! Badly, but you haggled! Tomorrow I'm sending you to buy chicken. Those butchers have been cheating me for fifteen years.

They pass a STATIONERY STALL. Arjun stops. He buys a notebook and pen. Later, at home, he begins writing — a letter, in English, to his parents. He writes about the village, about Krishna, about Raju, about cricket, about the morning field, about Lakshmi's gongura chicken. He writes what he cannot say aloud. He writes what the night silence demands of him.

He tears out the pages. Folds them. Puts them in the envelope with his parents' passports. A letter to the dead. The most honest letter he will ever write.

## EXT. IPPATTLA CRICKET GROUND-SUNSET

* * *

After a training session. The team has dispersed. Arjun sits alone on the pitch, cross-legged, the bat across his knees. The sun is setting. The ground is empty.

He closes his eyes. When he opens them, for just a moment — a trick of the light, a shift of the golden hour — he sees something: a figure standing at the boundary. A tall man in a cricket uniform. Smiling.

Arjun blinks. The figure is gone. Just the empty field and the dying light.

He stands. Picks up the bat. Walks home. His step is lighter than it's been in weeks.

INT. GOVERNMENT SCHOOL-CLASSROOM-DAY

Arjun sits in the back of the village school classroom, visiting as a guest at Raju's insistence. The TEACHER (50s, stern, with a stick he uses liberally) teaches math in Telugu. Arjun follows surprisingly well — math is math in any language.

A boy asks the teacher a question about fractions. The teacher's explanation is confusing. Arjun raises his hand. The class goes silent — the American boy wants to speak.

ARJUN (in a mix of Telugu and English) Sir, can I... may I try? To explain?

The teacher, grudgingly, gestures for him to proceed. Arjun goes to the blackboard. Using a cricket analogy — strike rate, run rate, averages — he explains fractions. The class, mostly cricket-obsessed boys, GETS IT immediately.

RAJU (to his classmates) See? My anna is genius. GENIUS.

The teacher stares. Then nods. Then asks Arjun to come back next week.

* * *

EXT. IPPATTLA-NEEM TREE-AFTERNOON

Arjun and Raju sit in the shade of the great neem tree at the village center. It's too hot to do anything else. The village is in its afternoon coma — even the dogs have given up.

RAJU Anna, tell me about America. What is it like?

ARJUN Big. Clean. Cold in winter. Everyone has a car. And a phone. And a TV bigger than that wall.

RAJU Do they play cricket?

ARJUN Some. Not like here. In America, cricket is a weekend thing. Here it's... everything.

RAJU Anna, do you miss it? America?

## Arjun thinks about this honestly.

ARJUN I miss my house. My room. My PlayStation. I miss my friend Danny. I miss... I miss my mom's dosas. But you know what's weird? I don't miss AMERICA. I miss the life I had there. And that life is gone. It doesn't exist anymore. So what am I missing? A ghost.

RAJU (quietly) That is very sad, anna.

ARJUN Yeah. But also... it means I get to build something new. Here. That's kind of cool, right?

* * *

RAJU (brightening) Very cool, anna. We will build AMAZING things. I will teach you Telugu. You will teach me American. We will win the tournament. And in ten years we will start our own cricket academy. ARJUN AND RAJU CRICKET ACADEMY. Best in Rayalaseema!

ARJUN (laughing) I love that you have a ten-year plan.

RAJU In this village, anna, you need a plan. Otherwise you end up like Yellappa — farming and bowling. No offense to Yellappa. His farming is better than his bowling.

They laugh. The neem tree drops a cluster of berries on Raju's head. He curses in Telugu. Arjun laughs harder. Two boys, killing time, building a bond that will outlast this summer.

## INT. SRINIVAS REDDY'S HOUSE-EVENING

Srinivas is alone on his veranda, chewing paan, watching the sun set. He looks older, tireder. Something is weighing on him.

Kavitha brings him chai. Sits beside him — an unusual move in this household where the women don't linger in the patriarch's space.

KAVITHA (in Telugu) Nanna, can I say something?

SRINIVAS (grunting) When has that ever stopped you?

* * *

KAVITHA Arjun visits Thatha every three days. Without being asked. He sits under the mango tree and listens to Thatha's stories. He's learning Telugu — for Thatha. He's not doing it for the land or the money or to make us look bad. He's doing it because he loves Thatha. And Thatha loves him. And you... you're missing it.

Srinivas is silent for a long time.

KAVITHA (CONT'D) Harish mama left. That was his choice. But Arjun came back. That should count for something, Nanna. Don't punish the son for the father's decision.

She stands. Leaves. Srinivas stares at the darkening sky. His jaw works. The granite of Rayalaseema pride, developing its first crack.

## EXT. VILLAGE TEMPLE-FESTIVAL DAY

A village festival for the local goddess. The temple is decorated with mango leaves and marigold garlands. The entire village gathers. Drums. Incense. A goat led around the temple for blessing.

Arjun, in a borrowed white kurta, moves through the crowd with Raju. For the first time in weeks, he doesn't draw extra attention. His darkened skin, his lungi-wrapping, his Telugu greetings have made him, if not invisible, then at least unremarkable.

He spots THATHA near the temple entrance, sitting in a plastic chair, attended by Kavitha. The old man looks frailer each time Arjun sees him.

ARJUN (in halting Telugu, rehearsed)
Thatha... bagunn...ara?
(Grandfather... are you... well?)

* * *

Thatha's clouded eyes widen. Then fill with tears.

THATHA (in Telugu) You learned Telugu? You are speaking Telugu to me?

ARJUN (in Telugu, with an American accent) Konchem konchem. Little little. Raju is teaching me. Every day one new word. Today's word was "cricket." But Raju says in Telugu it is just... "cricket."

Thatha laughs — a rasping, ancient sound, like wind through dry leaves. He reaches out a trembling hand and cups Arjun's face. He says something that Arjun doesn't fully understand — the words are old, formal Telugu — but the meaning transmits through the touch: I lost your father. But you came back. And that is a miracle I do not deserve.

KAVITHA (stepping forward, in English) He said: "Harish came home after all."

Arjun and Kavitha exchange a look. She is the one person in Srinivas's household who doesn't regard him with suspicion or contempt. She gives him a small, encouraging smile.

Across the temple courtyard, Padma watches. Her expression is layered — disapproval, calculation, and something else. Something she would never admit to: the uncomfortable recognition that this boy, whom she pushed out of her house, is winning the family's heart.

## EXT. VILLAGE PATH-DAY

Arjun walks past Srinivas's compound on his way to cricket practice. SURESH stands at the gate, arms folded, blocking the path like a small-time bouncer.

* * *

SURESH (in English, mocking) Look at this. The America boy playing village cricket. What happened? Couldn't make it in American leagues? Had to come here to feel important?

Arjun keeps walking. Doesn't engage. Suresh falls into step beside him.

SURESH (CONT'D) You know what my father says? Your father's share of the village land belongs to us now. He left. He abandoned it. Under village rules, the property goes to the brother who stayed and worked it. You showing up changes nothing.

Arjun stops. Turns. Looks at Suresh with an expression that is not angry, not hurt, but simply weary with the stupidity of it.

ARJUN Suresh, I didn't come here for land. I came here because nobody in America wanted me. I don't want your house or your fields or your tractor that doesn't start. I want to play cricket and learn Telugu and figure out how to live without my parents. If that threatens you somehow, that's your problem, not mine.

He turns and walks on. Behind him, Suresh's smirk falters. He expected confrontation and got composure. He doesn't know what to do with that.

INT. KRISHNA'S HOUSE-ARJUN'S ROOM-PRE-DAWN

Tournament day. Arjun wakes before the alarm, before the rooster, before even the first birdsong. He lies still for a moment. Then he gets up.

He washes his face. Puts on his Ippattla cricket jersey — the one Lakshmi stitched. He looks at himself in the small mirror on the wall. A tanned, lean boy with his father's face and his mother's eyes, wearing a homemade jersey in a village house in Rayalaseema.

He stops. Swallows. Looks at the ground.

SRINIVAS (CONT'D) (barely audible) I was proud. And I was ashamed. At the same time. Proud of you. Ashamed of myself. Because I should have... when you first
came... I should have...

He can't finish. Twenty-five years of pride won't let him form the words. But Arjun hears what the man can't say. He hears it in the spaces between the words, in the way Srinivas's thick farmer's hands grip his knees, in the tremble of a voice unaccustomed to vulnerability.

ARJUN (gently) It's okay, Uncle. I understand.

SRINIVAS (looking up) You understand? How can you understand?

ARJUN

* * *

Because my dad understood too. He never talked bad about you, Uncle. Not once. He'd say: "Your Srinivas uncle is a stubborn man, but he's the one who stayed. He kept the family together. That takes a different kind of courage." He respected you.

Srinivas stares at Arjun. His eyes are wet. In Rayalaseema, men do not cry. But Srinivas's eyes are wet, and he doesn't bother hiding it.

SRINIVAS (in Telugu) Your father said that?

ARJUN Every time your name came up. Which was a lot. You were important to him, Uncle. Even from far away.

A long silence. The afternoon heat shimmers. A crow calls from the neem tree. Then Srinivas stands. He walks to Arjun. And he does something that no one in the village has ever seen Srinivas Reddy do: he puts his hand on the boy's head. A blessing. The traditional gesture of an elder acknowledging a younger family member's place.

SRINIVAS (in Telugu) You are welcome in this house. Whenever you want. The door is open.

Arjun nods. He doesn't need the room. He has a home. But he understands what this gesture costs the man — and that makes it priceless.

He picks up his groceries. Walks back to Krishna's house. Behind him, Srinivas watches from the veranda, his hand still raised in something between a wave and a prayer.

EXT. IPPATTLA VILLAGE-KRISHNA'S HOUSE-MAGIC HOUR

* * *

The WIDE SHOT. The Rayalaseema sun descends toward the granite hills, painting the sky in colors that have no names — colors that exist only here, only now, for this one unrepeatable evening.

In the yard of Krishna's modest house, two figures play cricket in the dying light. Arjun bowls a gentle off-spinner. Raju heaves at it — a wild, joyful slog — and sends the ball soaring over the wall and into the cotton field. Both boys chase after it, laughing, their shadows stretching long across the red earth, elongating until they merge with the land itself.

CRANE UP: The village shrinks below us. The houses. The temple. The fields. The granite boulders and acacia trees and the ancient, patient earth that has absorbed a thousand generations of human arrival and departure.

And somewhere in this vastness — in a house with a cow in the yard and a hand pump and a ceiling fan that clicks — a boy from Edison, New Jersey sits down to eat rice and curry with his right hand, surrounded by the family that chose him.

A boy who had everything and lost it all.

A boy who was sent to the edge of the world by people who didn't want him.

A boy who found the one thing that was never in any trust fund or insurance policy or Princeton guest room:

A place where he is wanted. Not because he is useful or profitable or convenient. But because, on a hot night in a small village, a farmer with cricket-scarred hands opened his door and spoke the only words that ever mattered:

## "You are home."
