                    LOVE LASTS FOR 7 SECONDS

                          Written by

                     Navakanth Reddy Dumpa


          CONTROLLING IDEA: Love endures because it lives not in
          memory, but in the body, in the soul, in the spaces
          between moments.


FADE IN:


                                                            PAGE 1

                        ACT ONE

                   "THE WORLD SHE BUILT"

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR LAKE - DAWN

The sky over Hyderabad bleeds tangerine at its edges. The
Tank Bund Road is still half-asleep — a lone milk truck,
pigeons adjusting on the Necklace Road railings.

The water is still.

PRIYA ANAND (23) stands at the stone parapet. Slim. Dark
eyes that hold everything they see as if they've never seen
it before — because they haven't.

She wears a cotton kurta the color of turmeric. Three
things: a small notebook strapped to her left wrist with a
rubber band. A tablet in a rubber case clipped to her
dupatta. A dozen POST-IT NOTES tucked into the elastic of
the wrist notebook — each one a different color.

She reads a yellow Post-it:

                    POST-IT (V.O.)
          YOU ARE PRIYA. YOU ARE 23. YOU ARE
          SAFE. TODAY IS WEDNESDAY. RAJU
          BHAIYA KNOWS YOU. THIS IS HOME.

Her lips move silently with the words.

She looks up at the lake.

Her thumb runs along the edge of the notebook strap — once,
twice, three times.

Then —

SEVEN SECONDS.

Her eyes clear. She blinks. Looks at the lake as if she's
seeing it for the first time. Then her gaze drops to the
yellow Post-it still in her hand. She reads it. Nods once.
Tucks it back in.

This is her life. Seven seconds at a time.

RAJU BHAIYA (40s, a man built like a question mark — always
leaning forward) pushes his chai cart along the path. He
sees Priya. His face arranges itself into something
practiced and gentle.

                    RAJU BHAIYA
          Priya madam. Irani chai?

Priya turns to him. Her eyes search his face — finding the
name on a green Post-it before she even needs to look.

                    PRIYA
          Raju Bhaiya.
               (a small exhale of relief)
          Haan.

He pours. She watches the chai arc from the pot. Her tablet
buzzes. She looks at the screen:

          TABLET SCREEN: "YOU HAVE BEEN STANDING HERE FOR
          11 MINUTES. YOUR MOTHER KNOWS WHERE YOU ARE.
          YOU ARE SAFE. IT IS 6:14 AM."

She touches the screen gently. Accepts the information like
a gift.

                    RAJU BHAIYA
          Same time, same place. Every morning.

                    PRIYA
          Do I always get chai?

                    RAJU BHAIYA
               (handing her the glass)
          Always.

She wraps both hands around the glass. Sips. The heat — the
cardamom — her shoulders drop half an inch.

Some things the body knows even when the mind won't hold
them.

INT. ANAND RESIDENCE - JUBILEE HILLS - MORNING

A two-bedroom flat stacked with memory aids. Not clinical —
loving. LAKSHMI ANAND (52, silver streaks in oiled hair,
the specific exhaustion of someone who has loved longer than
they've slept) has constructed a world.

Whiteboards on the kitchen wall. Color-coded. RED = urgent.
GREEN = routine. BLUE = joy.

On the RED board: "PRIYA'S SEVEN-SECOND LOOP IS REAL. SHE
IS NOT PRETENDING. SHE IS NOT CONFUSED. SHE IS YOUR
DAUGHTER."

Not for Priya. For visitors who don't believe.

KIRAN ANAND (27, software engineer, the kind of man whose
jaw locks before his mouth opens) stands at the counter
eating upma standing up. He checks his phone. Checks the
door.

                    KIRAN
          She's not back yet.

                    LAKSHMI
               (not looking up from the
                stove)
          She's at the lake. Raju Bhaiya
          texted.

                    KIRAN
          She shouldn't go alone.

                    LAKSHMI
          She goes every morning.

                    KIRAN
          That doesn't make it —

                    LAKSHMI
          Safe? She built that route herself.
          Post-its, the tablet, Raju Bhaiya.
          It took her six months to build it.
          Don't dismantle it.

Kiran's jaw tightens. He sets the bowl down hard.

                    KIRAN
          One incident. One bad person who
          realizes she resets every —

                    LAKSHMI
          Kiran.

He stops. The weight of his mother's voice.

                    LAKSHMI (CONT'D)
          She is not a problem to be solved.
          She is your sister.

He picks up his bag. Leaves. The door doesn't slam — which
is somehow worse.

Lakshmi stands alone in the kitchen. Her hand finds the
edge of the counter. Her fingers press into the tile.

She lets out one breath that has been held for years.

INT./EXT. HITECH CITY - DESIGN STUDIO "KALAKAR" - MORNING

A glass-and-concrete building in Madhapur. Bold graphic
design agency posters in the lobby — bold colors, South
Indian cinema aesthetic fused with global minimalism.

ADITYA SHARMA (25, lanky, the kind of handsome that sneaks
up on you, born in Hyderabad to a Punjabi family — speaks
Telugu with a Delhi accent that embarrasses him) bursts
through the doors with a rolled tube of concept art under
one arm and a cold coffee from the vending machine in the
other.

He's late. He's always late. He doesn't look late — he
looks like lateness is a style choice.

MEERA (25, Aditya's colleague, the sharpest person in any
room she walks into, Hyderabad-born, Telugu-English code-
switcher, wearing a saree to the office because she can) is
already at her desk.

                    MEERA
          Client presentation is in twenty
          minutes.

                    ADITYA
          I know.

                    MEERA
          You were supposed to finalize the
          brand board last night.

                    ADITYA
          I did.

                    MEERA
          You sent me a voice note at 2 AM
          humming what you described as "the
          color orange."

                    ADITYA
               (unrolling the concept art)
          That's a creative brief. Orange
          is a feeling.

Meera looks at the board. Despite herself — it's beautiful.
Warm terracotta, saffron, deep teal. Hyderabadi color
language translated into branding.

                    MEERA
          You're lucky you're good.

                    ADITYA
               (already heading to the
                presentation room)
          I'm also charming.

                    MEERA
               (under her breath)
          Debatable.

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR LAKE BOARDWALK - MIDDAY

Priya has a routine she doesn't know she has.

She walks the same 400-meter stretch of boardwalk between
10 AM and noon. The route is marked in her tablet's GPS
with a blue line. Every 100 meters, a small audio alert:
"YOU ARE STILL ON YOUR PATH."

She carries a sketchbook. Not for words — for shapes.
She draws the lake. The kites. The BUDDHA STATUE rising
from the water in the distance.

She draws quickly — muscle memory — because she has seven
seconds and then everything resets. So each drawing is
seven seconds of pure looking.

She fills page after page with seven-second views of the
world.

A CHILD runs past with a kite string. Priya's pencil
catches the angle. Her eyes reset. She looks at the
drawing. Smiles at what her hands made.

There is something extraordinary in her face in this
moment: pure, uncomplicated wonder.

She doesn't know she's been here a hundred times. Every
time is the first time.

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR LAKE BOARDWALK - MOMENTS LATER

ADITYA jogs along the same boardwalk, earphones in,
completely in his head. He's listening to Telugu indie
music — something that sounds like rainfall on a tin roof
translated into guitar.

He rounds the bend — nearly crashes into PRIYA.

She stumbles. Her sketchbook hits the ground.

He catches her elbow automatically.

Their eyes meet.

Her eyes are completely open — no calculation, no
performance of meeting-a-stranger. Just direct, total
presence.

He doesn't know what to do with that.

                    ADITYA
          Sorry, sorry — I wasn't looking —

She looks at him. Then at the sketchbook on the ground.
Then back at him.

Seven seconds.

She blinks. Looks at the sketchbook. Looks at this
stranger. Doesn't know how the sketchbook fell or why
he's here.

                    PRIYA
          Hello.

He blinks.

                    ADITYA
          ...Hi? I just — I almost knocked
          you over —

                    PRIYA
          Did you?

                    ADITYA
          Just now. You dropped your —

He picks up the sketchbook. Hands it to her.

She takes it. Opens to the last page. The seven-second
drawing of the child's kite.

                    PRIYA
               (delighted)
          This is good. Did I do this?

He stares at her.

                    ADITYA
          You were drawing when I ran into
          you. Just now.

                    PRIYA
          Oh.

She studies the drawing as if it's a gift from a stranger
who happens to share her handwriting.

                    PRIYA (CONT'D)
          I don't remember drawing it.

A pause. He watches her tablet buzz.

TABLET SCREEN (VISIBLE TO AUDIENCE): "YOU JUST MET
SOMEONE. IT'S OKAY."

She reads it without embarrassment.

                    PRIYA (CONT'D)
          I should explain something to you.

                    ADITYA
          You really don't have to —

                    PRIYA
          My memory resets every seven
          seconds. So I won't remember this
          conversation. But I'm very glad
          to be having it.

He processes this.

His mouth opens. Closes.

She watches him with enormous patience — the patience of
someone who has had this moment many, many times and doesn't
know it.

                    ADITYA
          Every... seven seconds.

                    PRIYA
          Give or take.

                    ADITYA
          So right now —

                    PRIYA
          Right now I know your face,
          what you said, and that you
          have kind hands.

He looks at his hands. They are, objectively, just hands.

                    ADITYA
          How do you know where you are?
          Where to go? How do you —

Her tablet buzzes again. She glances at it.

                    PRIYA
          My family. Post-its. A very
          patient chai vendor. And —

She lifts her sketchbook.

                    PRIYA (CONT'D)
          I draw what I see. So even if
          I forget seeing it, I made it.

A beat.

The wind off the lake moves between them.

                    ADITYA
               (quietly)
          That's incredible.

                    PRIYA
          It's Tuesday. Or Wednesday. The
          Post-it says Wednesday.

She smiles. Bright, clean, weaponized in its innocence.

His jaw closes.

Something behind his sternum does something he can't name.

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR LAKE BOARDWALK - CONTINUOUS

He falls into step beside her. Not planned. Not decided.
Just happens.

                    ADITYA
          I'm Aditya.

                    PRIYA
          Priya.
               (beat)
          You'll have to tell me again
          in a few seconds.

                    ADITYA
               (half-laughing)
          Aditya. Graphic designer. Born here,
          raised here, pretends Delhi accent
          is cool.

                    PRIYA
          Is it?

                    ADITYA
          Absolutely not.

She laughs. A real laugh — head back, shoulders in.

Seven seconds.

She looks at him. A stranger walking beside her.

                    PRIYA
          Who are you?

He's ready for it now. Something in his posture adjusts —
becomes more careful, more deliberate.

                    ADITYA
          Aditya. We just met. You drew a
          kite and I almost knocked you
          into the lake.

                    PRIYA
          Did you apologize?

                    ADITYA
          I did.

                    PRIYA
               (considering)
          Are you a good person?

                    ADITYA
               (the honest answer)
          Mostly. I'm working on the rest.

She nods. Satisfied by this.

                    PRIYA
          Walk with me?

He does.

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR LAKE - RAJU'S CHAI CART - MOMENTS LATER

Raju Bhaiya clocks Aditya from twenty meters away. His
eyes make the specific calculation of a man who has watched
over Priya every morning for a year.

They approach.

                    RAJU BHAIYA
               (to Priya, neutral)
          Madam. New friend?

                    PRIYA
               (checking her tablet)
          This is Aditya. I met him just now.

                    RAJU BHAIYA
               (to Aditya, quietly)
          You know about her condition?

                    ADITYA
          She told me.

Raju Bhaiya holds Aditya's gaze for three full seconds.

                    RAJU BHAIYA
          I'm here every morning, six to
          noon. I have her mother's number.

The implication is absolute.

                    ADITYA
          I understand.

Raju pours chai. Hands one to Priya, who has already moved
on — she's looking at the Buddha statue on the water.

                    PRIYA
          Look at that. Is that always there?

                    ADITYA
               (accepting the second cup
                from Raju)
          Since 1992.

                    PRIYA
          It looks new.

He looks at the statue. Then at her face, seeing it fresh.

                    ADITYA
          Yeah. It does.

INT. ANAND RESIDENCE - EVENING

KIRAN sits on the sofa with his laptop. PRIYA comes home.
She reads the whiteboard. Reads the Post-its on the door.
Reads the tablet notification: "YOU ARE HOME. YOUR BROTHER
IS IN THE LIVING ROOM. HIS NAME IS KIRAN. HE IS SAFE."

She enters. Kiran looks up.

                    KIRAN
          How was the lake?

                    PRIYA
          Good. There was a man.

Kiran's entire body becomes alert.

                    KIRAN
          What man?

                    PRIYA
          A graphic designer. He was kind.
               (looking at her sketchbook)
          I drew a kite. And there's a
          new page here — just the word
          "Aditya" written in my own

She shows him. It's true. At some point in her loop she

                    KIRAN
          You wrote a man's name in your
          sketchbook.

                    PRIYA
          It seems I liked him.

                    KIRAN
          Priya —

                    PRIYA
          Kiran. The star is good. I wouldn't
          draw a star next to someone who
          wasn't good.

She goes to her room. He sits with the sketchbook for a
long time, looking at the name.

ADITYA.

With a star.

Aditya sits on his balcony. City lights spread below —
Hyderabad at night, a quilt of gold and white.

He has his sketchbook open. He's trying to work on a
client brief.

He draws, instead: a face. Eyes that hold the world like
it's entirely new.

He stares at it for a long time.

His phone buzzes. MEERA.

                    MEERA (V.O.)
               (through phone)

                    ADITYA
          Nothing. I met someone at the lake.

                    MEERA (V.O.)
          Someone.

                    ADITYA
          A girl. She has this condition where

Silence.

                    MEERA (V.O.)
          Aditya.

                    ADITYA
          I know.

                    MEERA (V.O.)

                    ADITYA
          I know.

                    ADITYA
          Yes.

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR LAKE - SERIES OF MORNINGS - MONTAGE

Days pass.

MORNING ONE: Aditya brings a drawing pad. He sits next to
her and draws the lake. She draws the lake. Seven-second
pictures from two different eyes. At the end he shows her
his drawing, she shows him hers. She adds a star to her
page.

MORNING TWO: He learns to introduce himself in the window
before she resets. He gets the rhythm of it — seven
seconds of presence, then rebuild. He starts to feel the
gaps, instinctively.

MORNING THREE: He brings her a red Post-it. Written on it:
"ADITYA IS SAFE. HE DRAWS THINGS. HE KNOWS." She reads
it. Tucks it into her notebook. Looks at him.

                    PRIYA
          You wrote this?

                    ADITYA
          I asked Raju Bhaiya what format
          you use.

She holds the Post-it. Her thumb runs along its edge.

                    PRIYA
          No one's ever made me a Post-it
          before.

MORNING FOUR: It rains. They sit under Raju Bhaiya's
chai cart umbrella. The lake goes silver. Priya draws the
rain in quick strokes — seven seconds of rain, repeated.
Aditya watches her hands.

MORNING FIVE: Kiran appears at the far end of the boardwalk.
He watches Aditya. Aditya clocks him and waves. Kiran
does not wave back. Priya doesn't notice any of it. She's
watching a kingfisher.

INT. ANAND RESIDENCE - PRIYA'S ROOM - NIGHT

Priya sits at her desk. Her room is a masterpiece of
adaptive design. Whiteboard walls. Photos labeled with
names. A mirror with a morning script:

          "GOOD MORNING, PRIYA. TODAY IS [DAY]. YOU
           ARE LOVED. CHECK YOUR TABLET. DRINK WATER.
           YOU ARE SAFE."

She opens her sketchbook to today's pages. Sees drawings
of the dinner. Aditya across the table. The biryani.
Kiran's serious face. Lakshmi's hands.

And one word at the bottom of the last page, written
in her own hand:

          "ADITYA ★"

She touches the star. Doesn't know why she drew it.

But something in her chest is warm.

She records a voice note — her ritual, a message to
herself for morning:

                    PRIYA
               (into the recorder)
          Today something good happened.
          I don't remember it. But I drew
          a lot of stars, so I think you
          should trust the morning.
          Good night, Priya.

She clicks off the recorder. Sets it in its labeled spot.

Gets into bed.

The city of Hyderabad hums below.

EXT. CHARMINAR - DAY - THREE WEEKS LATER

The great monument. Mid-afternoon heat. Tour groups.
Hyderabadi daily life swirling around its ankles like
always.

Aditya and Priya walk through the old city together.
This is new — not just the lake, but the deeper grid
of Hyderabad's heart.

He has been coming to the lake every morning for three
weeks. Each morning starts the same. Each morning she
adds his Post-it. Each morning she draws him a star.

INT. PRIYA'S ROOM — AFTERNOON

Rain on the window. Priya writing in her long-form 
journal — the cloth-covered one, deep teal, kept 
on the desk beside the post-it wall.

She doesn't write in it often — only in the longer 
windows, the rare ones when the loop extends slightly, 
when she has four or five minutes of continuity.

She writes:

"I don't know what my story looks like from the outside.
I know it has gaps — enormous ones. I know people 
see me and feel pity or wonder or the particular 
unease of watching someone live in a way they couldn't.

But from the inside, it doesn't feel broken. It feels 
like standing at a window that's always clean. No 
accumulated grime of resentment. No old argument 
sitting at the table. Every morning the same clean 
light.

I chose this man in some window I don't remember. 
But my hands know him. My body says: stand here. 
This is the right shape to stand next to.

I don't know his faults. I only know his constancy. 
Maybe that's unfair to him — he can't give me his 
full self because I can't hold the difficult parts. 
But he keeps showing up with his full self anyway 
and trusting that what I have of him is real, 
even if it's not complete.

This is what I know: he carries what I can't. 
He's angry on my behalf. He walks on the side 
closest to the road.

Maybe love doesn't require the full story. 
Maybe it only requires the body's testimony. 
The hand that reaches. The face that opens.

Write this down. Because in seven seconds you'll 
forget you thought it.

But you'll feel it again tomorrow. At the lake.
At 6:47 AM. In the warmth before knowledge.

That's enough. That's everything."

She finishes. Reads it once.

Closes the journal.

Her hand goes to the post-it wall. She selects a 
blue post-it.

She writes: "LOVE IS WHAT THE BODY KNOWS."

She pins it to the center.

          What?

                    PRIYA
          Beginning. Every 
          morning. The same lake, 
          the same water, 
          the same shore — 
          but the light is 
          new every time. 
          And the light is 
          the whole thing. 
          Without the new light, 
          it's just water.
               (pause)
          Maybe that's what 
          I am. The same 
          water, every morning. 
          And you're the new light.

He looks at the lake. He looks at her.

                    ADITYA
          That's going in 
          your notebook.

                    PRIYA
               (already writing)
          Already there.

"I AM THE LAKE. YOU ARE THE NEW LIGHT."

She presses it flat on the page.

Blue note. Her handwriting. Both of their words.

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR — BOARDWALK — DAWN — TWO YEARS AND EIGHT MONTHS

SUPER: "AUGUST"

The monsoon edge. The particular August morning 
in Hyderabad when the season hasn't decided to 
leave yet — the air wet, warm, the lake the 
color of pewter at rest, clouds piled at the 
horizon. Not raining. Not clear. Both.

The boardwalk before the city wakes.

Raju Bhaiya at his cart, the steam from his 
vessel curling up into the wet air. He sees 
a figure at the far end of the boardwalk and 
his hands move, familiar, to prepare.

PRIYA. Walking the railing. Not toward him — 
the long slow walk she does on mornings when 
the window is generous. Her notebook in one 
hand, her other hand trailing on the railing 
bars, each one a separate note in a melody 
she is playing by touch.

She stops.

Looks at the lake.

The lake the same as it was three years ago — 
the same water, the same shore, the same 
Buddha at the center. The light this morning 
is lower, heavier with cloud, but present.

She opens her notebook.

Inside cover. She reads it. Her own handwriting 
— two and a half years of evolution, but the 
essential entry unchanged, only more decorated, 
more certain at the edges.

She looks up from the notebook.

The lake. The dawn. The railing.

Something in her face: not peace, exactly — 
something larger than peace. Acceptance made 
so complete it stops being a feeling and 
becomes a fact.

She speaks — to herself, to the lake:

                    PRIYA
               (quiet, certain)
          Here I am.

The lake: the pewter water, the cloud-filtered 
light, the familiar shape of the city waking 
at the edges.

Here she is.

Footsteps. The particular weight of them.

She doesn't count. She doesn't have to count 
anymore — the body has built a map that runs 
below counting, below knowing. The body simply 
recognizes.

He sits beside her on the ledge.

His shoulder against hers.

The same geometry as always. The same warmth.

She doesn't turn.

                    PRIYA
          The lake is very quiet 
          this morning.

                    ADITYA
          The monsoon makes it 
          think before it moves.

She looks at the water.

                    PRIYA
          My notes say I've been 
          married almost three years.

                    ADITYA
          Four months to the anniversary.

                    PRIYA
          Do we come here every morning?

                    ADITYA
          Every day you're in 
          the city. Yes.

She considers this.

                    PRIYA
          My feet know the railing.

                    ADITYA
          Every stone of it.

                    PRIYA
          Three years of mornings.

                    ADITYA
          Before that too. 
          You were here before me.

She turns from the lake to look at him. The 
full look — the one that goes past the surface, 
not because she has memory behind it but because 
the body's accumulated knowing is there, 
under the eyes, in the quality of the attention.

                    PRIYA
          Tell me one thing I 
          wouldn't know.

He looks at the lake. Decides.

                    ADITYA
          You've started teaching. 
          A class at the studio — 
          Kalamkari for beginners. 
          Saturday mornings. 
          You have eleven students.

She looks at her hands.

                    PRIYA
          My hands teach?

                    ADITYA
          Your hands demonstrate. 
          You talk while you work. 
          Your students say 
          you're the only teacher 
          who makes them feel 
          like the not-knowing 
          is part of the work.

                    PRIYA
          Because it is.

                    ADITYA
          Yes.

Raju Bhaiya arrives. Two cups. He sets them 
down with the economy of long practice, the 
small bow of acknowledgment, and returns 
to his cart without a word.

They hold the cups.

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR — BOARDWALK — CONTINUOUS

The cloud at the horizon shifts. A break 
in it — a line of gold, low, the specific 
color of the sun when it has to fight 
through cloud to announce itself.

The lake goes from pewter to bronze.

                    PRIYA
               (watching the light)
          There.

                    ADITYA
          Yes.

                    PRIYA
          It does this every morning?

                    ADITYA
          Different every morning. 
          Same surprise.

She watches the light move across the water.

                    PRIYA
          What did you want 
          to be, before this?

He looks at her.

                    ADITYA
          What?

                    PRIYA
          My notes say you're 
          a graphic designer. 
          Before you knew what 
          you were doing with 
          your life. What did 
          you want?

He thinks about this honestly.

                    ADITYA
          I wanted to make things 
          people would look at 
          for longer than they 
          had to. Not because 
          the things were loud — 
          because they were right. 
          Because they fit the 
          thing they were describing.

                    PRIYA
          Do you do that?

                    ADITYA
          I try.

                    PRIYA
          What does "right" mean?

He looks at the light on the water.

                    ADITYA
          When a thing looks 
          like it couldn't be 
          any other way.
               (pause)
          When you take away 
          everything that doesn't 
          belong and what's 
          left is necessary.

She absorbs this.

                    PRIYA
          Is that what we are?

He looks at her.

                    PRIYA (CONT'D)
          What's left when you 
          take away everything 
          that doesn't belong.

A beat. The light on the lake.

                    ADITYA
          Yes.

She looks back at the water.

                    PRIYA
          Write that. Not for me — 
          for your wall.

He takes a blue post-it from her notebook. 
Writes:

"WHAT'S LEFT WHEN YOU TAKE AWAY EVERYTHING 
THAT DOESN'T BELONG."

Three post-its in his wallet now.

He puts it there. She watches.

                    PRIYA (CONT'D)
          You have a wall now.

                    ADITYA
          A small one.

                    PRIYA
          They start small.

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR — BOARDWALK — CONTINUOUS

The gold at the horizon widening now. The 
lake going fully bronze, then, as the sun 
clears the cloud edge, shifting toward 
the particular amber of Hyderabad morning — 
the color that belongs to no other city.

Around them, the boardwalk beginning to 
populate: the morning walkers, a jogger, 
an old man with a newspaper. The city 
returning to itself.

Raju Bhaiya's cart drawing its first customers. 
His voice carrying — the easy patter of a 
man in his place, doing his work.

Priya resets.

Her notebook. The inside cover. The ring 
on her finger, the bangle. Aditya beside her.

She looks at the lake.

She looks at him.

                    PRIYA
          You feel familiar.

                    ADITYA
          You've known me a long time.

                    PRIYA
          My notes say almost 
          three years.

                    ADITYA
          Yes.

                    PRIYA
               (simply)
          You feel like home.

His jaw works once. The thing behind his eyes 
— not new, not old, just present. Always present.

                    ADITYA
          I know.

She puts her head on his shoulder.

The lake. The morning. The city finding its 
voice around them.

The particular quality of this: two people 
at a railing, watching a city wake, the water 
moving its slow patient way. Neither of them 
needing to name what they have because the 
body has already named it, in the only language 
that doesn't require memory to survive.

She resets.

Notebook. His shoulder warm under her head. 
She finds the inside cover from this angle — 
she knows the words by now in the way she 
knows the railing: by touch.

She doesn't move her head from his shoulder.

                    PRIYA

                    ADITYA

                    ADITYA

                    ADITYA

                    PRIYA
          That's the whole method.

                    ADITYA

The morning around them deepening — the 
light fully gold now, the lake alive with it, 
the city in its full chorus: the azaan 
carrying from Charminar, the auto horns, 
the crow, the fruit seller on the far side 
of the road already calling out his morning 
inventory, the children somewhere below 
the embankment arguing about something 
that doesn't matter and matters enormously.

All of it. The whole city. Alive, present, 
exactly itself.

And here: the boardwalk. The railing. 
Two people and a lake and the morning 
in between.

Her head on his shoulder.

His hand over hers.

Raju Bhaiya, from his cart, watching with 
the expression of a man watching something 
inevitable and being glad he was present 
for it every step of the way.

               (to no one, in Telugu,

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR — BOARDWALK — LATER

The full morning now. The sun above the 
buildings. The lake in its daytime self — 
the gold gone, replaced by the clear blue 
of October, the Buddha vivid against it.

Priya standing at the railing. Her notebook 
open to a fresh page. Writing — the long 
window, the hand moving steadily.

Aditya beside her, his sketchbook, 
drawing what he always draws: the city 
and the lake and her in it, the figure 
at the railing who is always exactly here.

She finishes writing. Reads it back.

Tears it out.

Holds it for a moment.

Then she folds it — not to keep it, not 
to give it to him — she folds it into 
the shape her hands know without instruction: 
a boat. Small, precise, the paper thinning 
at the folds from the weight of having 
been handled carefully.

She sets it on the ledge.

Looks at it.

                    PRIYA
          The lake should have this one.

She picks it up. Leans over the railing. 
Sets it on the water.

The paper boat on Hussain Sagar. White, 
small, the ink of her handwriting just 
visible through the fold.

It moves. The lake accepting it — the 
small current pulling it away from 
the shore, out toward the open water, 
toward the Buddha, toward the center 
of things.

They watch it go.

Aditya doesn't ask what it said.

The boat moves.

Gets smaller.

                    PRIYA
               (watching it)
          I'll write it again.
               (pause)
          I always do.

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR — THE LAKE — CONTINUOUS

THE PAPER BOAT on the water.

Moving slowly away from the boardwalk, 
carried by the lake's patient current.

Past the morning reflections — the city 
inverted in the still surface, the sky 
doubled, the whole world both above 
and below.

Past a water bird landing on the far shore, 
precise and sudden and certain.

Past the zone where the morning light 
fragments into gold on the surface.

The boat small now. A white point. Moving.

The writing inside it — the thing she wrote 
in the long window, legible only to the 
lake now — dissolving as the paper takes 
in water, slowly, the ink releasing, the 
words becoming part of the water, the 
water becoming part of the city, the city 
becoming part of the morning that is always 
the first morning.

It is enough.

EXT. HUSSAIN SAGAR — THE LAKE — DAWN

THE LAKE.

Full morning light now. The water still 
and copper and alive. The city reflected 
in it — the minarets, the glass towers, 
the particular skyline of a city that 
has been building itself for four hundred 
years and has not finished.

The Buddha at the center. His expression 
unchanged. The oldest face in the water.

A ripple from somewhere — the movement 
of one thing touching another, the signal 
traveling outward in rings, each ring 
perfect and brief and gone.

Then still again.

The lake: a surface that remembers nothing 
and reflects everything, infinitely present, 
infinitely new, infinitely the same.

The city waking around it.

The morning doing what the morning does.

HOLD ON THE LAKE.
