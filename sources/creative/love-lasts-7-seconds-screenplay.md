## ACT ONE: THE MORNING PROTOCOL

---

**INT. PRIYA'S FLAT — BEDROOM — PRE-DAWN**

Darkness. Then a slow, pale light through thin curtains.

A small flat. Tidy to the point of deliberateness — nothing left to chance. Every surface has exactly one object on it, placed with the precision of someone who knows that disorder costs more than it would cost most people.

On the bedside table: a laminated card, face-up, in a plastic stand. It has been positioned so it is the first thing visible upon waking.

The card is cream-coloured. The text is printed in large, clear font.

We read it:

```
    YOUR NAME IS PRIYA.
    YOU HAVE A CONDITION CALLED ANTEROGRADE AMNESIA.
    THIS MEANS YOU CANNOT FORM NEW MEMORIES.
    THIS IS NOT YOUR FAULT.
    YOUR NOTEBOOKS ARE ON THE SHELF.
    READ THE BLUE ONE FIRST.
    THEN THE CREAM ONE.
    THEN CHECK: DO YOU KNOW WHERE NANDITA IS?
    DO YOU KNOW WHAT DAY IT IS?
    THE ANSWERS ARE IN THE NOTEBOOKS.
    IT'S OKAY. YOU HAVE EVERYTHING YOU NEED.
```

PRIYA IYER (early 30s) lies still. Her eyes open. She stares at the ceiling for a moment — the particular stillness of someone who has learned not to move until they understand where they are.

Then she turns. She sees the card.

She reads it.

Her face does not collapse. It is not the face of someone reading terrible news for the first time. It is more precise than that — a face doing a necessary calculation.

She sits up. Reaches for the laminated card. Holds it. Reads it again, more slowly.

She nods once, to herself.

---

**INT. PRIYA'S FLAT — MAIN ROOM — MOMENTS LATER**

A shelf runs the length of one wall. On it: three notebooks, positioned left to right with exact spacing.

- A dark blue hardback. Thick. Worn at the corners.
- A cream A5. A pen is clipped to the cover.
- A green B6. Smaller. Well-thumbed.

Below the shelf: twelve cardboard boxes, stacked in two neat rows of six. Each spine is labeled in someone else's handwriting — a careful, old-fashioned script. The labels read: **PRIYA / NOTEBOOK ARCHIVE / VOL. 1**, **VOL. 2**, and so on, up to **VOL. 12**.

Priya stands before the shelf in her nightclothes. She picks up the dark blue notebook — the night summary book — and opens it to the most recent entry.

We PUSH IN on the page. Her handwriting, neat and deliberate:

```
    WEDNESDAY, [DATE]

    Today I stayed home until midday. Nandita came at 11. We had
    tea. She told me she has a job interview next week — I wrote
    her a note in the green book so I remember to ask.

    At 2pm I went to the pharmacy. The man at the counter knows
    me. He did not make me feel strange.

    I did not go outside again.

    Tomorrow is Thursday. Kabir Mehta comes on Thursdays.
    He is an architect. I have met him four times. He is patient
    about the notebooks. I think I like him. I cannot remember
    liking him, but I wrote it down, so it must be true.

    It is okay.
```

Priya closes the blue notebook. Places it back on the shelf with precision.

She picks up the cream notebook.

She sits at the table and begins to read from the beginning of the current section. Her finger moves along the lines. She is not reading the way most people read. She is reading the way a person reads a file on someone they have never met.

Her face is very still.

---

**INT. PRIYA'S FLAT — MAIN ROOM — LATER**

Priya sits with the cream notebook open. A cup of tea has appeared. She doesn't remember making it, but there it is, still warm.

She has been reading for some time. She stops at a page with a note written in red ink, underlined twice:

```
    KABIR MEHTA — THURSDAYS AT 10AM.
    HE ALWAYS BRINGS CHAI. DO NOT OFFER TO MAKE TEA FIRST.
    YOU HAVE MET HIM BEFORE. YOU LIKE TALKING TO HIM.
    THIS IS CONFIRMED. SEE ENTRIES: [page numbers listed]
    HE KNOWS ABOUT THE NOTEBOOKS.
    IT'S OKAY TO WRITE WHILE HE TALKS.
    HE DOES NOT MIND.
```

Priya reads this twice. Then she uncaps her pen and writes in the margin, in the precise timestamp format she uses for everything:

```
    [TIME] — re-read. I do not feel that I know him.
    But I know I have written this. So I trust it.
```

She sets the pen down.

She looks at the wall across from her. On it: a framed photograph. A woman in her fifties, warm-faced, laughing. Written below the photograph in a small card: *Leela. Your mother. She died. You have grieved her.*

Priya looks at the photograph for a long moment.

Then she picks up the green notebook.

She turns to a page headed **KABIR MEHTA** in careful capitals. Below it:

```
    First met: [date]. At Nandita's insistence.
    Occupation: architect.
    Physical: tall. Dark. Slightly worried-looking when he listens.
    Important: he brings chai from the place on the corner.
         He says he has a regular order there now. This is true —
         Nandita confirmed.
    He asks good questions. Write down what he asks so you can
         think about it later.
    Do not apologize for the notebooks. He already knows.
    He said once: "You're not your memory, Priya."
         I wrote this down because I wasn't sure if I believed it.
         I still don't know.
    
    Last interaction: [most recent date].
    He said he would come back.
    He always does.
```

Priya runs her finger over the last line.

She closes the green notebook. Places it back.

She looks at the cardboard boxes along the bottom of the shelf.

She crouches in front of them. Runs one hand along the spines, reading the labels in her mother's handwriting. Her finger stops at **VOL. 1**.

She does not open it.

She just rests her hand on it.

---

**INT. STAIRWELL / PRIYA'S FLAT — LATER THAT MORNING**

A KNOCK at the door.

Priya is already at the table, cream notebook open, pen in hand. She checks the time on her phone. Writes:

```
    [TIME] — knock at door.
```

She goes to the door. Opens it.

NANDITA KRISHNAN (mid-40s, solid, warm, with the look of someone who has decided that cheerfulness is a form of discipline) stands in the hallway with two paper cups of coffee. She has been here many times. Her body is comfortable in the doorway.

**NANDITA**
*(bright, not performatively bright — real)*
Good morning.

Priya looks at her. A beat — the particular beat of someone making rapid computations.

**PRIYA**
Nandita.

**NANDITA**
That's the one.

She holds up the coffees.

**NANDITA (CONT'D)**
I brought the good kind.

Priya steps back to let her in. As she does, she is already writing in her notebook, moving back to the table without looking up:

```
    [TIME] — Nandita arrived. Coffee. She looks tired
    but is pretending she isn't.
```

Nandita has seen this a thousand times. She sets the coffees down, pulls out a chair, sits. She waits for Priya to finish writing.

**NANDITA**
Did you sleep?

**PRIYA**
The notebook says yes.
*(she looks up)*
I don't remember sleeping. But I woke up in my bed, which is
consistent with having slept.

Nandita smiles. There is something behind the smile — something that lives at the back of it — but she doesn't let it come forward.

**NANDITA**
Kabir comes today.

**PRIYA**
*(without looking up — already writing)*
I know. It's in red ink.

**NANDITA**
How many times have you read that entry this morning?

**PRIYA**
Twice.
*(pause)*
Three times.

**NANDITA**
Do you want me to stay until he gets here?

Priya considers this. Sets the pen down.

**PRIYA**
Did I ask you to stay last time?

**NANDITA**
No.

**PRIYA**
Then I probably didn't need you to.

**NANDITA**
You probably didn't.

A pause. They drink their coffees. This is the rhythm they have developed — the comfortable silences built up not in Priya's memory but in Nandita's, a whole history of mornings that only one of them carries.

**PRIYA**
The notebook says I like him.

**NANDITA**
You do.

**PRIYA**
But I don't feel that I know him.

**NANDITA**
*(carefully)*
What does it feel like?

**PRIYA**
*(thinking)*
Like reading someone's diary. Someone who shares my handwriting.
She went somewhere and met someone and thought he was patient.
But I don't remember the meeting. I just know it happened.

Nandita nods. She doesn't say *I know* or *that must be so hard.* Those phrases are for people who want to perform understanding. Nandita's understanding is older and less vocal than that.

**NANDITA**
Do you trust her? The you who wrote it?

**PRIYA**
*(a small pause)*
Most of the time.

**NANDITA**
That's enough.

---

**INT. PRIYA'S FLAT — MAIN ROOM — 10:07 AM**

Another knock.

Priya is at the table. The cream notebook is open. She has written the time at the top of a fresh section and is waiting, pen in hand.

She opens the door.

KABIR MEHTA (mid-30s, tall, carrying two paper chai cups from a corner place, wearing the slightly too-formal jacket of someone who wanted to look uncasual) stands in the hallway.

He looks at her the way people look at someone they have thought about since the last time they saw them.

Priya looks at him. The computation again — rapid, practiced.

**PRIYA**
Kabir Mehta.

Something crosses his face — he has heard his own name said this way before, and each time there is a fraction of a second where it registers that she is confirming, not greeting.

**KABIR**
*(simply)*
Hi.

**PRIYA**
You brought chai.

**KABIR**
I always do.

**PRIYA**
*(turning back into the flat)*
I know. I wrote it down.

He follows her in. His eyes go to the shelf — the three notebooks, the twelve boxes. He has seen this before. His body is easy in this room.

He sits. Sets the chai down. Watches her write:

```
    10:07 — Kabir Mehta arrived. Chai from the corner shop.
    He looks the same as in the green notebook description.
    Slightly worried-looking when he listens.
```

She looks up.

**PRIYA**
It's the fourth time. That you've come.

**KABIR**
*(not a correction, just confirmation)*
Fourth Thursday.

**PRIYA**
I don't feel that I know you.

**KABIR**
*(carefully)*
Do you feel like you've met me?

**PRIYA**
*(thinks)*
No. But the notebook says I have. And the notebook is — 
*(she touches the cream cover)*
— more accurate than my feelings.

Kabir nods. He has learned not to fill this kind of silence.

**PRIYA (CONT'D)**
*(reading from the green notebook)*
"He asks good questions." That's what I wrote.
*(she looks up)*
So ask one.

A beat. Kabir considers.

**KABIR**
When you read about yourself in the notebooks — the version of
you who met me the first time, the second time — do you feel
like that's you?

Priya is already writing. A beat while she writes.

**PRIYA**
*(without looking up)*
No. She's someone I've read about. I understand what she
experienced. I can trace the logic of it. But I can't
feel it.

She looks up.

**PRIYA (CONT'D)**
It's like the difference between reading that you once fell off
a bicycle and actually remembering the road, the speed, the
skin coming off your palm.

*(beat)*

I know all my history. But I don't live in any of it.

Kabir looks at her.

**KABIR**
That sounds very lonely.

**PRIYA**
*(writing)*
I suspect it is. I've written that word down several times.
*(reads back what she just wrote)*
I just wrote it again.

---

**INT. PRIYA'S FLAT — MAIN ROOM — CONTINUOUS**

They sit across from each other. The chai has been drunk. The notebooks are open on the table between them — a visible third presence in the room.

**KABIR**
Can I ask — the boxes.
*(he gestures at the twelve boxes along the shelf)*
How many?

**PRIYA**
Twelve. The first one starts from when I was discharged from
hospital. After the encephalitis.

**KABIR**
That's — how many years?

**PRIYA**
The labels are in my mother's handwriting.

A silence.

**KABIR**
She kept them organized for you.

**PRIYA**
Until she couldn't anymore.
*(matter-of-fact)*
I labeled them myself after that. Tried to match her style.
But you can see the difference.
*(she doesn't look at the boxes)*
I can always see the difference.

Kabir says nothing. He has learned that some things should not be responded to, only received.

**PRIYA (CONT'D)**
The notebooks are — 
*(she pauses, choosing the precise word)*
— they're not a record. Most people think they're like a diary.
But a diary is something you go back to and the memory returns
with it. You read "that summer in Goa" and you feel the heat.

*(beat)*

I go back and I understand what happened. Like reading a case
file. Very thorough evidence of a life. But the life itself
doesn't come back.

**KABIR**
Does that bother you? That distinction?

**PRIYA**
*(writing)*
It used to. In the early months I tried to — 
*(reads her own notebook)*
— I wrote that I kept trying to *remember harder.* As if I
could force it. As if the memories were there and just
refusing to come.

She closes the notebook for a moment. Her hands rest on the cover.

**PRIYA (CONT'D)**
They're not there. The hippocampus —
*(she taps the side of her head)*
— there's a dark void in my temporal lobe. In the scan you can
see exactly where the tissue is gone. Where the nerve cells
are just — absent. The white areas where the inflammation was.
And then, four years later, just dark. Empty.
*(opens the notebook again)*
I photographed the scan. It's in here somewhere.
*(she doesn't look for it)*
I look at it sometimes. To remind myself that this isn't a
failing of will. It's geography.

Kabir watches her.

**KABIR**
Who told you to photograph it?

**PRIYA**
I told myself. I found the note later — it's in my handwriting.
I wrote under the photo: *This is the inside of my head. The
dark part is the part that should hold your memories. It is
gone. The notebooks are what live there instead.*

*(beat)*

I thought that was rather good, actually.

Something shifts in Kabir's face.

**KABIR**
It is.

**PRIYA**
*(writing)*
You said that as though it surprised you.

**KABIR**
You surprise me. Every Thursday.

Priya stops writing. Looks up at him.

**PRIYA**
I won't remember this.

**KABIR**
I know.

**PRIYA**
But you'll come back next Thursday anyway.

**KABIR**
*(simply)*
Yes.

Priya looks at him for a moment. Then she writes it down:

```
    He said yes without hesitation. I noted the tone.
    File under: people who mean what they say.
```

## ACT TWO: WHAT NANDITA CARRIES

---

**INT. NANDITA'S FLAT — KITCHEN — EVENING**

Nandita at her kitchen table. A cup of tea she has forgotten to drink. Her phone face-down.

She is staring at the middle distance.

Her HUSBAND, RAJAN (50s, gentle, a man who has long since accepted that some of what his wife carries, she will not put down), comes in from the other room. He sits across from her without asking.

**RAJAN**
How was she today?

**NANDITA**
Kabir came.

**RAJAN**
The architect.

**NANDITA**
She read to him from the green notebook. His entry. All the
things she'd written about the previous Thursdays. She read
it to him like she was briefing a stranger.

*(pause)*

He sat there and listened to a woman read him a description
of himself. And when she finished he asked her a question
about what it felt like to read it.

**RAJAN**
*(carefully)*
Is that bad?

**NANDITA**
No. That's — that's the right thing to do.

She picks up the tea. Puts it down.

**NANDITA (CONT'D)**
Do you know what she said to me, in the first week after the
hospital? When she finally understood what the condition meant?

**RAJAN**
Tell me.

**NANDITA**
She said: *Nobody is going to love me now.*

*(beat)*

She said it very quietly. She didn't say it to make me fix it.
She said it as a statement of fact.

*(beat)*

I told her that wasn't true. She wrote it down — both the thing
she'd said and what I'd told her. She nodded. She wrote *Nandita
says this is not true.* And then she looked at me and said,
*I'll have to take your word for it.*

Silence.

**RAJAN**
And?

**NANDITA**
And he comes every Thursday. And she reads him her own notes
about how much she likes talking to him, and she doesn't
remember liking him, but she trusts the notes enough to
keep letting him in.

*(beat)*

She has taken my word for it. Every single Thursday morning,
she takes my word for it. And she lets him in.

---

**INT. PRIYA'S FLAT — MAIN ROOM — THURSDAY EVENING**

Priya at her table. The cream notebook is beside her but closed. The dark blue night-summary notebook is open.

She writes:

```
    [TIME] — End of day summary. Thursday.

    Nandita came this morning. Coffee. She looked tired.
    I did not ask why — I have probably already asked in previous
    entries and it would be repetitive.

    Kabir Mehta came at 10:07am.
    Occupation: architect.
    This was his fourth visit.

    He brought chai.

    We talked about the notebooks and what it means that I can
    understand my history without feeling it. He asked good
    questions. I said this in the green book before and I say
    it again here: he asks good questions.

    He said something about being surprised by me.

    I am not sure what to do with that.
    I wrote it down because I thought it might matter.
```

She pauses. Looks at what she has written. Then she adds, in slightly smaller writing:

```
    I noticed I was not anxious while he was here.
    That is unusual.
    I usually write the time every few minutes when I am anxious.
    Today I went seventeen minutes between timestamps.

    I think that means something.
    I am not sure what.
    I will check next Thursday whether it happens again.
```

She closes the blue notebook.

She goes to the shelf. Runs her hand along the boxes — one, two, three, all the way to twelve. Stops at the last one.

She does not open it.

She just rests her hand there, the way a person rests a hand on something that contains everything that makes them who they are.

---

## ACT THREE: DR. RANGAN AND THE DARK VOID

---

**INT. NEUROLOGY CLINIC — CONSULTATION ROOM — DAY**

Sparse. Bright. A light box on the wall holds a brain scan.

DR. ADITI RANGAN (50s, precise, with the manner of someone who has learned to be honest without being cruel) sits across from Priya. Nandita is beside Priya. Priya has both the cream notebook and a small loose notepad.

She is writing as Dr. Rangan speaks.

**DR. RANGAN**
The most recent scan shows no change from last year's.

**PRIYA**
*(writing)*
Is that good or bad?

**DR. RANGAN**
In your case, stable is the best outcome we can hope for.
We're not expecting deterioration, but there will be no
recovery of the tissue that has already atrophied.

Priya writes. Dr. Rangan waits.

**PRIYA**
I have a photograph of the earlier scan. In my notebook.
The one from four years ago.

**DR. RANGAN**
You've kept it?

**PRIYA**
I photographed it and wrote a note to myself about what it
means. I look at it when I need to remember that this is
structural, not volitional.

*(she finds the page — a printed photograph taped in, with her handwriting below)*

"This is the inside of my head. The dark part is the part
that should hold your memories. It is gone. The notebooks
are what live there instead."

Dr. Rangan is quiet for a moment.

**DR. RANGAN**
That's a remarkably clear description.

**PRIYA**
I've had a long time to arrive at it.

*(writing)*

Can you explain what I'm looking at, for my notes?

Dr. Rangan turns to the light box. Indicates two images — one older, one recent.

**DR. RANGAN**
The earlier scan shows these white areas here — this was active
inflammation. The encephalitis attacking the hippocampal tissue.

*(pointing)*

Four years later: this.

A dark void in the right temporal lobe. The absence is total and absolute.

**DR. RANGAN (CONT'D)**
The white areas have become dark. That means the tissue is gone.
Not damaged. Gone. The nerve cells that formed new declarative
memories — episodic memory, personal memory — they're no longer
there.

Priya is writing rapidly.

**PRIYA**
*(without looking up)*
And the memories I had before the illness?

**DR. RANGAN**
Largely intact. Semantic memory — facts, language, general
knowledge — unaffected. Procedural memory — how to walk, how
to write — unaffected. The hippocampus encodes *new* memories.
Your old ones were already consolidated elsewhere.

**PRIYA**
*(still writing)*
So I know who I was. I can't become anyone new.

A pause.

**DR. RANGAN**
That's not entirely accurate. You *are* becoming someone.
Your notebooks —

**PRIYA**
*(flatly, but not harshly)*
Are a prosthetic. I know.

*(beat)*

A very good one. But the information goes in, not the experience.
I read what I did. I don't feel what I lived.

*(she caps her pen)*

Is there anything else in the scan?

**DR. RANGAN**
*(gently)*
No. You're stable, Priya.

**PRIYA**
Good.
*(she writes)*
"Stable. No change. No recovery expected. This is the shape
of the life."

She closes the notebook. Looks at the scan for a moment.

Then she takes out her phone and photographs it — just as she once photographed the earlier scan.

**PRIYA (CONT'D)**
For the record.

---

**EXT. STREET OUTSIDE CLINIC — DAY**

Priya and Nandita walk.

Priya is writing in the loose notepad as she walks — small, practiced movements, never breaking stride.

**NANDITA**
You don't have to write right now.

**PRIYA**
I know.

*(she keeps writing)*

**NANDITA**
How are you feeling?

**PRIYA**
I won't be able to answer that in ten minutes, so I'm writing
it down now.

*(she shows Nandita the pad)*

"Stable. Which is not the same as fine. But it's what there is."

Nandita looks at the words.

**NANDITA**
*(quietly)*
It's okay, Priya.

Priya looks at her.

**PRIYA**
You always say that.

**NANDITA**
*(simply)*
It's always true.

A pause. They walk.

**PRIYA**
I wrote it down once — that you say it.
*(from memory, rare)*
"Nandita says it's okay. She says it every time. I think
she knows that I won't remember hearing it. But she says it
anyway. Possibly because the saying matters more than the
remembering."

*(beat)*

I think I was right about that.

Nandita looks straight ahead. Something in her face she doesn't let become a full expression.

**NANDITA**
You were right about that.

---

## ACT FOUR: EVERY THURSDAY

---

**INT. PRIYA'S FLAT — MAIN ROOM — THURSDAY MORNING (SEVERAL WEEKS LATER)**

The familiar tableau. Shelf, three notebooks, twelve boxes. The laminated card on the bedside table has been replaced — the original wore thin, and Priya wrote a careful copy. This one is slightly different, because it is made by a person who has done this before.

Priya at her table. The morning protocol complete.

She is reading the green notebook — the social records book. She turns to **KABIR MEHTA** and reads it through. It is longer now. There are new entries she does not remember writing.

She reads:

```
    [DATE] — eighth visit.
    He brought chai again. He always brings chai.
    He asked whether I ever dream about memories I no longer have.
    I said I don't think so, but that I sometimes dream and wake
    up feeling like I was somewhere else, and there is nothing
    to anchor the feeling to.
    He was quiet for a long time after that.
    I don't know what it meant to him.
    I don't think I can know.
    But I wrote it down because it seemed like a moment.
```

And below that, in slightly different pen:

```
    He said something today. I'm not certain he meant for me to
    write it. But I did.
    He said: "I think about you every week. I don't know what
    you'll remember about last Thursday. But I remember
    everything."
    I asked him: does that bother you?
    He said: no. It means one of us is carrying it. That feels
    right to him.
    I wrote: which one?
    He said: both of us, actually. Just in different ways.
```

In the margin, in red ink:

```
    THIS ONE. COME BACK.
```

Priya reads the red-ink note. Reads it again. She doesn't know when she wrote it. She doesn't know what she meant. But she wrote it in red, and red means: this is important enough to survive the morning.

KNOCK AT DOOR.

She checks the time. Writes it. Goes to the door.

Kabir. Chai. The slightly too-formal jacket.

She looks at him. He looks at her. And for a moment, neither speaks.

**PRIYA**
*(reading from the green notebook)*
Eighth visit. No — ninth?

**KABIR**
Ninth.

**PRIYA**
*(writing)*
Ninth.

He comes in. Sits in his chair — it has become his chair through the accumulation of Thursdays that only he remembers.

**PRIYA (CONT'D)**
*(looking up from the notebook)*
I found something you said. In the margin. I wrote it in red.
*(reads)*
*He said: one of us is carrying it. That feels right to him.*

*(beat)*

Did you say that?

**KABIR**
*(simply)*
Yes.

**PRIYA**
Why does it feel right?

Kabir sets down his chai. He has been thinking about this. He has been thinking about this since last Thursday.

**KABIR**
Because I'm an architect. I think about — load-bearing things.
Which structures carry weight so that others don't have to.

*(beat)*

You carry the present perfectly. Every Thursday, you're
completely present — you're not somewhere else, you're not
distracted by everything that happened to you last week.
You're just here. You read what you've written and then
you're here.

*(pause)*

I can carry the history. I have the right kind of memory for it.

Priya is writing. She writes this down word by word.

**PRIYA**
*(writing)*
You make it sound almost — structural. Practical.

**KABIR**
It is practical.

**PRIYA**
*(looking up)*
Most people make it sound like a tragedy.

**KABIR**
Most people aren't architects.

A pause. Something passes between them — something that, for Priya, exists only in this exact moment, and for Kabir will persist for the rest of the week, and for both of them matters in a way that neither is yet saying directly.

**PRIYA**
*(writing)*
I want to ask you something.

**KABIR**
Okay.

**PRIYA**
I have no way of knowing whether I've asked this before.

**KABIR**
Ask it.

**PRIYA**
When you leave on Thursday — what do you think about?

A long pause.

**KABIR**
I think about whether you'll look at the green notebook before
next Thursday. Whether you'll read what you wrote about me
and feel anything, or just understand it.

*(beat)*

I think about whether there's a version of me that exists for
you between Thursdays. Even just on paper.

**PRIYA**
*(very quietly)*
There is. I've read it.

**KABIR**
I know. But reading it isn't the same as —

**PRIYA**
*(finishing)*
Remembering it. No. It's not.

*(beat)*

But it's not nothing, either. Reading about you and then
you walking through the door — there is a continuity in that.
Not the one you'd have with most people. But not nothing.

She looks at what she has written.

**PRIYA (CONT'D)**
I'm writing this down. Even though I won't remember the feeling
of saying it.

**KABIR**
I'll remember the feeling of hearing it.

**PRIYA**
*(a small, real smile)*
One of us is carrying it.

**KABIR**
*(quietly)*
Both of us. Just differently.

---

**INT. PRIYA'S FLAT — MAIN ROOM — LATER**

The chai is finished. The notebooks are covered in new entries.

Kabir is standing — about to go. He has his jacket on. His hands are at his sides.

He is looking at the shelf. The three notebooks. The twelve boxes.

**KABIR**
Can I ask you something?

**PRIYA**
You ask good questions. I wrote that.

**KABIR**
What would happen if one of the boxes was lost?

Long silence.

**PRIYA**
*(very still)*
That question goes into a particular category.

*(beat)*

When I first came home from hospital — before my mother had
organized everything — I misplaced a loose note I had written.
Something important. I couldn't find it.

*(pause)*

I became — the notebooks describe it as a crisis. Two hours.
My mother sat on the floor with me.

*(she touches the nearest box without looking at it)*

The boxes are not records. They are — the notebooks say this
better than I can say it now, because I have written it when
I was feeling it — the boxes are proof that I existed. That
the person who is me *was here.* Kept being here.
If they were gone —

*(she stops)*

The person who wrote this morning's notes would still be here.
But she would be — untethered. Without evidence.

**KABIR**
*(very carefully)*
But you would still be you.

**PRIYA**
*(considering)*
I don't know if I am me without the evidence that I have been.

*(pause)*

My mother wrote the spine labels. The older ones. When she
was still able to. Her handwriting is different from mine.
I can see exactly where she stopped and I had to continue.

*(beat)*

If you erased the boxes, you would erase her too. The version
of her that decided my notebooks were worth keeping. Worth
labeling. Worth making into something a person could look
at and say: *this is a life.*

Kabir does not speak.

**PRIYA (CONT'D)**
*(with a kind of fierce calm)*
So. Do not let anything happen to my boxes.

**KABIR**
I won't.

**PRIYA**
*(writing)*
You said that without hesitation.

**KABIR**
It also didn't require hesitation.

---

**INT. PRIYA'S FLAT — MAIN ROOM — FRONT DOOR**

Kabir at the door. About to leave.

**KABIR**
I want to ask you something and I'm going to ask it even though
I know you may have no sense of what I mean by it.

**PRIYA**
*(pen ready)*
Go ahead.

**KABIR**
I want to come more than once a week.

Priya looks at him.

**PRIYA**
What did you have in mind?

**KABIR**
Every day, if that would be — if that's possible. I know every
morning you start again. I know you'd read the green book and
I'd be an entry, not a memory.

*(beat)*

But I'd be a daily entry. There would be more writing. More
evidence. The entry would grow.

*(pause)*

I'd rather be in the part of you that the notebook *can* hold
than not be in you at all.

Priya is writing. She writes for a long time without looking up.

Then she reads back what she has written, out loud, as if confirming it:

**PRIYA**
"Kabir Mehta wants to come every day. He says he would rather
be in the notebook than not be in it. He understands what
that means — that I will read him every morning and not
remember the previous evening. That for him there is a
continuous thread, and for me there is an entry that grows
longer."

*(she looks up)*

Why?

**KABIR**
Because.
*(and this is difficult for him — he is not someone who
finds this easy)*
Because last week, when I was leaving, I walked to the corner
and thought: she's reading the green book right now. She's
reading it and she doesn't feel what I feel but she
*understands* it, she knows what we talked about.

*(beat)*

That's enough. I decided that's enough.

Silence.

**PRIYA**
*(very quietly)*
You decided alone.

**KABIR**
Yes.

**PRIYA**
Without being able to ask me, because I wouldn't remember
us discussing it.

**KABIR**
*(acknowledging this)*
Yes.

**PRIYA**
*(writing)*
That's — 
*(she writes before she finishes speaking)*
— that's very strange.

**KABIR**
I know.

**PRIYA**
Also. 
*(a pause)*
I would not have chosen this. If I'd had a choice. Any of it.

**KABIR**
I know.

**PRIYA**
But you're asking me now.

**KABIR**
Yes.

She looks at the notebook. Then at him.

**PRIYA**
I'm going to write this in red ink.

*(she does so, very deliberately)*

"KABIR MEHTA WANTS TO COME EVERY DAY. HE HAS THOUGHT
ABOUT WHAT THIS MEANS. HE UNDERSTANDS IT. HE IS ASKING.
I SAID:"

*(she looks up)*

Yes.

Kabir exhales.

**PRIYA (CONT'D)**
*(writing the final word, in red)*
"YES."

*(she closes the notebook)*

*(very quietly)*

Come tomorrow. Come in the morning. Bring chai.

**KABIR**
*(at the door)*
I'll have a regular order.

**PRIYA**
I know. I'll write that down too.

---

**INT. PRIYA'S FLAT — MAIN ROOM — THAT EVENING**

Late. Rain on the window.

Priya at the table. The dark blue night-summary notebook is open. She writes:

```
    [DATE]

    Important parts of today, for the record:

    Kabir Mehta came. Ninth visit. Thursday.
    He brought chai. He always brings chai.
    He asked to come every day.
    I said yes.

    He said: "I'd rather be in the part of you the notebook can
    hold than not be in you at all."
    I wrote this in red. It is important.

    I do not know what I feel about him, because I cannot hold
    feelings past seven seconds. But I know what I wrote. And I
    know that I wrote it in red. And I know that I wrote yes.

    This is what I have. It is not nothing.

    Nandita was right. People will love me. One of them has
    decided to try. I have decided to let him.

    Tomorrow he will come and I will read this and I will open
    the door and he will have brought chai and I will write his
    name at the top of a new timestamp and we will begin.

    We will always be beginning.

    But we will begin together.

    It's okay.
    It really is okay.
```

She closes the notebook.

She sets the pen down.

She looks at the shelf — three notebooks, twelve boxes, her mother's handwriting, her own handwriting, the whole accumulated weight of a life made entirely of paper and trust.

Outside, the rain.

She is still.

Then she picks up the pen again and opens the cream notebook to a fresh page. At the top, she writes:

```
    FOR TOMORROW-PRIYA:
    He is coming.
    His name is in the green book.
    Read what you wrote in red.
    Let him in.
    It's going to be okay.
```

She caps the pen.

SLOW PUSH IN on the shelf. The boxes. The laminated card on the bedside table. The notebooks, waiting.

```
                                                        FADE TO BLACK.
```

## WRITER'S NOTES: THE NEUROLOGICAL GROUND

*These notes are for the director, actors, and crew — the decisions behind what is on the page.*

**On anterograde amnesia:** Priya cannot form new declarative memories — episodic or semantic — due to severe hippocampal atrophy. The condition is not portrayed as confusion or disorientation. She is cognitively intact. She is simply cut off from the continuity that most people take to be the self. She compensates through an externalized memory architecture — the three-notebook system — that functions as a cognitive prosthetic.

**On how Priya speaks and moves:** She should not seem sad or lost. She has adapted. The notebooks are not a workaround for her — they are her. Her manner should be precise rather than distressed. The calculation behind her eyes when she opens the door to Kabir is not grief; it is the practiced work of someone who has made efficiency a form of survival.

**On what Priya reads versus what she feels:** When Priya reads her notebook, she understands but does not feel. This distinction must be played visually — in her face, she processes. She does not soften into memory. There is no nostalgic smile when she reads Kabir's entry. There is recognition of information. The feeling of the previous Thursday does not return with the reading of it. This is the neurological reality, and it is the emotional core of the story.

**On the "it's okay" protocol:** Nandita's repeated phrase is not habit or dismissal. It is a deliberate intervention that works on implicit/emotional memory — the limbic system, which survives hippocampal damage. Priya's body registers Nandita as safe even when her mind cannot hold the history of why. The phrase is a technique, used knowingly. It should be played simply, never performatively.

**On the boxes:** Twelve cardboard boxes, spines labeled in two distinct handwritings — Leela's until it stops, Priya's continuing from that point forward. Priya rarely touches them. They are presence rather than tool. The camera should treat them with weight — not symbolically, but materially. They are not a motif. They are where the life is stored.

**On Kabir:** He is not rescuing her. He is making a structural decision — load-bearing, in his word. He is not romanticizing the condition; he has understood it with great precision and decided, in full knowledge of what it means, that he wants to be present inside its reality. His feeling is real; his entry in her notebook is proof; both coexist.

**On what the notebooks cannot hold:** The screenplay does not attempt to show what Priya feels but cannot record. That territory is intentionally left to silence, to the space between her face and the notebooks. It is there. The notebooks prove it is there. That is enough.
