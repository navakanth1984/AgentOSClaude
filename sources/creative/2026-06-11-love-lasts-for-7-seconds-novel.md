# LOVE LASTS FOR SEVEN SECONDS

*A Novel*

**Navakanth Reddy Dumpa**

---

*For everyone who is always present.*
*For everyone who counts to seven. Stays.*

# PART ONE — THE WORLD SHE BUILT

## Chapter One — 6:14 AM

The sky over Hyderabad bleeds tangerine at its edges, and Priya Anand is watching it happen for the first time.

This is true every morning. It will be true tomorrow.

She stands at the stone parapet of Tank Bund with both hands flat on the railing, and the lake spreads out before her, still as held breath. Hussain Sagar in the last dark before sunrise. The Buddha statue rises from the middle of the water, a grey shape becoming gold at its edges, and Priya looks at it the way you look at something you have never seen before, because she hasn't. Not in any way that counts. Not in any way her mind will let her keep.

A milk truck grinds past on the road behind her. Pigeons adjust their positions on the Necklace Road railings with small bureaucratic complaints. The water does not move.

There is a notebook strapped to her left wrist with a rubber band, and a tablet in a rubber case clipped to her dupatta, and a dozen Post-it notes tucked into the elastic of the wrist notebook, each one a different colour. Her kurta is the colour of turmeric. She does not remember choosing it. Someone chose it — some earlier Priya, this morning, standing in front of a cupboard she trusts more than she trusts her own recollection — and that earlier Priya had good taste, so it worked out.

She pulls a yellow Post-it free and reads it.

YOU ARE PRIYA. YOU ARE 23. YOU ARE SAFE. TODAY IS WEDNESDAY. RAJU BHAIYA KNOWS YOU. THIS IS HOME.

Her lips move with the words. Her thumb runs along the edge of the notebook strap — once, twice, three times — a small mechanical habit, the body's way of saying *received*.

She looks up at the lake.

And then it happens, the way it always happens, the way it has happened every seven seconds for three years: a soft interior slippage, like a page turning in a book held by someone else. Not darkness. Not absence. Just — newness. The lake arrives in her eyes as if it has just been installed. The tangerine sky declares itself. Her gaze drops to the yellow Post-it still in her hand, and she reads it, and she nods once, and she tucks it back into the elastic.

YOU ARE PRIYA. YOU ARE SAFE. THIS IS HOME.

All right, then.

This is her life. Seven seconds at a time. She does not experience it as tragedy, because tragedy requires a before, and she does not carry a before. She carries a notebook, a tablet, twelve Post-its, and a body that knows things her mind will not hold — and that, this morning, like every morning, turns out to be enough.

A chai cart comes squeaking along the path, pushed by a man in his forties built like a question mark, always leaning forward as if into a wind only he can feel. He sees her and his face arranges itself into something practised and gentle, the way you set a table for a guest who arrives every day and never remembers the house.

"Priya madam," he says. "Irani chai?"

She turns. Her eyes search his face. There is a green Post-it in her wrist notebook with his name on it, but she finds she doesn't need it — something in her recognises the lean of him, the squeak of the cart, the specific gravel of his voice, before any note can confirm it. The recognition arrives without a memory attached, the way warmth arrives from a wall that held the sun all day.

"Raju Bhaiya," she says, and a small exhale of relief goes out of her. "Haan."

He pours. The chai falls in a long bronze arc from pot to glass, and she watches it the way other people watch fireworks. Her tablet buzzes against her hip. She looks at the screen.

YOU HAVE BEEN STANDING HERE FOR 11 MINUTES. YOUR MOTHER KNOWS WHERE YOU ARE. YOU ARE SAFE. IT IS 6:14 AM.

She touches the screen gently, accepting the information like a gift, which is what it is. Eleven minutes. Somewhere behind her eyes, ninety resets have come and gone in those eleven minutes, ninety small page-turns, and through all of them her feet have stayed planted on this exact stone, because the route is in her body and the body does not reset.

"Same time, same place," Raju Bhaiya says, handing her the glass. "Every morning."

"Do I always get chai?"

"Always."

She wraps both hands around the glass. The heat travels up through her palms, and the cardamom reaches her before the first sip, and her shoulders drop half an inch — a small, involuntary settling, the body saying *yes, this, I know this*. Some things the body knows even when the mind won't hold them. The heat of a glass. The smell of a lake at dawn. The shape of a morning built, brick by brick, by a girl who decided that forgetting everything was not the same as having nothing.

She sips. The sky goes on bleeding its colours into the water.

Seven seconds.

The lake arrives in her eyes, brand new.

She looks down. There is warm chai in her hands. There is a yellow Post-it at her wrist. *YOU ARE PRIYA. YOU ARE SAFE.*

All right, then.

## Chapter Two — The House of Instructions

Lakshmi Anand had constructed a world, and the world fit inside a two-bedroom flat in Jubilee Hills.

It was not a clinical world. That distinction mattered to her more than she could say, so she had never tried to say it; she had simply built it into the walls. The whiteboards in the kitchen were colour-coded — red for urgent, green for routine, blue for joy — but the markers sat in a steel tumbler that had belonged to her own mother, and the magnets holding the photographs were shaped like mangoes, and the photographs themselves were labelled in a hand that had written two thousand school notes and ten thousand grocery lists and one hospital admission form that had changed everything. The flat was stacked with memory aids the way a temple is stacked with offerings. Not equipment. Devotion.

On the red board, in large letters, she had written: PRIYA'S SEVEN-SECOND LOOP IS REAL. SHE IS NOT PRETENDING. SHE IS NOT CONFUSED. SHE IS YOUR DAUGHTER.

The note was not for Priya. It was for visitors who didn't believe — the relatives who arrived with theories, the well-wishers who suggested ayurvedic doctors and astrologers and discipline. Lakshmi had stopped arguing with them years ago. Now she just stood them in front of the red board and let them read.

She was fifty-two. There were silver streaks in her oiled hair and a specific exhaustion in her face — the exhaustion of someone who has loved longer than she has slept. She stood at the stove this morning making upma, and she did not look up when her son spoke, because she already knew what he was going to say. He said it most mornings.

"She's not back yet."

Kiran stood at the counter eating his breakfast standing up, which he did when he wanted the household to know he was too burdened to sit. Twenty-seven years old, a software engineer, the kind of man whose jaw locked before his mouth opened. He checked his phone. Checked the door. Checked the phone again.

"She's at the lake," Lakshmi said. "Raju Bhaiya texted."

"She shouldn't go alone."

"She goes every morning."

"That doesn't make it—"

"Safe?" Lakshmi set the ladle down. "She built that route herself. The Post-its, the tablet, Raju Bhaiya. It took her six months to build it. Don't dismantle it."

Kiran's jaw tightened. He put the bowl down on the counter, hard enough that the spoon jumped.

"One incident," he said. "One bad person who realises she resets every—"

"Kiran."

He stopped. Not because the word was loud. It wasn't. His mother's voice carried weight the way the foundation of a building carries weight — silently, totally, without needing to be noticed until you tried to move against it.

"She is not a problem to be solved," Lakshmi said. "She is your sister."

He picked up his bag. He left. The door did not slam, which was somehow worse — a door closed with that much control was a man swallowing an argument whole and taking it to work in his stomach.

Lakshmi stood alone in the kitchen. Her hand found the edge of the counter and her fingers pressed into the tile, and she let out one breath that had been held, by her private accounting, for approximately three years.

Then she picked up the ladle and went back to the upma, because the upma did not care about anyone's grief, and there was a kind of mercy in that.

The flat ticked around her. On the green board: PRIYA — LAKE TILL 7:30. BUS PASS IN BLUE BAG. On the blue board, in Priya's own handwriting, from some evening when the window of her continuity had stretched long enough for her to climb a chair and write it: AMMA MAKES THE HOUSE SMELL LIKE HOME. KEEP THIS.

Lakshmi did not look at the blue board while she cooked. She knew it was there the way you know your own pulse is there.

She kept it. She kept everything. That was the job — to be the memory her daughter could not be, to hold the full unbroken length of the story so that Priya only ever had to hold seven seconds of it. People called it a burden when they were being kind and a tragedy when they were being honest, and they were wrong both times. You could not explain this to anyone who hadn't lived it: there was a fierce, almost holy privilege in being the keeper of someone's days. Her daughter woke every morning into a world with no yesterday. It was Lakshmi's work to make sure that world, every single time, was worth waking into.

The upma steamed. Outside, three floors down, Hyderabad cleared its throat and began.

## Interlude — What Lakshmi Keeps

The hospital corridor had a specific light, and Lakshmi would carry that light for the rest of her life: fluorescent, slightly green, the light of places where time is measured in test results.

Priya was twenty when it happened. A fever first — ordinary, three days, the kind every mother has nursed a hundred times. Then the fever turned, became something with a long name, and for nine days her daughter lay in a ward in Somajiguda while a virus, with no malice and no plan, made its small precise fire in the part of her brain where moments are turned into yesterday.

Encephalitis, the doctors said. And then, later, when the fever had gone and the damage stayed: anterograde amnesia, severe, atypical. The neurologist — a kind man who looked progressively less kind as he ran out of better news — explained it with diagrams. The hippocampus. Consolidation. The machinery that moves experience from the bright front room of the mind into the long archive at the back. The fire had taken the corridor between the rooms. The front room still worked — worked perfectly. The archive still held everything from before — her childhood, her schooling, her mother's face as it had been at fifty. But nothing new could make the journey. Every experience lived its seven seconds in the bright front room and then, finding no corridor, vanished.

"How long will it last?" Lakshmi asked.

The doctor looked at his diagrams as if they might have changed.

"You should set up systems," he said.

The first year was the war. There is no other word for it, and Lakshmi, who had been raised to choose gentle words, stopped looking for one. Priya woke every morning into terror — a body three years older than the last thing it remembered, a calendar that had betrayed her, a mirror running ahead of her at full speed. She would scream for minutes that for her were the first minutes of the catastrophe, every single day, because every single day the catastrophe was new. Lakshmi learned to be in the room before her daughter woke. Learned the exact sentences that worked and the exact sentences that made it worse. Wrote the sentences down. Taped them to the mirror.

GOOD MORNING, PRIYA. TODAY IS [DAY]. YOU ARE LOVED. CHECK YOUR TABLET. DRINK WATER. YOU ARE SAFE.

Her husband did not survive the first year. Not in the way of dying — in the way of leaving, by degrees, into longer office hours and then a transfer he did not fight and then a flat in Pune and then a silence that formalised itself into papers. Lakshmi signed them at the same kitchen table where she would later draw the first colour-coded whiteboard. She did not hate him. This was the thing she could never make anyone understand, least of all Kiran, who hated him enough for the household entire. Some people cannot live inside a loop. Some people need the story to go somewhere, and when it doesn't — when the story becomes the same seven seconds, repeated, forever — they starve. He had starved. She had watched him starve while her daughter, who actually lived in the loop, somehow didn't.

That was the discovery of the second year, the one nobody had prepared her for: Priya adapting. Not recovering — the corridor was gone, the doctors had been honest about the corridor — but building. It started with the sketchbook. Then the Post-its, which were Priya's own idea, arrived at in one of the early long windows: "If I can't keep anything inside," she said, twenty-one years old, hollow-eyed, holding the pad of yellow squares like a tool she had just invented, "then I'll keep it outside." Then the colour system. Then the tablet, which Kiran programmed, pouring his guilt and his fear into code because code was the language he could pour them into. Then the routes. Then Raju Bhaiya, whom Priya had selected herself — had tested, though Lakshmi would not learn about the testing protocol for another year — as the fixed star of her mornings.

By the third year, her daughter had done the thing that Lakshmi still could not explain to the relatives who came with their theories and their astrologers: she had built a life. Not a managed decline. Not a supervised existence. A life — with joy in it, filed under blue.

People said to Lakshmi, in the tone reserved for condolences: *It must be so hard. She'll never remember anything you do for her.*

And Lakshmi had stopped explaining, because the explanation sounded like madness to anyone who hadn't lived it: *She remembers nothing, and she trusts me completely. Every morning she reads a note that says her mother loves her, and every morning she believes it without needing the evidence, and then I get to spend the whole day being the evidence.*

There was a Post-it on the inside of Lakshmi's own cupboard door, where no one else ever looked. She had written it in the worst month of the first year and never taken it down:

SHE IS NOT GONE. SHE IS HERE MORE THAN ANYONE.
YOUR JOB IS TO MAKE HERE WORTH IT.

She read it most mornings. It was, she supposed, her one blue note.

## Chapter Three — Orange Is a Feeling

The Kalakar design studio occupied the second floor of a glass-and-concrete building in Madhapur, and at 9:40 that morning its doors burst open to admit Aditya Sharma, who was late, who was always late, who had elevated lateness to something resembling a design philosophy.

He came through the lobby with a rolled tube of concept art under one arm and a vending-machine cold coffee sweating in his free hand. Twenty-five years old, lanky, the kind of handsome that snuck up on people around the third conversation. Born in Hyderabad to a Punjabi family, he spoke Telugu with a Delhi accent that embarrassed him precisely enough to be charming. He did not look late. He looked like lateness was a style choice, which was the most annoying thing about him, his colleagues agreed, in a field of strong contenders.

Meera was already at her desk, because Meera was always already at her desk. She was the sharpest person in any room she walked into, Hyderabad-born, a Telugu-English code-switcher who wore a saree to a startup office because she could and because it unsettled exactly the right people. She did not look up.

"Client presentation is in twenty minutes."

"I know."

"You were supposed to finalise the brand board last night."

"I did."

"You sent me a voice note at 2 AM humming what you described as 'the colour orange.'"

"That's a creative brief." He was already unrolling the concept art across the spare table. "Orange is a feeling."

Meera turned in her chair to deliver the look she kept for these moments — and stopped. The board lay open on the table, and despite herself, despite eleven months of accumulated evidence that Aditya Sharma needed to be managed like weather, it was beautiful. Warm terracotta bleeding into saffron, a deep teal holding the bottom of the composition like a held bass note. He had taken the colour language of the old city — the wet-wall ochre of Begum Bazaar, the iridescent green of a Charminar dupatta stall — and translated it into branding so clean it looked inevitable.

"You're lucky you're good," she said.

"I'm also charming." He was already heading for the presentation room.

"Debatable," she said, under her breath, to the empty doorway.

He was good, though — that was the genuinely irritating part. He saw the way light moved in things. He could walk past a bangle stall he'd passed a hundred times and stop dead because the noon sun had entered the glass at a new angle, and twenty minutes later that angle would be a gradient in somebody's logo. What he could not do, in Meera's professional assessment, was point that attention at his own life. He noticed everything and attached to nothing. He was twenty-five and had treated Hyderabad like a layover for three years — beautiful, sure, but surely not the destination. The city kept offering itself to him in colour and he kept converting the colour into deliverables and sending it back.

He won the client over in eighteen minutes, orange-as-a-feeling and all. Afterwards he stood at the window of the presentation room with the dregs of the cold coffee, looking north, where past the towers of Hitech City the land dipped away towards a lake he jogged beside sometimes and had never once really seen.

That was the morning of the day his life changed. He would spend a long time afterwards trying to remember whether he'd felt anything — some pull, some premonition, the universe clearing its throat.

He hadn't. It had been a Wednesday. The coffee had been too sweet. That was all.

## Chapter Four — The Collision

Priya has a routine she doesn't know she has.

Between ten and noon she walks the same four hundred metres of boardwalk, and the route is marked in her tablet's GPS as a blue line, and every hundred metres the tablet murmurs to her: *you are still on your path*. She carries a sketchbook. Not for words — for shapes. She draws the lake. The kites. The Buddha rising out of the water. She draws quickly, because she has seven seconds and then everything resets, so each drawing is seven seconds of pure looking — no composition anxiety, no second-guessing, no comparing this drawing to the last one, because there is no last one. There is only ever this one.

She fills page after page with seven-second views of the world.

A child runs past trailing a kite string, and her pencil catches the angle of it — the taut diagonal, the small fist. Reset. She looks down at the drawing on her page. A kite. A child's arm. It is good, and she smiles at what her hands made, the uncomplicated smile of someone praising a stranger's work, because that is what it is to her. Every page in this sketchbook is a gift from a stranger who happens to share her handwriting.

There is something in her face as she draws that the morning walkers have learned to glance at and then look away from, embarrassed, the way you look away from something too private to watch. It is wonder. Pure, undefended, uncomplicated wonder. She does not know she has been on this boardwalk a hundred times.

Every time is the first time.

---

Aditya was on the boardwalk because of a song.

A Telugu indie band had put out a single that sounded like rainfall on a tin roof translated into guitar, and it was exactly the wrong music to work to and exactly the right music to run to, so he was running, earphones in, entirely inside his own head, doing nine and a half minutes a kilometre, which he would have called jogging and any athlete would have called walking with commitment.

He rounded the bend past the second drink stall.

The collision was not dramatic. That bothered him later, when he had reasons to want the story to be better — there was no slow-motion, no spilled flowers, none of the cinema his brain had been trained on. He simply came around the curve with his eyes on the water, and she was there, mid-step, sketchbook raised, and his shoulder caught hers and she stumbled, and the sketchbook went down flat on the stone with a sound like a single clap.

His hand caught her elbow before he knew he'd moved. Automatic. The body deciding before the mind.

"Sorry, sorry — I wasn't looking—"

She looked at him.

Afterwards, he would try many times to describe what was different about it, and would fail in front of Meera, in front of Kiran, in front of his own sketchbook at two in the morning. The closest he ever got was this: everyone he had ever met looked at him through a film of preparation. People assembled a face before they showed it to you — interest, politeness, wariness, charm — everyone arrived pre-arranged. Her eyes were completely open. No calculation. No performance of meeting-a-stranger. Just direct, total presence, as if the full attention of an entire person had been poured into one look with nothing held back in reserve, because — though he didn't know it yet — nothing could be held in reserve. Reserve requires a yesterday to store things in.

He didn't know what to do with it. Nobody ever knew what to do with it.

She glanced at the sketchbook on the ground. Back at him. And then — he saw it happen, the very first time, without understanding what he was seeing — something turned over softly behind her eyes, a page going blank, and the boardwalk and the lake and his face arrived in her all over again, new.

"Hello," she said.

He blinked. "...Hi? I just — I almost knocked you over—"

"Did you?"

"Just now. You dropped your—" He picked up the sketchbook and held it out.

She took it and opened it to the last page, the way you'd check a parcel. The kite drawing. The child's arm, the taut string.

"This is good," she said, delighted. "Did I do this?"

He stared at her. "You were drawing when I ran into you. Just now."

"Oh." She studied the drawing with friendly curiosity. "I don't remember drawing it."

A pause. Her tablet buzzed. She turned it over and read the screen without any attempt to hide it from him — and that, too, he noted without understanding: no embarrassment, none of the shame people carry around their own machinery.

YOU JUST MET SOMEONE. IT'S OKAY.

"I should explain something to you," she said.

"You really don't have to—"

"My memory resets every seven seconds. So I won't remember this conversation. But I'm very glad to be having it."

He processed this the way you process a sentence in a language you thought you spoke. His mouth opened. Closed. She watched him with enormous patience — the patience, though he could not have known it, of someone who has stood at this exact threshold of someone's comprehension many hundreds of times and retains not one of them.

"Every... seven seconds."

"Give or take."

"So right now—"

"Right now I know your face, what you said, and that you have kind hands."

He looked down at his hands. They were, objectively, just hands. There was ink on the right thumb.

"How do you know where you are?" he said. "Where to go? How do you—"

Her tablet buzzed again. She glanced at it.

"My family. Post-its. A very patient chai vendor. And—" she lifted the sketchbook, "—I draw what I see. So even if I forget seeing it, I made it."

A beat. The wind came off the lake and moved between them, carrying water-smell and far-off traffic.

"That's incredible," he said, and heard, as he said it, how completely he meant it.

"It's Tuesday. Or Wednesday." She checked her wrist. "The Post-it says Wednesday."

She smiled then — bright, clean, weaponised in its innocence — and something behind Aditya Sharma's sternum performed a manoeuvre he had no name for, a sensation he would have described, if forced, as the feeling of a door he didn't know he had being opened by someone who didn't know she was opening it.

He fell into step beside her. It was not planned. It was not decided. It just happened, the way the important things do.

"I'm Aditya."

"Priya." A beat. "You'll have to tell me again in a few seconds."

"Aditya," he said, half-laughing. "Graphic designer. Born here, raised here, pretends the Delhi accent is cool."

"Is it?"

"Absolutely not."

She laughed — a real laugh, head back, shoulders in, the whole instrument engaged — and then, mid-stride, the page turned. He watched her surface from it. She looked at him: a stranger, walking beside her, at a polite distance, on her boardwalk.

"Who are you?"

He was ready for it this time, or thought he was. Something in his posture adjusted — became more careful, more deliberate, the body of a man choosing to take a test he had not studied for.

"Aditya. We just met. You drew a kite and I almost knocked you into the lake."

"Did you apologise?"

"I did."

"Are you a good person?" She asked it the way other people ask the time.

He gave it a second, because the question deserved one. "Mostly. I'm working on the rest."

She nodded, satisfied by this in some deep procedural way, as if honesty about the percentage mattered more than the percentage.

"Walk with me?"

He did.

---

Raju Bhaiya clocked the stranger from twenty metres out.

His eyes performed the specific calculation of a man who had watched over Priya madam every morning for a year — height, gait, distance maintained, hands visible — and by the time the two of them reached his cart, the calculation had returned a provisional verdict of *acceptable, pending interview*.

"Madam," he said to Priya, neutrally. "New friend?"

Priya checked her tablet. "This is Aditya. I met him just now."

Raju Bhaiya turned to Aditya and lowered his voice into a register reserved for state business. "You know about her condition?"

"She told me."

The chai-seller held his gaze for three full seconds. Aditya stood inside the look and let it do its work, understanding instinctively that flinching would be answered and that charm — his usual exit — would be catastrophic.

"I'm here every morning, six to noon," Raju Bhaiya said. "I have her mother's number."

The implication was complete and required no elaboration.

"I understand," Aditya said.

Raju poured. He handed the first glass to Priya, who had already moved on — she was looking out at the Buddha statue standing on the water, her face wide open.

"Look at that," she said. "Is that always there?"

"Since 1992," Aditya said, accepting the second glass.

"It looks new."

He looked at the statue — grey stone, thirty years of weather, the most photographed object in the city, a thing he had jogged past two hundred times and stopped seeing in the first month. Then he looked at her face, seeing it fresh.

"Yeah," he said. "It does."

## Chapter Five — The Name with a Star

Priya comes home in the late afternoon, and the flat receives her the way it has been built to receive her.

She reads the whiteboard by the door. She reads the Post-its on the door itself, arranged at her eye height in a vertical line, like the markings on a ruler that measures safety instead of length. Her tablet chimes its arrival note: YOU ARE HOME. YOUR BROTHER IS IN THE LIVING ROOM. HIS NAME IS KIRAN. HE IS SAFE.

She enters. A man on the sofa with a laptop. *Kiran. Brother. Safe.* He looks up, and his face does a thing faces do around her — a quick covert scan, reading her the way she reads her notes.

"How was the lake?"

"Good," she says. "There was a man."

The change in her brother is total and instantaneous. The laptop is closed. The spine is straight. The whole body comes to a kind of operational attention.

"What man?"

"A graphic designer. He was kind." She looks down at her sketchbook, flips it open, going back through the day's pages the way an archaeologist goes through a stranger's luggage. The lake. A kite. The chai cart. And then — she stops. "I drew a kite. And there's a new page here — just the word 'Aditya' written in my own handwriting. With a little star."

She holds the book out. It's true. There on the page, in her own unmistakable hand, between a sketch of the Buddha and a study of a kingfisher: ADITYA ★. At some point inside her loop — in some seven-second window now permanently sealed to her — she stopped, took the pencil, and left herself a breadcrumb she no longer knows the reason for.

"You wrote a man's name in your sketchbook," Kiran says.

"It seems I liked him."

"Priya—"

"Kiran." She closes the book and holds it against her chest, and her voice has the calm of a person who has had to become an expert witness on her own behalf. "The star is good. I wouldn't draw a star next to someone who wasn't good."

She goes to her room. The door clicks.

Kiran sits with the sketchbook for a long time after she has gone to bed — she left it on the table; she will not remember leaving it — and looks at the name. The handwriting is hers. The star is hers. The certainty of it is hers, and that is the problem, that is exactly the problem: his sister, who cannot remember a face for seven consecutive seconds, has issued a verdict on a stranger and signed it with a star, and there is no court in which he can cross-examine the evidence, because the witness is gone. The witness is always gone.

ADITYA. With a star.

He took a photo of the page. He was not proud of it.

## Chapter Six — The Balcony

Aditya's apartment in Banjara Hills had a balcony that justified the rent, and at eleven that night he sat on it with his sketchbook open on his knee and the city spread below — Hyderabad after dark, a quilt of gold and white stitched with the red tail-lights of the Ring Road.

He was supposed to be working on a client brief. The file sat open on the laptop behind him, cursor blinking in an empty artboard, patient as a creditor.

He drew, instead, a face. Eyes that held the world like it was entirely new.

He was still looking at the drawing when the phone buzzed. Meera.

"You looked distracted in the afternoon check-in," she said. "What happened?"

"Nothing. I met someone at the lake."

"Someone."

"A girl. She has this condition where her memory resets every seven seconds."

The silence on the line had texture. He could hear her recalibrating.

"Aditya."

"I know."

"That's not a situation. That's a responsibility."

"I know."

"Do you?"

He looked at the drawing. The pencil eyes looked back, open, unguarded, already better than anything in the client file would ever be.

"I went back to the lake after she left," he said. "I sat there for two hours."

"Oh no."

"Every seven seconds she looked at me like she was seeing me for the first time. And every time she did—" He stopped. Started again, because Meera was the one person he didn't perform for. "Every time she did, I wanted to be worth seeing."

A long silence. Below the balcony, an auto took the curve with its radio playing an old Ilaiyaraaja song, the sound rising and falling away like something thrown and caught.

"You're in trouble," Meera said.

"Yes," he said.

## Chapter Seven — Learning the Rhythm

He was at the boardwalk before her the next morning, which meant he was at the boardwalk at six, which meant he had been awake at four-thirty staring at his ceiling rehearsing introductions like a man preparing for the strangest job interview of his life — one where the interviewer would be new every seven seconds and the job was simply to be there.

He stood at the parapet, nervous in a way he did not usually permit himself to be. The lake was iron-grey going gold.

Priya arrived at 6:22, same route, the body keeping the appointment the mind knew nothing about. She read her Post-it at the top of the steps. She accepted the chai Raju Bhaiya had already poured — the cart had arrived before either of them; Raju Bhaiya regarded punctuality as a moral category — and she walked the boardwalk, and Aditya stepped forward into her morning.

She saw him. A stranger, placed neatly in her path, at a respectful distance.

"Good morning," she said.

"Good morning. My name is Aditya. We met yesterday. You wrote my name in your sketchbook with a star."

He had thought about the sentence all night. It needed to carry everything and demand nothing — who he was, that there was a yesterday, that the yesterday was hers as much as his, and that the evidence was in her own hand where she could audit it.

She stopped. Looked at him. Looked down at her sketchbook, found the page, the name, the star. Looked back at him, and he watched her conduct the entire trial in four seconds — handwriting verified, star admitted into evidence, verdict reached.

"I did," she said.

"I'm hoping that means something."

"It means I trusted you enough to leave myself a note." A beat. "That's quite a lot, for me."

He exhaled. And then the page turned — he was learning to see it now, the soft going-blank, the world reinstalling itself in her eyes — and she blinked and found a stranger standing in front of her, and found, also, that her own hand had drifted to the sketchbook page with the star, as if it had been waiting there.

"Hello," she said. "Are you Aditya? There's a note about you."

"That's me. Aditya. You were just telling me what the star means."

"What does it mean?"

"That you trusted me."

She considered this, looking at the star, her thumb moving along the edge of the page.

"Then I suppose you should sit with me a while," she said.

He did.

---

The mornings that followed acquired, without either of them announcing it, the structure of a practice.

The first morning he brought a drawing pad and sat beside her and drew the lake while she drew the lake — seven-second pictures from two different pairs of eyes. At the end he showed her his page and she showed him hers, and hers were better, and he told her so, and she said "I have an unfair advantage, I have no idea what I did last time," and he laughed hard enough that a jogger turned around. She added a star to her page.

The second morning he learned to introduce himself inside the window — to land name, context, and evidence in under seven seconds, the world's strangest elevator pitch, refined take after take. He began to feel the rhythm of the loop the way you feel a song's time signature without counting it: presence, presence, presence, *turn*, rebuild. He started to sense the reset half a beat before it arrived, something minute and tidal in her breathing.

The third morning he brought her a red Post-it. He had asked Raju Bhaiya about the colour system first — the chai-seller had answered with the gravity of a man disclosing state codes — and he had written it the night before, four drafts, in his best lettering: ADITYA IS SAFE. HE DRAWS THINGS. HE KNOWS.

She read it. She tucked it into her wrist notebook with the others. Then she looked at him, and her thumb ran along the Post-it's edge — once, twice, three times.

"You wrote this?"

"I asked Raju Bhaiya what format you use."

"No one's ever made me a Post-it before," she said. The sentence came out level, but her thumb kept moving along the paper's edge, and Aditya — who watched hands for a living, who had built a career on noticing where the light enters glass — understood that he had just been present for something larger than its container.

The fourth morning it rained, and they stood under the chai cart's umbrella while the lake went silver, and Priya drew the rain in fast vertical strokes — seven seconds of rain, repeated, page after page — and Aditya watched her hands and made no move at all towards his own pad.

The fifth morning, a man appeared at the far end of the boardwalk and did not approach. Compact, deliberate, arms folded — watching them with the fixed patience of security footage. Aditya recognised the jawline from the framed photo Priya had once shown him on her tablet's family screen. He raised a hand. Kiran Anand did not wave back.

Priya noticed none of it. She was watching a kingfisher hold its impossible line above the water.

## Chapter Eight — The Test

Aditya never told anyone the whole of it — not Meera, not Kiran, not even, in any complete way, Priya herself, who had asked about it once at Golconda and gotten the short true version. The long true version he kept, because it was the closest thing he had to a conversion story, and conversion stories don't survive being told at parties.

It was the second week. He had learned the introductions by then, learned the rhythm, learned the geometry — and he had begun, without noticing, to feel competent. That was the word for the disease of it. He arrived at the boardwalk that morning with two cups of chai and a small contented sense of having mastered the difficult interface of her, and the lake should have darkened with foreboding, but lakes don't do that outside of films.

She was at the railing. He came up the steps — loose stone, step left — and said his sentence, name, profession, no sugar, and held out the cup.

And she looked at him, and the page turned, and something went wrong.

He never found out what. Maybe his shadow fell at an angle that some buried circuitry read as threat. Maybe his voice that morning had a register in it left over from an argument with a client. Maybe nothing — maybe the dice of her seven seconds simply came up dark, the way anyone's seven seconds sometimes do, except that anyone else's seven seconds have context wrapped around them like padding, and hers stood naked and alone. Whatever it was: she surfaced from the reset, and saw a strange man one arm's length away holding something out to her, and the morning shattered.

"Get away from me."

The cup stopped in the air between them.

"Priya — I'm Aditya, we—"

"I don't know you." Her voice had gone to a pitch he had never heard from her, and her hand was scrabbling at the wrist notebook, but fear is faster than paper, fear is the oldest system in the building and it does not wait for the notes. "I don't know you. Get away from me."

Every instinct he owned told him to fix it. To stay, to talk, to produce the evidence — the star in her sketchbook, the Post-it in her own handwriting — to litigate. He was a man who fixed things with charm and documentation, and he stood there with his whole toolkit in his hands.

And he put it down.

"Okay," he said, and stepped back. One step. Two. "I'm going."

He set the chai on the far end of the ledge, where she could reach it or not, and he walked down the steps and along the path and did not look back, and behind him — though he didn't see it; Raju Bhaiya saw it, and Raju Bhaiya was the one who told Kiran, and Kiran told no one until much later — Priya stood gripping the railing through the next reset, and the next, the fear draining out of each fresh present like water finding its level, until at last she looked down and found the notebook and read ADITYA. HE DRAWS THINGS. and a stranger's chai going cold on the stone, and wrote, slowly, in the wrist notebook, the entry the inside cover would carry from then on:

TESTED: PASSED.

She did not remember administering the test. The test did not require her to. That was the genius of the system she had built: it ran without an operator. Anyone could be kind to her face — her face was new every seven seconds; kindness to it cost nothing. The system measured the only thing that couldn't be performed: what a man does in the moment her fear erases all his credit, when no apology will be remembered, when staying would be forgiven by the reset and leaving earns him nothing.

He left. He earned nothing. The nothing was the score.

The next morning he came up the steps with two cups, not knowing if he was welcome, and she turned at the sound of his footsteps and her notebook was already open and she said, "The note says you're safe. Did something happen yesterday? There's a new entry."

"You asked me to leave," he said. "I left."

She studied him for a moment over the rim of the cup.

"Good answer," she said, and he understood that he had just been told the result of an examination he hadn't known he was sitting, and that somewhere in the oldest part of him, below charm, below documentation, he had passed.

## Chapter Nine — The Brother

Kiran had Aditya's LinkedIn, Instagram, and college records by Thursday, and he was not proud of that either, and he did it anyway, because pride was a luxury for people whose sisters could remember the faces of the men who hurt them.

"He's there every morning now," he told Lakshmi, standing in the kitchen doorway. "This Aditya. Graphic designer from Banjara Hills."

"You researched him?"

"I have his LinkedIn, Instagram, and college records."

Lakshmi set the ladle down. Turned.

"Kiran."

"He's building a routine with her." The words came faster now, the case file opening. "She adds his Post-it to the notebook every morning. She draws him. She's leaving herself breadcrumbs back to him."

"That's beautiful."

"What if he hurts her?"

"What if he loves her?"

"She won't remember it."

"She doesn't remember breakfast," Lakshmi said, quietly. "She still gets hungry."

The sentence went into Kiran and stayed there. His jaw loosened — just for a moment, just a degree, the lock slipping before it caught again.

"I need to meet him."

"Then invite him to dinner. Don't interrogate a stranger on a boardwalk like you're CBI."

He interrogated the stranger on the boardwalk like he was CBI.

He arrived before Aditya the next morning and placed himself at the top of the steps, and when the designer came up them the two men faced each other in the flat early light. Kiran was four inches shorter and somehow filled more of the boardwalk.

"Kiran Anand. Priya's brother."

"Aditya Sharma. I know who you are."

"She doesn't remember you between days. You know that?"

"I know."

"She wakes up every morning with no memory of the day before. You're not building a relationship. You're building the same conversation every morning with someone who can't—"

"Build it with me. Yes. I know."

"Then why—"

"Because she draws me every morning without knowing why." Aditya's voice stayed level, but his hands had stopped moving, and a man whose hands stop moving is a man who has put everything into the words. "And she writes my name with a star. And she doesn't know she does it. Her body knows. Her hands know. Even if her memory doesn't."

Kiran stared at him for a long moment, conducting his own seven-second trial with three years of fear for a jury.

"You're either very good or very dangerous," he said.

"I'm trying to be the first one."

A long beat. The lake slapped softly at the stone below them.

"My mother wants to meet you," Kiran said. "Dinner. Friday. Don't be late."

He walked away. Aditya stood at the top of the steps and let the breath go out of him in a long unsteady thread.

## Chapter Ten — Tangedu

For the dinner, Lakshmi cooked with intention: Hyderabadi biryani in the old style, baingan ka salan, double ka meetha sweating ghee at its edges. The table was set formally, which in the Anand house meant the steel plates with the engraved border, the ones that had survived three moves and one flood.

Aditya arrived with flowers. Not roses — roses would have been a man reciting from the standard script. He came holding a newspaper cone of tangedu, the Telangana state flower, yellow and wild and faintly medicinal-sweet, the kind of thing that grew in ditches and on temple walls and in the borders of every drawing Priya had ever made of the lake.

Lakshmi opened the door, saw the flowers, and something behind her eyes shifted — a recalibration, an entire category of doubt being quietly retired.

"How did you know?"

"I asked Raju Bhaiya what she draws the most."

"Come in, Aditya."

Priya was at the dining table, rearranging the Post-its on the day's whiteboard with the absorption of a curator hanging a show. She looked up. A stranger in the doorway holding yellow flowers. Her hand went to her wrist notebook; she read the blue Post-it without hurry.

ADITYA. SAFE. DRAWS THINGS. FRIEND.

"You're the one with the star," she said.

"That's me."

She smiled, wide and genuine. "Did you bring flowers?"

He handed her the tangedu. She held the cone to her face and breathed in, and her eyes closed, and from inside the dark behind her own eyelids she said: "These grow near the lake."

"You draw them a lot."

The page turned. She surfaced, found herself holding flowers, found a stranger across the table, found the smell of biryani in the air and a blue note at her wrist.

"Hello. Are you joining us for dinner?"

"I am," he said, sitting down across from her, finding the rhythm. "Kiran's mother invited me."

"Amma's cooking is the reason I know I'm home before I read the Post-it."

Through the meal, Kiran watched the designer navigate his sister's loop. Every seven seconds the conversation died and was reborn, and every time, the man found a different door back in. *I'm Aditya — you were just telling me about the lake. Aditya. You said the biryani smells like Wednesday. Me again. You just laughed at something I said — I think it was about the chai.* Never robotic. Never weary. Each re-entry built slightly differently, custom-fitted to whatever seven seconds had just been lost.

Priya did not know she'd already had this conversation four times in ten minutes. But she laughed each time, and the laugh was real every time, and Kiran sat there confronting a fact he had no shelf for: his sister was having a wonderful evening that she would not remember, and it was no less wonderful for that, and the man across the table seemed to be one of perhaps five people on earth who understood this without being taught.

Lakshmi watched too. Under the table, she clasped her hands.

"Do you understand," she said, picking her way into a gap between resets, "what you're... what this would be?"

"You'd be re-introducing me to her every morning," Aditya said. "She'd never know I was there the day before."

"Most people find that—"

"Heartbreaking?"

"Unsustainable."

Aditya looked across the table. Priya had discovered the biryani bowl was beautiful and was drawing it, head bent, pencil quick, entirely gone into the work.

"She's drawing the biryani bowl because she loves it," he said. "She won't remember the drawing tomorrow. But she drew it. It happened. It was real." He turned back to Lakshmi. "I want to be something that happened. Even if she forgets the happening."

Lakshmi pressed her lips together. Once. It was the gesture of a woman closing a door on tears the way you close a door on a room you intend to come back to later, alone.

Kiran looked at his plate.

---

That night, in her room — the room that was a masterpiece of adaptive design, whiteboard walls, labelled photographs, the mirror with its morning script taped to the glass — Priya opened her sketchbook to the day's pages.

The dinner. A man across the table, drawn in nine quick lines. The biryani bowl. Kiran's serious face. Amma's hands.

And one word at the bottom of the last page, in her own writing: ADITYA ★.

She touched the star. She didn't know why she'd drawn it. But something in her chest was warm — sourceless, like the heat off a wall after sunset — and she had learned, over three years, to treat that warmth as data.

She recorded her voice note, the nightly ritual, a message thrown forward to the woman who would wake up in her bed tomorrow:

"Today something good happened. I don't remember it. But I drew a lot of stars, so I think you should trust the morning. Good night, Priya."

She clicked off the recorder and set it in its labelled spot.

Outside, three floors down, Hyderabad hummed to itself in its sleep.

## Chapter Eleven — The Evening Ritual

Evening is the hardest engineering problem, and Priya solved it herself, and nobody outside the family knows how beautiful the solution is.

Morning has the mirror script and the notes and Amma in the next room. Daytime has the routes, the tablet, the boardwalk, the blue GPS line. But evening — evening is when the light changes and the body gets tired, and a tired body reads the world differently, and there is an hour, somewhere around dusk, when the resets land harder. The clinicians have a word for it that she keeps on a red note and never reads. Her own word for it is better: the brackish hour. The hour when the day's water and the night's water mix.

This is what she built for it.

At six, the lamps come on — warm bulbs, chosen over three weekends of testing, because the overhead tube light made every reset arrive like an interrogation and the warm lamps make it arrive like being woken gently from a nap. At six-fifteen, music: the eighties Telugu songs, Amma's songs, because melody is body-territory, and a known song running under a reset is a handrail running under a dark staircase. She surfaces, and for half a second she doesn't know the room — and then the song is there, already familiar, telling her: *somewhere, you have heard me a hundred times. You have a somewhere. Stand down.*

At seven she reads the wall. All of it, every evening, slowly, left to right, red then green then blue, the day's additions last. This is not checking. This is the *Ramayana* recited nightly; this is liturgy. The wall is the one text in her life that accumulates, and reading it whole is the closest she comes to what other people must feel paging through their own remembered years. Here is a woman who tested a stranger and wrote SAFE. Here is a woman who stood at Golconda. Here is a woman whose brother said she picked well. All of them her. All of them vouching for her, in her own hand, across the unbridgeable distance of days.

At nine, the voice note. She holds the recorder close, because the recorder has heard everything and judged nothing:

"Good evening, Priya. It's me. You. Today the notes say you drew six pages and the chai was good and nothing red happened. I don't remember the afternoon and you won't remember this sentence, but listen — we are doing well. Whatever you wake into tomorrow, you built it. Trust the woman who built it. Good night, Priya."

She files the recorder in its labelled place. She lies down in the dark with the wall glowing faintly to her left like a banked fire.

And here is the thing she has told no one, because it would frighten Amma and mystify the doctors: she is not afraid of sleep. Other people, she has gathered from books, fear losing the day to it. She loses every day, in arrears, seven seconds at a time; sleep takes nothing from her that waking wasn't already taking. What sleep gives her instead is the same thing it gives everyone, undiminished — the body slowing, the warmth gathering, the soft dissolve. And in the morning a script on a mirror, written by people who love her, telling her where the day's door is.

Every night she falls asleep somewhere in the middle of a reset, and so her last conscious moment is always the same moment everyone's is: this one. Just this one.

She has decided this means she falls asleep the way the lake does. Holding nothing. Reflecting everything.

It is enough. She wrote that on a blue note once, the first blue note, the one all the others descend from, and it is pinned at the top corner of the wall like a north star:

IT IS ENOUGH.

## Chapter Twelve — Charminar

Three weeks later, Lakshmi arranged an afternoon in the old city, and Priya walks through the Charminar bazaar with her sketchbook up and her pencil moving, and the world pours itself at her in seven-second instalments and she catches what she can.

Bangle stalls — caught. The hot metal smell of the kebab griddles — caught. Old men in skullcaps playing carrom in a doorway — caught. Women in iridescent dupattas, colours that don't exist anywhere else in the city, the green-purple of a beetle's back — caught, caught.

Aditya walks beside her carrying her bag. He's learned not to talk while she's drawing. He's learned the geometry she needs: slightly behind, slightly left, in her peripheral vision at all times so that every reset finds him already accounted for, already part of the scene, never a sudden stranger at her shoulder. Nobody taught him this. He watched her flinch once, three weeks ago, when he came up on her right, and he never came up on the right again.

He has become fluent in a language with no words.

Her new Post-its for the trip, in Amma's hand: YOU ARE IN CHARMINAR. THIS IS OLD CITY. THE MAN BESIDE YOU IS ADITYA. HE IS SAFE. YOU HAVE BEEN HERE BEFORE. YOUR TABLET HAS THE ROUTE HOME.

She stops at the base of the monument and looks up. Four minarets, four hundred years, pigeons rising off the stone in a grey applause.

"Have I been here before?"

"Many times, according to your Post-it."

"Does it always look like this?"

"I don't know," Aditya says. "I only started seeing it through your eyes a few weeks ago."

She looks at him — a fresh assessment, the trial convened again, evidence weighed.

"You feel familiar," she says.

His breath catches. Three weeks of mornings, and she has never said anything like it. The notes can carry facts forward. Nothing is supposed to carry *this* forward — and yet here it is, surfacing in her like a coin shaken loose from a wreck.

"Is that good?" he manages.

"That's rare," she says.

They sit on the steps of a chai stall as the afternoon call to prayer unspools from the Mecca Masjid, the sound lying down over the bazaar like a hand laid on a shoulder. She drinks her chai with both hands around the glass. He waits. He has learned that her questions arrive on their own schedule, sent up from somewhere below the loop.

"I have a question," she says.

"Ask."

"Why are you here?"

"At Charminar?"

"With me. In general. If I forget you — if every day is new — what do you get?"

He takes his time. The prayer winds down. The pigeons resettle.

"Every morning I watch you read my Post-it," he says. "And every morning you make the same choice — you accept that I'm safe, and you let me walk beside you."

"I choose you every morning?"

"You choose me every morning."

She looks out at the bazaar, at the crowd doing its slow permanent dance around the stalls.

"That sounds like a very good kind of morning," she says.

"It's the best part of my day," he says, quietly.

She turns a fresh page and draws him — quick strokes, seven seconds, done. Then she examines the result with her usual collegial interest in her own work, and goes still.

"I draw people I like with their hands visible," she says.

He looks. In her rendering his hands are open. Reaching, slightly — towards the viewer, towards the artist, towards whatever she saw.

"I drew you with open hands," she says.

"What does that mean?"

"I'm not sure," she says. "But it feels true."

---

"You're falling," Meera said the next morning, setting a coffee on his desk and herself on the corner of it.

"I know."

"Fast."

"Meera—"

"I'm not telling you not to. I'm telling you to go slow enough that she can actually — in whatever way is possible for her — fall with you."

"She chose the star. I didn't draw it for her."

"I know." Meera looked at him over her cup. "Has she said anything?"

"She said I feel familiar."

Meera set the cup down with the deliberateness of a woman placing a full stop.

"Aditya. For Priya — 'familiar' isn't small. Familiar is extraordinary."

"I know," he said. He had been sitting with it all night, turning it over, holding it to the light. "I know."


# PART TWO — THE WEIGHT OF REMEMBERING

## Chapter Thirteen — The Star Kept

The sky bled pink over the Buddha statue, and Aditya came up the boardwalk steps with two paper cups of Irani chai and stopped, because Priya was doing something he had never seen her do.

She stood at the railing with the wrist notebook open and her pen out, the morning entry half-made — and she had paused. Her finger was tracing a small star drawn in the corner of the page. Faint. Yesterday's. She didn't know whose hand had made it; the pen hovered over it the way you hover over a word in a language you used to speak.

She didn't erase it.

He watched her not erase it, and something in his chest unlocked — a door going off its latch, light coming under.

"Subah ki namaskar," he said.

She looked up. The spine straightened — the stranger-alert posture he knew by now, the body's perimeter check. Then her eyes dropped to the chai in his hands, and travelled to his face, and back to the chai.

"You know I'll ask who you are in about seven seconds."

"I know."

He handed her the cup anyway.

"And that doesn't bother you?"

"It bothers me that it doesn't bother me," he said.

She wrapped both hands around the cup. The heat travelled up through her palms, and her shoulders made their small settling drop, and she looked down at the page again.

"You drew the star."

His eyes flicked to the corner of her notebook. "You kept it."

They drank in silence. The Buddha watched from the water, committed to neither of them.

---

At the studio, Meera caught him doing it again — small circles of the pen on the edge of a proof sheet, the same seven-pointed star, over and over, a man writing one sentence in shorthand.

"You're doing it again."

He looked down at the page. Didn't stop. "She kept the star."

"Aditya."

"She didn't erase it. She *traced* it."

Meera set down her coffee with the air of a project manager opening a new file. "Okay. So what's the system?"

He turned from the desk. His knee bounced once and stopped — he'd been thinking about this for three nights.

"Every morning I introduce myself the same way. Same order. Name, profession, chai preference."

"Chai preference?"

"She remembers facts better than faces. If I'm consistent — if I'm exactly the same every morning — I become a pattern."

"She can't store patterns."

"Her body can," Aditya said.

Meera went quiet.

"She already leans towards the railing when I come up the steps," he said. "Before she looks up. Before she knows why."

A long beat. Outside the glass, Hitech City performed its perpetual construction of itself.

"That's either very beautiful or very sad," Meera said.

"I think it might be both."

---

The mornings stacked.

Morning one: he arrived; she startled; he said "Aditya Sharma, graphic designer, I drink my chai without sugar"; she measured him against the note at her wrist and relaxed. The star sat in its corner.

Morning two: less startle. Her shoulders dropped a beat before she looked up. Same introduction — and she repeated it back to him, "Aditya. Designer. No sugar," checking the inventory, and he blinked because she had never done that before.

Morning three: she was already holding out a second cup of chai when he reached the railing. She didn't know why she'd asked Raju Bhaiya for two. His throat tightened around nothing.

Morning four: rain, and one shared newspaper held overhead, and Priya laughing — sudden, unguarded, head back — because the sports section soaked through first and the cricket scores ran down his face in grey ink.

Morning five: she drew a star in *his* sketchbook. Unprompted. He looked at it for a long time after she'd gone back to the lake, and then he closed the book very carefully, the way you close a door on a sleeping child.

## Chapter Fourteen — Golconda

They climb the stone steps of Golconda on a Saturday, and the city falls away below them with every flight — Hyderabad's glass towers visible through sixteenth-century archways, old and new stitched together at the horizon's hem.

Priya's wrist notebook is in her hand. Every few steps she checks it: ADITYA. FRIEND. SAFE. The word SAFE is underlined twice.

"Who wrote this note?" she asks.

"You did. This morning."

She studies the handwriting. Hers, unmistakably — the tail on the E, the pressure of the downstrokes.

"I trusted myself enough to write 'safe' in capital letters," she says. "That means something."

"What does it mean?"

She looks out at the city. Her fingers tighten on the notebook.

"That I probably tested you. Before I trusted you. I always test people before I write 'safe.' I don't remember the test, but I remember the rule."

He lets that sit. The wind comes over the ramparts carrying dust and kite-string and someone's far-off radio.

"What was the test?" she asks.

"You asked me to leave once. When we first met. Just — get away from me. You were afraid, and the fear was bigger than you expected."

"And you left?"

"I left."

She looks at him sideways. "That's either very kind or very calculated."

"You said those exact words to me last Thursday also."

She barks a laugh — short, startled by herself, the laugh escaping before the loop can confiscate it.

At the top of the fort, the whole city lies below them like an argument between centuries. She stands at the edge wall with her palm flat on stone that has been holding people's palms for four hundred years.

"It's strange to think that for you, we have a history," she says. "Weeks of mornings. Things I said. Things I laughed at. But for me—" she opens her hand towards him, giving him the fact like a small object, "—every time is the first time."

"Does that bother you?"

Her chin lifts slightly. Not defensiveness. Something with better foundations than defensiveness.

"It used to. I spent two years being bothered by it. Now I think — I get to fall in love with this city every single morning. Nobody else gets that."

He looks at her, and his jaw does something complicated, the hinge of it working against a sentence that wants out.

"Nobody else gets to fall in love with the right person every single morning either," he says.

She processes that. Something in her chest shifts — a micro-adjustment, a compass finding north — and he watches it move through her face like weather.

"That's either very beautiful or very—"

"—sad," he says. "I know."

They look at each other for a moment that the loop cannot touch because it is happening right now, and right now is the one jurisdiction where she has full citizenship.

She writes something in her notebook. He doesn't look. When they walk back down the steps, she is one step closer to him than when they came up.

---

In her room that evening, Priya reads through her wall — red urgent, green routine, blue joy — and adds a new blue note at the very centre, not the edges:

GOLCONDA. THE VIEW. THE WAY HE SAID "EVERY MORNING." KEEP THIS.

Lakshmi appears in the doorway and sees where the note has been pinned, and her expression softens the way stone doesn't and mothers do.

"You had a good day."

"I must have."

"The blue ones in the centre are always the good days."

Priya turns from the wall. Her thumbnail finds her collarbone and runs along it once — grounding, the body checking its own coordinates.

"Amma. Do you think it's possible to love something you can't hold?"

Lakshmi crosses the room and sits on the edge of the bed, and takes a moment, because the question deserves a moment, and because her daughter is the only person alive who can ask it with full authority.

"I think love doesn't need to be held," she says. "It just needs to keep arriving."

A long beat.

"That's what I said to him," Priya says. "On the boardwalk. I think. The note says I was happy afterward."

"Then it was the right thing to say."

## Chapter Fifteen — The Hand Knows

Shilparamam on a Sunday is a city of hands. Looms going in the weaver's row, the slap and turn of clay at the potter's wheel, brass workers raising a racket like industrious rain. Priya moves through the craft village with her small digital camera working — photographs don't reset; she has built her archive in machines — and Aditya carries the cloth bag two steps behind and to the left.

She stops at a kalamkari stall.

A woman in her sixties sits cross-legged before a stretched cotton panel, painting with a bamboo pen, building an impossible thing: a tree of life, branch over branch, bird inside flower inside vine, the pattern self-similar all the way down. Priya leans in. Her eyes track the brushstroke the way they track kingfishers.

"How do you remember the pattern?" she asks. "It's so complex."

The painter looks up. She takes in the wrist notebook, the tablet, the young woman's wide-open face, and answers the question as asked.

"I don't remember it. My hand remembers it. I just let it move."

Priya straightens slowly, the way you straighten when something lands in you with weight.

"My hand remembers things too," she says. "I write the same way every morning. Same letters. Same pressure."

The painter nods. This is ordinary truth to her, the first lesson of her first teacher, fifty years old in her body.

"The hand is older than the mind," she says, and goes back to the tree. "It knows things the mind forgets."

Aditya stands two steps behind, watching Priya's face as something opens in it — a door, light under it.

"That's why I can still draw my mother's face," Priya says, "even when I can't remember what she looks like right now."

She says it to the painter. She has never said it to him. His throat moves, and he looks away at a rack of block-printed cloth and pretends, badly, to evaluate it.

Afterwards they sit under the trees, and she eats mango pickle out of a banana-leaf twist, and he sketches.

"Can I ask you something?" he says, not looking up.

"You're going to anyway."

"What's your most important Post-it? Right now. The one you'd read first if everything went wrong."

She doesn't hesitate. "'You are loved. The evidence is in the next room.'"

A beat. His pencil stops.

"That's — what does that mean?"

"My mother left it. When I was first diagnosed. She said I should have one fact I couldn't argue with." A pause. "The evidence is her. She's always in the next room."

He looks up from the sketchpad. On the page: her face — the line of her jaw, the way her eyes go soft when she processes something true. He closes the pad before she can see.

"You have a good mother."

"I have the best mother." A beat. "I know that even though I forget. It just keeps being true before I can forget it."

---

Alone in the studio that night, with Hitech City humming blue through the windows, Aditya pinned a photo above his desk — Priya at the boardwalk, taken from a distance, her face turned to the lake, unaware. Below it he stuck a single yellow Post-it of his own:

*She chose the star. She keeps the star. She draws the star back without knowing why. The body remembers.*

He sat back. His hand drifted up and found his collarbone and pressed, once.

Her gesture. He had caught it like a tune. He did it now without noticing — the body taking dictation from the body, in its old language, below the floor of thought.

## Interlude — The Boardwalk's Keeper

Raju Bhaiya had been pushing the cart along that stretch of Tank Bund for nineteen years, and in nineteen years a man learns that a boardwalk is not a place. It is a population.

There were the joggers, who came in generations, each generation discovering the dawn as if they had invented it. There were the old men with newspapers, who held parliament on the benches and resolved nothing daily. There were the couples in the first secrecy of love, who thought the 6 AM lake belonged to them alone and were charmed to discover it had a chai cart, and the couples in the last formality of marriage, who walked a metre apart and bought one cup between them. There were the broken-hearted, who stood too close to the railing, and whom Raju Bhaiya had learned to approach with a free chai and a complaint about the weather, because a man holding hot chai and discussing the monsoon has rejoined the world whether he meant to or not.

And then, a year and some months ago, there was the girl.

He had known something was different by the third morning. Same time — not roughly the same time; the same time, to the minute, which no healthy young person manages. Same route. Same pause at the same section of railing. And the eyes: she looked at the lake the way pilgrims look at the river, every single morning, with a first-time face. On the fourth morning he offered her chai and she read his face like a document and consulted a yellow square of paper before answering. On the sixth morning a woman in her fifties came, introduced herself as the girl's mother, sat with him on the parapet for half an hour, and explained.

He had listened, stirring the pot, asking nothing.

"So she will never remember you," Lakshmi had finished. "You'll serve her chai every morning and every morning you'll be a stranger. I wanted you to know what you were taking on. Some people find it disturbing."

Raju Bhaiya had considered the foam on the chai for a while.

"Madam," he had said at last, "I have customers who have come every day for fifteen years and have never once seen me. I am the hand that gives the glass. She—" he nodded down the boardwalk, to where Priya stood drawing the lake, "—sees me new every morning. Every morning she decides again that I am worth trusting. Tell me, who respects me more?"

Lakshmi had paid for her chai, left a number, and walked back along the boardwalk, and Raju Bhaiya had watched her stop, out of her daughter's sightline, and stand a moment with her hand over her mouth. Then she had assembled herself and gone on. He had seen that too, in nineteen years — the assembling. Mothers were the great engineers.

From then on he had kept the watch without being asked. He had her mother's number and had used it twice: once for a fainting spell in the June heat, once for a man — there was always, eventually, a man of the other kind — who had noticed the girl's resets and begun arriving with his curiosity arranged unpleasantly. Raju Bhaiya had not called the number for that one, in the end. He had simply repositioned the cart to the man's favourite vantage and stood there, twenty minutes a morning, polishing glasses and looking at him, until the man found a different lake.

Then the designer came, and Raju Bhaiya ran the assessment all guardians run: the distance the boy kept, the hands always visible, the leaving when she said leave. He watched him return morning after morning to be forgotten, and pass the girl's test without knowing it was running, and learn the geometry nobody taught.

Nineteen years of the boardwalk had given Raju Bhaiya a private taxonomy of love. Most of what visited the railing was weather — beautiful, seasonal, gone. Once in a long while, something else came: the kind that builds. He had no word for it in any of his three languages, but he knew the signs the way he knew tomorrow's rain in his knee, and the morning he watched the boy step over the loose stone for the first time without looking down — the body already keeping the route, the way her body kept hers — he went back to his cart and, for reasons he chose not to examine, made the chai that day with one extra cardamom each.

The day they finally stood at the railing with her head on the boy's shoulder, he said one word to the lake, in Telugu, very quietly. The lake, which heard everything on that boardwalk and kept all of it, made no reply.

"There it is."

## Chapter Sixteen — Footsteps

The morning of the midpoint arrived looking like any other morning, which is how midpoints travel.

Priya stood at the railing with no notebook open. Just looking. The light was fuller — first warmth of summer in it — and the lake held the Buddha up to the morning like an offering.

Aditya came up the steps.

"I know you're there," she said, without turning.

He stopped dead. "I haven't said anything."

"Your footsteps. Third step has a loose stone. You always step over it slightly to the left and then your weight shifts forward. I hear it."

He stared at her back. The chai went slightly off-level in his hands.

"You've never told me that."

"I've never known I knew it before right now."

She turned to face him. Her eyes were steady — not confused, not frightened. Something else was happening in her face, something he had no word for then and would spend months finding words for afterwards: recognition without memory. The body arriving before the mind, the way thunder's light arrives before its sound.

"You feel familiar," she said.

She had said it before, at Charminar, weeks ago. The words were the same. The weight was new.

"I know," he said.

"Not just — stranger-familiar. Not 'I've met you before' familiar. Something else." A pause; she searched for it with the precision she brought to everything. "Like — I've been safe before. And the shape of safe is the same shape as you."

His right hand closed around nothing and opened again.

"Priya—"

"Don't explain it." Her voice stayed soft. "I don't want the explanation. I just want to — sit in it for a moment. Before the moment goes."

They stood at the railing. The lake spread out below; the Buddha rose from the water, permanent, patient, holding its one expression through every reset the world had ever thrown at it.

Seven seconds.

She forgot.

But when she turned to introduce herself to the stranger beside her, her hand was already reaching for his — and she stopped, and looked at her own hand, surprised, hanging there in the air between them like a word arrived from a language she didn't know she spoke. Then she looked up at him, and her face said what her mouth then confirmed:

"I'm Priya. And you already know me, don't you."

"Yes."

"Good."

Her hand stayed out. He took it. She looked at their joined hands the way you look at a sentence you understand in a script you cannot read, and her fingers settled between his, finding their places like people taking their usual seats.

## Chapter Seventeen — The Blue Wall

Kiran came home that evening with his usual inventory glance — sister, mother, exits — and found Priya at the dining table with a new blue Post-it riding the cover of her wrist notebook, carried against her chest the way you carry a book you are in the middle of.

He couldn't read it from here. He waited.

"Subah kaisa tha?" he asked.

"Good. I think very good."

She set the notebook on the table, face down. Her fingers tapped it once — a private gesture, a knock on a door only she could hear through.

"That man," Kiran said. "The designer. Aditya. He comes every morning."

"I know."

"What do you know about him?"

She picked up the notebook and opened it to the inside cover, where the permanent reference entries lived, and read aloud in the flat voice of a clerk producing a record:

"Aditya Sharma. Twenty-five. Graphic designer at Kalakar Studio, Hitech City. Drinks chai without sugar. Comes every morning. Has never once tried to use my condition against me. Tested: passed."

Kiran's face shifted at the jaw. He hadn't known about the test. He hadn't known she ran tests.

"You have a system."

"I've always had a system. You just don't ask to see it."

A long beat. The ceiling fan turned through it.

"I'm asking now," Kiran said.

She took him to her room.

He stood in the doorway first — he hadn't been inside in months, a restraint he billed to himself as respect and which his curling fingers exposed as fear — and then stepped in, slowly, while his sister gestured at the wall with the practised ease of a curator.

"Red is urgent — medical, safety, things I can't miss. Green is routine — what to eat, where my keys are, which bus goes where. Blue is—" she paused, choosing, "—joy. Things I want to carry even when I can't carry them."

Hundreds of notes. Some faded, some fresh. Lives stacked on lives, the archaeology of a self that rebuilt itself daily and kept the blueprints.

His eyes found the blue cluster at the centre.

GOLCONDA. THE VIEW. HE SAID "EVERY MORNING."

THE KALAMKARI PAINTER. THE HAND KNOWS.

HE TOOK MY HAND. I DIDN'T KNOW WHY I REACHED. BUT IT FELT LIKE COMING HOME.

His throat locked. He stood in front of his sister's secret autobiography — the one her brain refused to write and her soul had insisted on — and read what she had chosen to keep.

"He's in all the blue ones," he said.

"Most of them. Lately."

Kiran turned from the wall and looked at his sister — really looked, the way brothers forget to, the way guilt teaches you to look sideways. He saw her. Not the condition. Not the nightmare that had begun three years ago in a hospital corridor that still appeared in his dreams with its specific light. Her: the woman who ran tests before she gave trust. Who had built a colour-coded system for joy.

"I went to his studio," he said.

Her eyes sharpened.

"Last month. I wanted to — I don't know what I wanted. I told him to be careful with you."

"And?"

"He said: 'I know.'"

A beat.

"That's what he always says," Priya said.

"I know." A pause; the next sentence cost him, and he paid. "I think it's actually true when he says it."

She searched his face. Found the payment receipt there.

"I'm not apologising for watching out for you," he said.

"I'm not asking you to."

"But I think—" he stopped, started again. "I think you picked well. Even without remembering that you picked."

Her face did something then that she would not let become crying — she had made a rule about crying in front of Kiran, years ago, written on no wall but kept anyway — but her eyes went bright for one full second, the light getting in under the door of the rule.

"Go eat your dinner, Bhaiya."

He went. At the doorway he paused.

"The blue ones look good on you," he said. "The wall."

Then he was gone. She stood in the centre of her colour-coded life, looking at the blue concentrated at the heart of it like a galaxy seen edge-on.

She added one more note:

KIRAN SAID I PICKED WELL. KEEP THIS.

## Chapter Eighteen — Blue Bangles

Laad Bazaar at midday is a riot with a four-hundred-year lease. Glass bangles stacked floor to ceiling in walls of fire, the air thick with attar and hot metal, vendors calling over each other in Urdu and Telugu, the lane itself narrow enough that commerce becomes intimacy whether anyone wants it or not.

Priya moves through it with her camera, photographing everything. Aditya follows, two steps behind and to the left, carrying a cloth bag — because she likes to buy things, and forgets she's bought them, and buys them again, and he has decided that the universe can afford her this.

She stops at a stall and lifts a set of blue glass bangles to the light.

"Blue," she says. "Like my notes."

"You have three sets of blue bangles at home."

"Do they look like these?"

"Exactly like those."

She sets them back. Picks them up again.

"But these ones I chose today," she says. "The ones at home — some other version of me chose those. These are mine."

He watches her hold them. He doesn't argue. There is no argument; she has just stated her condition's entire jurisprudence in two sentences, and she is right.

She buys them. He adds them to the bag, alongside the other things she doesn't know she's already bought this afternoon: one packet of rose-petal tea, one small brass turtle, a bookmark printed with a Ghalib couplet. She'll find them all tonight and not know they're from today. She'll think someone gave them to her.

He finds he's fine with that. He finds, in fact, that he envies whoever she'll imagine the giver to be.

They end up at an old Irani café off the bazaar lane — cracked tile, slow ceiling fan, the particular silence of a place that has been exactly itself for seventy years. Osmania biscuits on a chipped plate. Chai so dark it's almost red.

"Tell me something about yourself," she says. "Something I probably don't ask for."

"What do you usually ask?"

"Name. Profession. How we met. The facts." She dips a biscuit and eats it. "Tell me something that's not a fact."

He thinks. The fan turns.

"I draw the same thing every time I'm scared," he says. "A door. Just — a door. With light coming under it. I don't know what's on the other side. But the light means someone's home."

She holds her glass with both hands, the heat against her palms.

"That's—" she pauses. "I do something like that. I write the same sentence when I don't know what's happening: 'You are here. This moment is real.'"

"Does it help?"

"Every time. For about seven seconds."

They look at each other across the chipped plate. Somewhere behind the counter, a radio is playing something from the eighties at the volume of a memory.

"Your door with the light under it," she says. "Is anyone specific on the other side?"

He holds her gaze. "Getting more specific every morning."

Her thumb finds the new blue bangles on her wrist and runs along them once — glass, cool, smooth. She looks down at them as if discovering them, which she is. Looks back up at him.

"I feel like I've been here before. At this table."

"You have. Twice."

"Did I order the same thing?"

"You dipped the biscuit exactly the same way. Twice, then eat."

She looks at the biscuit in her hand. Dips it — twice. Eats it.

"Huh," she says. And then, very quietly, half to the table: "I'm still me. Even when I don't know I'm me."

It hits him differently than anything else she has ever said. He carries it out of the café and through the bazaar and home and into the next morning, where, as it turns out, he will need it.

---

Three months since the collision, and June lay on Hyderabad like a hand. But at the lake at six there was still a mercy in the breeze, and Priya's wrist notebook was fat now — pages added, folded, layered, her own archaeology — and Aditya came up the steps with a newspaper-wrapped parcel under one arm.

The loose stone. Step left, weight forward.

She turned before he spoke. Not the startled face. The other one — the face of someone who doesn't know why they're glad, but is.

"Good morning."

"Good morning."

"The note says you're bringing something today," she said, checking her cover page.

He held up the parcel. "Tangedu flowers. From the Necklace Road vendors. You said last week you loved the yellow."

She unwrapped it. The flowers spilled their colour into her hands — bright, wild, the gold the state had chosen for itself — and her face did something involuntary, a kind of shock at her own reaction, the body answering before any note could be consulted.

"I love these."

"I know."

"How did you—" She stopped. Read the notebook. "I told you. Of course."

She held the flowers, and her chin dropped once — a small, private bow to the fact of being known.

"What else do I love? That I've told you?"

He sat down beside her on the bench and thought, taking the inventory honestly, because she would know — somehow, below memory, she always knew — if he padded it.

"Tangedu flowers. The sound of rain on the lake before you can see the rain coming. Old Telugu film songs — the ones from the eighties your mother sang to you. The smell of wet stone. Blue things. Seven-pointed stars, for some reason."

She looked at the star in her notebook's corner.

"You drew the first one."

"You've been drawing them since. You don't know why."

"Maybe I do know why." She looked at him. "Maybe I know and can't say it in seven seconds, so I write it in shapes instead."

The sun broke over the Buddha. Gold poured itself across the water with the recklessness of something that gets to do this every day and never has to pay.

"Aditya."

"Yes."

"I think I love you."

He went very still. The lake went on burning.

"I know I'll forget I said that," she said. "And I know you know that. And I don't know how long I've been — arriving at this. But in this moment, I want you to hear it."

His hand found the edge of the railing. White-knuckled. He did not look away from her, because looking away from this would be the one unforgivable economy.

"I hear you," he said.

"Write it in my notebook. So I can find it."

He took her pen. He opened the inside cover, beneath her clerk's entry about him, and wrote four words in blue ink, in his own handwriting, distinct from hers, slanted where hers stood upright:

YOU SAID YOU LOVED.

She read it. She didn't remember saying it — the moment had already gone wherever her moments went. But it was written in blue, and it was witnessed by her own system, so she knew it was true.

She held the flowers. The lake was on fire with morning.

## Chapter Nineteen — What Meera Saw

Meera had a theory about Aditya Sharma, developed over eleven months of shared deadlines, and the theory was this: he was a man with an enormous talent for attention and no idea what it was for.

She had watched him spend it like a rich man's son — on gradients, on bangle stalls, on the way light entered glass, on whichever junior designer needed rescuing the week he noticed them, on women who mistook the attention for intention and left, sooner or later, with the specific bruise of having been *seen* and then *filed*. He wasn't cruel. Cruelty would have required him to know he was doing it. He was a lens that had never found its subject, swinging around the city, focusing brilliantly on everything for a week at a time.

Then he came back from the lake.

She marked the change the way you mark weather — not in any single thing, but in the totals. He started leaving the studio at five. He started arriving at eight with his shirt wrinkled from sitting on stone. He stopped narrating himself; the charm, which had always run like a generator under everything, went quiet, and what was left was — she had to look twice to be sure — concentration. The lens had stopped swinging.

"You know what's strange," she told him once, across the proof table, in the weeks before the party broke everything open. "I used to think you'd be a great designer if you ever learned to finish. Now you finish everything."

"Deadlines," he said.

"It's not deadlines." She tapped the proof. "It's her. You've started doing everything like it counts the first time. Like there's no revision round."

He looked up at that, and she saw the recognition land.

"There's no revision round," he said, slow with the weight of it, "with her. Whatever I am in the seven seconds, that's the whole of what I am. She can't average me out over time. There's no — accumulated goodwill. Every morning I'm exactly as good as I am that morning."

"Terrifying," Meera said.

"It's the opposite." He went back to the proof, and made one mark on it, decisive. "It's the only relief I've ever felt. You spend your whole life building a reputation — this store of credit, so people will forgive the day you're not at your best. She can't hold the store. So I stopped building it. I just have to actually *be* — every time." He made another mark. "Turns out it's simpler. Not easier. Simpler."

Meera, who would repeat this conversation to no one but think about it for years — who would, in fact, end two of her own relationships by its standard — went back to her desk and wrote nothing down, because she was not a Post-it person.

But she kept it.

## Chapter Twenty — The Party

Six weeks later, a terrace in Jubilee Hills, and the city spread below in its lit grid, and Aditya stood at the railing with a glass in his hand that was not his first and not his second.

The party belonged to Rohan — a colleague from his old studio, twenty-eight, loud, careless in the way money makes people careless — and the crowd was Hyderabad's twenty-five-to-thirty-five layer: design people, tech people, expensive lighting, music split between Telugu pop and imported bass. Aditya had come because Rohan had insisted and because Meera had said, not unkindly, that he had become a man with exactly one subject.

He had been at the railing for forty minutes.

"Bhai." Rohan materialised with two fresh drinks. "What's happening with you?"

"Nothing. I'm tired."

"You're not tired. You're — I don't know. You're somewhere else."

"I'm here. I'm literally here."

"You're thinking about that girl. The one you're always going on about."

Aditya didn't answer, which was an answer.

"Yaar, I'm saying this as a friend — is it not exhausting? Going there every morning and she doesn't even—"

"Stop."

"I'm just saying. It must be heavy. Carrying all that for someone who can't—"

"She carries more than either of us," Aditya said, sharp now. "Every single day."

Rohan backed off and refilled and drifted. The music rose. Aditya drank, and the city below shimmered, and the thought he never said out loud surfaced in the dark water of three drinks: *she is not there at night.* The gap between what she gave him in seven seconds and what he carried for twenty-four hours. He had been awake since five. The mornings were beautiful, and they cost him, and nobody — not Meera, not Kiran, not Raju Bhaiya with his three-second stares — had ever once asked what they cost him, because the ledger only ever ran one direction, and he had agreed to that, and most days the agreement felt like grace and tonight it felt like weight.

He drank more.

Divya found him after eleven. Twenty-six, sharp, a mutual friend; they had history — nothing serious, but the kind of warm-before-cold that makes parties complicated.

"You look like you're solving a math problem."

"I'm fine."

"Who is she?"

He looked at Divya. He shouldn't be talking. He knew he shouldn't be talking, the way you know it three drinks in — abstractly, like weather news from another city.

"Someone I met on the boardwalk. Three months ago."

"The amnesia girl."

His jaw locked. "Her name is Priya."

"Okay. Priya. Is it serious?"

"Yes."

"How does that even work? She forgets everything—"

"Not everything."

"Aditya—"

"She's not—" too loud now; heads half-turned; he didn't notice. "It's not some — tragedy. She's the most present person I've ever known."

Rohan had drifted back with the gravitational obedience of people to other people's discomfort. Others stood nearby. The music ran under everything.

"Present because she has no choice, no?" Rohan said.

"That's not—"

"I'm being real with you, man. Is it not — I mean, convenient? For you?" Rohan's glass made a small lazy circle that took in the whole arrangement. "She's easy to love because she can never remember anything bad you do."

The words landed.

Aditya went silent.

And the silence was wrong. Everyone standing there felt it go wrong — a beat, two beats, three, the music suddenly very loud against it. It was the silence of a man who has been handed a sentence and is checking it against something inside himself, and the check is taking too long, and the taking-too-long is itself the answer.

"I'm just saying—" Rohan began.

"I heard you," Aditya said.

But he didn't deny it.

Three feet away, a twenty-three-year-old named Ananya lowered her phone. She had been filming the terrace for her story — the lights, the crowd, thirty seconds of ambience — and the camera had been pointed their way for the whole exchange. She didn't realise what she'd caught.

She posted it.

---

Kiran's phone lit up at 1 AM with a message from a mutual acquaintance — no text, just a link.

He watched the story with the volume low so as not to wake the flat. The terrace. The city lights. Rohan's voice, tinny but clear: *she's easy to love because she can never remember anything bad you do.*

And Aditya. Silent. Not denying.

Kiran watched it four times. His thumb went white on the phone's edge.

Down the corridor, his sister slept inside her seven seconds, her wall glowing faintly blue in the streetlight, the note at its centre saying HE TOOK MY HAND. IT FELT LIKE COMING HOME.

## Chapter Twenty-One — The Empty Steps

Priya stands at the railing, and the steps stay empty.

She checks her notebook. Inside cover: ADITYA. FRIEND. SAFE. COMES EVERY MORNING. She waits. The lake performs its slow gold ignition for an audience of one. Raju Bhaiya brings her chai and stands a moment longer than usual, and says nothing, because there is nothing in his cart for this.

He doesn't come.

After twenty minutes she writes a note in red — red, the colour of things that cannot be missed — and folds it into the notebook: ADITYA DIDN'T COME. CHECK TOMORROW.

She photographs the lake and leaves.

---

The second morning, overcast, pre-monsoon grey. The red note from yesterday. She adds below it: HE DIDN'T COME YESTERDAY EITHER.

She waits. The steps stay empty.

She stands at the railing a long time, and her hand finds the iron and her thumb moves along it — the same path it traced months ago, when she stood looking at a small drawn star, not knowing whose hand had made it. The body holds its positions long after the mind has lost the war they were dug for.

Her body knows something is wrong. She does not know what it is. This is the particular cruelty her condition saves for special occasions: grief without an object, an ache with no address, the space where something was — and the space is real, the space is the realest thing on the boardwalk this morning, and she cannot name what used to fill it.

She takes out her camera and photographs the empty steps.

She doesn't know why she does this. But she does it.

---

That evening she pins the photograph to her wall. Not in red. Not in green. She takes a blue Post-it — blue, the colour of joy, and some future scholar of her wall would stop here, puzzled, at the blue note beside the photograph of nothing — and writes:

SOMETHING MISSING. DON'T KNOW WHAT. BUT THE SPACE WHERE IT WAS IS REAL.

Lakshmi watches from the doorway, holding the doorframe.

"Priya—"

"He didn't come again."

"I know, kanna."

"I don't know why I feel it. I can't remember what we were, exactly. But I know the morning is — less."

Her voice doesn't break. She has practice at this — at holding grief she can't identify, at mourning what she can't name. Of everyone alive, she is the best in the world at this, and it is a championship nobody would enter willingly.

"Do you want me to—"

"No. If he needs to be away, that's—" she checks the inside cover, the entry, the word underlined twice. "The note says he's safe. It says SAFE. So he's not hurt. He just — isn't here."

She turns back to the wall and straightens the photograph of the empty steps by a degree.

"I'm going to keep the photo," she says. "So I remember that I noticed. Even when I can't remember what filled the space."

## Chapter Twenty-Two — Stay Away From Her

Aditya came to Kiran's office because he didn't know what else to do with himself, and Kiran let him sit down because he had decided, somewhere in the four viewings of the video, to hear before he judged. It was the largest concession available to him, and it had taken all night to manufacture.

"I watched the video four times," Kiran said.

"I know what it looked like."

"Tell me what it was."

Aditya looked at the surface of the drafting table — clean, ordered, like its owner.

"I was drunk. Someone I don't like said something cruel about Priya. And I — I didn't defend her fast enough. Because there was a part of what he said that I was still — working through. In myself."

"'She's easy to love because she can never remember anything bad.'"

"Yes."

"And?"

Aditya raised his eyes and paid the full fare.

"And it's not why I love her. But I'd be lying if I said I haven't felt the mercy of it. The mercy of being forgiven before I've finished being bad."

A long silence. The office hummed its white-collar hum.

"That's honest," Kiran said.

"It's the worst honest thing I've ever said."

"My sister—" Kiran stopped, started again. "My sister built a system for herself. For trust. She doesn't give it easily. She tested you before she wrote 'safe.' You know she did that?"

"She told me."

"If she ever finds out — if someone tells her, or she sees something — the system doesn't protect her from that. From someone she trusted using her condition as comfort."

"I didn't use it—"

"You sat in it."

The silence that followed was the sentence, and both men knew it.

"Stay away from her," Kiran said.

Aditya's hands closed on the arms of the chair. "Kiran—"

"I'm not asking."

Kiran turned back to his drafting table, and his voice, when it came again, was the worst thing in the room because it wasn't cruel:

"She'll stop waiting after a week. She'll adjust the notes. She'll make a new routine. She's better at this than either of us."

Aditya stood. The chair scraped. He left.

---

He went to the lake. Midday — no one there at that hour, the glare flattening the water to white. He stood where she stands every morning, his hands on the metal her hands know, and looked at the city from her railing.

And he understood, for the first time, standing in her exact coordinates: this is what she has. This lake. This view. This stone under her feet — the same stone, every morning, and she is grateful for it every single time, grateful with her whole undivided self, because her self is never divided between today and yesterday, because there is no yesterday to divide it with.

He had been walking away from the lake for a week, and he carried everything — the party, the silence, the video, the verdict.

She could not walk away from anything. And she had built, out of that, a life with joy at its centre, filed in blue.

That was not mercy for him. That had never been mercy for him.

That was her grace, and he had been warming his hands at it.

He stood at the railing a long time.

---

"He told me to stay away," he said, on Meera's couch, the tea going cold in front of him.

"What do you want to do?"

"I want to go back. Every morning. And I want to deserve it."

"Those are two different timelines," Meera said.

"I know."

He picked up the tea. Set it down without drinking.

"What if I go back and I'm exactly who I've always been with her — fully present, nothing held back — but I also carry the knowledge that I failed her in a moment she'll never know about?"

"That's not a question. That's a confession."

"Meera."

"You want permission to go back without telling her. You know she can't hold the information — you tell her, seven seconds, it's gone — so confessing isn't really a confession. It's theatre."

He looked at her. She had never once made anything easier for him, which was why he was on her couch and not on anyone else's.

"Then what do I do?"

"You earn Kiran."

He looked up.

"He's the one who has the full memory," Meera said. "He's the one who carries what she can't. If you earn his trust back, you earn the right to stand next to her again. That's the only version of this that's clean."

He sat with it. The tea steamed itself out.

"And if he doesn't give me that?"

"Then you've lost her." Meera let it stand in the room at its full height. "That's the crisis, Aditya. This is it. Right now. This is what your story costs you."

## Chapter Twenty-Three — Three Weeks

The three weeks Aditya stayed away from the lake were the longest of his life, and he did them almost entirely at his desk, because the desk was the only place his hands had something to hold.

He worked. That was the visible part. He cleared the backlog and then the backlog under the backlog; he shipped a brand system in nine days that should have taken twenty; Meera began quietly removing things from his queue not because he was failing but because watching the speed had begun to frighten her. The work was clean and cold and finished, and clients praised it, and none of it had any light in it, and he knew it, and he shipped it anyway, because shipping it was the alternative to six o'clock.

Six o'clock was the problem. Six o'clock arrived every morning whether or not a man had decided to be done with mornings, and at six o'clock his body — trained by months of repetition, keeping faith with a route the way her body kept faith with hers — woke him without an alarm and pointed itself at the lake. He learnt to lie still through it. It was exactly as effective as lying still through hunger.

He drew doors. He noticed it in the second week — the proof-sheet margins filling up with them, door after door, and under every door the light, and he stopped labelling the light because he knew what it was at 6:47 in the morning and writing it down was a privilege he had revoked.

He did not watch the video again. He did not need to. He had discovered the difference between guilt and shame the way most people discover it — by owning both at once and feeling them pull in different directions. Guilt said: *you failed her in a moment; repair it.* Shame said: *the moment revealed you; there is nothing to repair, there is only what you are.* Guilt was useful; shame was a flat tyre. He spent the three weeks learning to tell them apart at speed, and on the days he managed it, he sat down and asked himself the only question that produced anything:

*What did the warmth actually consist of?*

Because Rohan had been wrong — he established this slowly, evidence item by evidence item, with the rigour of a man who no longer trusted his own advocacy. It was not that she couldn't remember anything bad he did. Kindness to her face cost nothing; the test had taught him that on day nine. What she couldn't do was *hold a grudge*, and what he had been warming himself at — he found it at last, in the third week, at two in the morning, and wrote it on a Post-it because by then her formats had colonised him completely — was this:

SHE CAN'T KEEP SCORE. SO AROUND HER, YOU STOP PLAYING THE GAME.
THE GAME WAS THE EXHAUSTING PART.
THE GAME WAS NEVER LOVE.

He looked at it a long time. Then he took a second Post-it and wrote the harder one, the one he would eventually say out loud to her in the rain, the one that had to exist on paper first before his mouth would agree to it:

YOU SAT IN THE WARMTH WITHOUT ASKING IF YOU DESERVED IT.
DESERVING IT IS A DAILY JOB.
GO BACK TO WORK.

He stuck them both inside his wallet, facing each other.

Then he opened his sketchbook to a fresh page, and drew her wall the way Lakshmi had described it to Meera — the colours, the clusters, the photograph of the empty steps with a blue note beside it — and folded the page, and slept four hours, and in the morning he went to see her brother.

## Chapter Twenty-Four — The Drawing

Three weeks of absence, seen from above, as if the city itself were keeping the log:

Priya at the railing, alone, each morning. Her adjustments were subtle. She had erased COMES EVERY MORNING from Aditya's entry — the system did not tolerate inaccuracy; inaccuracy was the one sin her world could not absorb — but she had not erased his name. The note that said YOU SAID YOU LOVED had migrated from the inside cover to a page in the middle: still there, no longer at the top.

She didn't know this. She didn't track the movement of her own notes with any continuity.

But the wall knew. The notebook knew. The wall was the autobiography her brain couldn't write, and like all honest autobiographies it recorded the retreats as faithfully as the victories.

---

Aditya sat on the steps across the street from Kiran's office building for an hour before he went in. He had no appointment and no argument. He had a sketchbook.

He drew Priya's wall — from memory, from imagination, from the one glimpse Lakshmi's descriptions had given Meera and Meera had given him. The blue notes clustered at the centre. The photograph of the empty steps. He drew it the way he drew when it mattered: slow, exact, nothing decorative.

He tore the page out. Folded it. Went inside.

Kiran looked up from the drafting table. "I said stay away."

Aditya set the folded drawing on the desk. Kiran didn't touch it.

"She put a photo of the empty steps on her wall," Aditya said.

Kiran's face stopped.

"She doesn't know why she took it. She just knows something is missing and the space is real. And she labelled it blue. The joy colour." A pause. "Because even the absence of someone she loves is something she wants to carry."

A long silence. The office AC ticked.

"How do you know this?"

"Your mother told Meera. Meera told me."

Kiran stood and went to the window, his back to the room, the city laid out in front of him — the distant spire of a mosque, the new glass of Hitech City, the two Hyderabads that had learned to hold each other.

"What do you want from me?"

"Tell me how to earn it back. Not with her — she doesn't remember the break. Tell me how to earn it back with you. Because you're the one who carries it. You're the one who holds what she can't. And if you don't trust me, I shouldn't be there."

The city went on outside the glass. Kiran's reflection in it held still.

"Why did you come to her in the first place?"

Aditya didn't hesitate.

"Because she was standing at the railing in the dark and she looked — not sad. She looked like she was deciding something enormous and doing it alone, and I thought: I want to know what she's deciding."

"And now?"

"Now I think I've spent three months learning what she already knows. How to be in one moment completely. And then the next one. And then the next one."

Kiran turned from the window.

"She's not your teacher."

"No. She's the person I love. Those aren't the same thing. I know they look the same from the outside, so I'm saying it to your face."

Kiran picked up the folded drawing. Held it without opening it, the way you hold an envelope you already know the contents of.

"The journalist," he said. "You know about that?"

Aditya's eyes sharpened.

"Someone from an online magazine. They heard about the video, did some digging. They want to write about her. The girl with seven-second memory. Inspirational content."

"That's—"

"I know what it is." Kiran set the drawing down, still folded. "She'll be at the lake tomorrow. 6:45. If someone shows up claiming to be a journalist, asking about her condition — she won't know to be afraid of them. She'll talk. Because she doesn't carry the knowledge of what happened before."

Aditya stood.

"I'm not asking you to go," Kiran said.

But he didn't tell him not to go either. And when the door had closed, Kiran sat back down and unfolded the drawing and looked at it for a long time — his sister's wall, rendered by the man he had banished, accurate down to the photograph of the empty steps, accurate down to things the man could not have seen and had somehow seen anyway.

He smoothed the fold flat with his thumb. He did not throw it away.

## Chapter Twenty-Five — Rain

Pre-monsoon, and the sky over the lake was the colour of bruised fruit — purple and orange at the horizon, the air carrying the loaded smell of rain that hasn't fallen yet.

Priya stood at the railing with her notebook open. She looked up at the sound of footsteps on the stairs — and the footsteps did something to her before her eyes had data: the loose stone, the step-over, slightly left, weight forward. Something in her face moved that was not recognition and was not nothing. Pre-recognition. The body's antenna catching the signal before the mind's receiver switched on.

Aditya stopped three feet from her.

"Priya. I'm Aditya Sharma. We know each other. Your notes will confirm it."

She looked at her notebook. Inside cover: ADITYA. FRIEND. SAFE.

"The note says 'safe.'"

"Yes."

"But you weren't here." Her finger found the red note, the folded one. "There's a red note that says you weren't here for a long time."

His jaw tightened. "I know. I'm sorry."

"You won't tell me why."

"Not right now. Later. There's—"

He saw the man then: thirty, lanyard, notebook, coming up the far set of steps with the over-rehearsed lope of someone trying very hard to look casual. Arnav. The journalist. His eyes were already on Priya with the calculated interest of a man pricing a story.

Aditya stepped sideways — just slightly, just enough. Put his body between Priya and the line of approach. The geometry he had learned for love turned out to work for defence without modification.

"Can we walk? Away from the main boardwalk."

Priya read his posture the way she read everything — fast, total, in the present tense.

"Why?"

"Because someone is going to come up those steps and ask you questions you don't want to answer about your condition."

One second of processing.

"Is this in my notes?"

"Not yet. But it's real."

Her thumb crossed the notebook cover once. She made the decision in the only place she could make decisions — here, now — and made it the way she made everything: cleanly.

"Okay."

They walked. Away from the main boardwalk, down the quieter path along the lake's back, where the tangedu trees stood in their pre-bloom green. Behind them, Arnav reached the railing where she always stood and found it empty.

As they walked, she wrote: JOURNALIST. AVOID BOARDWALK UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE. Red. Underlined.

"Why does someone want to write about me?"

"The video. From a party. Someone talked about you."

Her hand tightened on the notebook. "What did they say?"

He stopped walking. She stopped beside him. The storm assembled itself over the lake, the surface going restless, the first wind arriving like a rumour.

"They said something cruel. About your condition. About why you might be — easy to love."

She looked at him, head tilting. "Was it you? Who said it?"

His answer took two full breaths, and he gave her the truth in the only window she had:

"Someone else said it. I didn't say it. But I didn't stop it fast enough. And there was a part of it—" he forced himself through, "—a part of it that touched something I hadn't looked at yet. Something about how being loved by someone who can't keep score is a kind of gift. For me. And I've been sitting in the warmth of it without asking if I deserved to."

She held this. She had no memory of the morning he hadn't come, the three weeks, the video, the drawing on Kiran's desk. She had only these words, now, this man, this storm.

"And now?"

"Now I think the only version of this that's right is that you deserve to be loved by someone who is trying to be as good as you think they are."

The first rain came. Fat drops, scattered, and then the smell rose from the path — wet stone, the oldest perfume the city owned.

She lifted her face to it.

"I love this. The smell."

"I know. You've told me eight times."

She looked at him, rain on her face. "You kept track."

"Of everything."

She reached up. Her hand — wet now, the blue bangles bright with rain — found his jaw and turned his face toward her, an examiner's gesture, gentle and absolute.

"Read me my notes."

He opened her notebook and read from the inside cover, quietly, while the rain began in earnest around them:

"'Aditya Sharma. Twenty-five. Graphic designer. Drinks chai without sugar. Comes every morning. Has never used my condition against me. Tested: passed.'" He turned pages, going deeper, to the middle where the migrated note lived. "And this. Your handwriting. 'YOU SAID YOU LOVED.'"

She listened with her hand still on his face. Then she took the notebook from him and looked at the page — her words, and beside them his four words in blue, two hands on one page.

"I say it every morning?"

"Only once. But it's still true. The blue means you wanted to carry it."

She closed the notebook. The rain ran off the oilcloth cover in beads.

"Show me your door," she said.

He blinked.

"The drawing. The door with the light under it. You told me about it once."

He opened his sketchbook — he always carried it — and turned to the page he'd drawn alone in the studio, the night everything was still whole. The door. The warmth beneath it, the specific warm of early sun off water. The small letters in the corner: HUSSAIN SAGAR. 6:47 AM. And smaller: PRIYA.

She looked at it a long time, rain tapping the page's edge.

"I'm on the other side of your door."

"Yes."

"Then come home," she said.

---

He went back every morning after that, and the mornings rebuilt themselves, changed.

She offered him the half-eaten Osmania biscuit on the first morning, and he took it. She opened her umbrella over both of them on the second, unasked, and his shoulder found hers beneath it. On the third he brought two cups instead of one, and she held hers in both hands and looked up to find him already looking. On the fourth she found the blue note — YOU SAID YOU LOVED — and showed it to him, and he nodded, and she wrote underneath it, in her own hand: STILL TRUE.

His eyes closed once. Opened.

On the fifth morning, Kiran passed the boardwalk on his way to work and saw them at the railing. He didn't stop. But he didn't look away either, and his hands stayed in his pockets, and his jaw, by one degree, relaxed.

---

In Kiran's flat that evening there were two glasses of water on the table — both men careful tonight, nothing in the room but the words.

"The journalist spoke to Raju Bhaiya," Kiran said. "He told him nothing. But Arnav got enough from watching. He knows her routine. He knows about the notebook. The wall."

"He won't publish without her consent."

"He'll get her consent. He'll sit down with her and she'll answer his questions and she'll agree to the story and then she'll forget she agreed and it'll still run." Kiran's voice stayed flat the way a wire stays flat with current in it. "What version of consent is that?"

Aditya set his glass down and went to the window.

"We tell her explicitly. In the notes. Red."

"She already has it. Amma added it after the first time."

"Then he'll find another way. They always—"

"Aditya."

He turned. Kiran was looking at him with an expression that had been three weeks in the making, an expression with a cost sheet attached.

"She told me about the tangedu path. She wrote it in her journal — the long-form one, not the notebook. She writes in it on the evenings when she has a longer window." A pause. "She wrote: 'I don't know this person's full history with me. I don't know what came before. But every morning I find him already there — and the notes don't just say safe. They say the chai is already the right temperature. They say he learned the geometry. I think I chose well, even without remembering the choosing.'"

Aditya's eyes went to the window, the city lights, the bright tower of Hitech City standing in the dark like a filament.

"You broke it once," Kiran said. "I won't pretend you didn't. But you came back, and you've been—" he stopped; started again; finished it. "You've been earning it. Every morning. Without keeping score."

"I don't deserve her forgiveness."

"I know. That's why you keep going back. Because you know."

A long silence, and inside it, something settled between the two men like sediment finding the bottom.

"I trust you with her," Kiran said.

Three words, flat, like a man signing a document he'd been drafting for months.

Aditya's throat moved. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Earn it every morning. That's all I'm asking."

---

The standoff with Arnav, when it finally came, took place at dawn, with the monsoon a day away and the air electrical.

The journalist was at the railing first — her railing — waiting. Aditya saw him from the base of the steps and stopped, and the two men regarded each other across the geography of someone else's morning.

"You're always here before her," Arnav said.

"Yes."

"That's dedicated."

"She's a person. Not a story."

"I know that. That's why I want to tell her story. So other people know she's a person too."

"She doesn't need the world to know she's a person," Aditya said. "She needs her morning to be safe."

"I've spoken to neurologists. What she has — the pattern of reset, the way she's adapted — it's extraordinary. People would—"

"Would what? Be inspired? Feel better about their own problems because a woman with a condition woke up and made breakfast?"

Arnav's pen clicked once.

"You're not her family."

"No."

"You're not her legal guardian."

"No."

"Then you can't tell me to stay away from her."

"You're right. I can't." Aditya came up the last steps and took his place at the railing — her place, held for her. "But I can stand between her and you every morning until she arrives, and then walk her somewhere else. I can do that indefinitely. And you'll have to find a different story. One about someone who chose to be found."

Arnav studied him for a moment.

"You're in love with her," he said.

Aditya didn't answer. He didn't need to. The first light broke over the lake — orange, then gold, the Buddha catching it across the water — and footsteps sounded on the stone, and Priya came up the steps and saw Aditya first, and her face did the thing that ran ahead of memory.

Then she checked her notebook, and looked at the stranger with the lanyard, and her hand tightened.

"Red note," she said, just for Aditya. "Journalist."

"Yes."

She looked at Arnav directly, and what she said next she said without heat, without fear, with the flat procedural authority of a woman reading from her own constitution:

"I can't talk to you. My notes say I agreed not to. I trust my notes."

Arnav looked at her for a moment. His pen went still. And something in his face changed — not calculation collapsing into disappointment, but something quieter and more expensive: recognition. He was looking at a person who had built a sovereign system for living, and the system had just declined him admission, courteously, at the border.

"I understand," he said. He put the notepad away. "I'm sorry for the intrusion."

He went down the steps. They watched him go.

Priya took one breath and turned back to the lake.

"He'll come back."

"Maybe," Aditya said. "But today he left."

"Today is enough."

She held out her hand. He took it. And the monsoon broke — not violently, but the way the season's first rain actually comes to that city: soft, warm, almost apologetic, like something that has been waiting a long time at the door and is finally being let in.

---

That afternoon, in the long window — the rare extension, four or five minutes of continuity, rain on her bedroom window — Priya opened the cloth-covered journal, the deep teal one, and wrote:

*I don't know what my story looks like from the outside. I know it has gaps — enormous ones. I know people see me and feel pity or wonder or the particular unease of watching someone live in a way they couldn't.*

*But from the inside, it doesn't feel broken. It feels like standing at a window that's always clean. No accumulated grime of resentment. No old argument sitting at the table. Every morning the same clean light.*

*I chose this man in some window I don't remember. But my hands know him. My body says: stand here. This is the right shape to stand next to.*

*I don't know his faults. I only know his constancy. Maybe that's unfair to him — he can't give me his full self because I can't hold the difficult parts. But he keeps showing up with his full self anyway and trusting that what I have of him is real, even if it's not complete.*

*This is what I know: he carries what I can't. He's angry on my behalf. He walks on the side closest to the road.*

*Maybe love doesn't require the full story. Maybe it only requires the body's testimony. The hand that reaches. The face that opens.*

*Write this down. Because in seven seconds you'll forget you thought it.*

*But you'll feel it again tomorrow. At the lake. At 6:47 AM. In the warmth before knowledge.*

*That's enough. That's everything.*

She finished. Read it once. Closed the journal.

Then she took a blue Post-it from the stack and wrote five words on it and pinned it at the exact centre of the wall, over the heart of the blue cluster, where the story of her — the one she was writing without being able to read it — had been waiting for its title:

LOVE IS WHAT THE BODY KNOWS.

## Chapter Twenty-Six — The Geometry Lessons

There is a catalogue nobody compiled, of the things Aditya learned in the first year that no one taught him.

He learned that her startle had a shape: it began in the spine and resolved through the shoulders, and that if he was already inside her peripheral vision when a reset came, the startle never fired at all — so he built his entire physical life with her inside a 120-degree arc to her left, like a man living on a stage with one fixed light.

He learned that questions were better than statements in the first two seconds after a turn. *You were just telling me about the lake* worked; *we were talking* did not. The question handed her the wheel. The statement told her someone else had been driving.

He learned her hierarchy of evidence: her own handwriting outranked everything; blue ink outranked black; the inside cover outranked the pages; and anything in Kiran's aggressive capitals was constitutional law. He learned to write in her notebook only when invited, and that being invited was a temperature you could feel in the conversation, and that the invitation, once given, was among the largest things she had to give — a citizenship in the country of her continuity.

He learned the windows. The ordinary seven seconds, first — but then the deeper weather of it: the way cold mornings stretched the loop, the way stress shortened it, the way the long windows came most often in the evening, at the wall, or with a brush in her hand. He kept a private log for two months until he caught himself optimising — planning the important sentences for predicted long windows, like a man timing the market — and burned the log, ashamed, and went back to saying important things whenever they were true.

He learned that she knew. That was the one that rearranged him. Not the facts — the facts came and went with the tide. She knew *him*, in the way the kalamkari painter's hand knew the tree of life: a knowledge below retrieval, written into the system that doesn't reset. She proved it in a hundred small accidents — the second cup ordered before he arrived, the lean toward the steps before his footfall registered, the way her drawings of him, morning after unconnected morning, gave him open hands.

The notes carried the facts. The body carried the truth. He had it the wrong way around for months, he realised — he had thought he was using the notes to reach her, when the notes were only ever the formal paperwork of something the body had already ratified.

After that he relaxed in some final way, like a man who stops shouting when he learns the other person was never deaf in the language that mattered.


# PART THREE — THE SHAPE OF HOME

## Chapter Twenty-Seven — The Repaired Stone

Post-monsoon, and the city had been washed clean. The lake lay still in the golden hour, and the Buddha caught the early light like a man who has been waiting all night for exactly this and intends to show no excitement about it.

The loose stone on the steps had been repaired. New grey concrete, slightly brighter than the old. Somewhere in the municipal machinery, somebody had filed a maintenance request, and somebody else had honoured it, and a small wrongness that two people had built a private language around was gone.

Aditya stood on the steps looking at it. For a moment his face was the face of a man who has come home to find the tree he carved initials into cut down for timber.

Then he smiled — small, private — and stepped over the spot where the loose stone used to be, slightly left, weight forward, out of pure habit. The body keeping faith with a world that no longer required it.

He went up.

"The stone is fixed," Priya said, at the railing.

"Yes."

"How do I know the stone used to be loose?"

"Because you've been stepping around it for seven months. Your body remembers even when your notes don't."

She looked down at her own feet with the expression of a woman discovering her shoes had been conducting a correspondence behind her back.

"My body knew to step around a loose stone."

"Your body knows a lot of things."

She opened her notebook. The inside cover had grown over the months, the entries accreting like coral:

ADITYA SHARMA. 26. GRAPHIC DESIGNER. HAS LOVED YOU EVERY MORNING FOR SEVEN MONTHS. SAFE. KIRAN APPROVED. AMMA APPROVED.

"Twenty-six," she said.

"I had a birthday. You brought me an Osmania biscuit with a candle in it."

She stared at him.

"You'd forgotten by the time the candle lit," he said. "But you helped me blow it out. You seemed very committed to the cause."

Her mouth opened. Closed. And then she laughed — real, sudden, her hand going to her mouth, the sound rising up over the lake and hanging there.

"An Osmania biscuit."

"With a candle. And you sang half of 'Happy Birthday' and then stopped and looked at me and said, 'Who is this for?'"

She laughed harder. The lake took the sound and held it. A jogger smiled without knowing why.

He watched her laugh, and his face was not performing happiness. It was receiving it — the face of a man standing in rain that has finally, after a long dry season, decided to come.

---

"You're going to propose," Meera said at the studio, looking at the framed poster leaning against his wall — two figures at a railing, the lake, the words HUSSAIN SAGAR — 6:47 AM. "Aren't you."

Aditya didn't look up from the screen.

"I'm going to tell her. Every morning. For as long as we have. What I feel. Not the future — she can't map the future. But the feeling. The real one. Over and over, every day, fresh. Like she hears it for the first time. Because she does."

"And you can live with that?"

He turned from the screen.

"I can live with giving her something that feels like the first morning every morning. Yes. I think most people would call that the dream."

Meera looked at the poster a while.

"She made you better," she said.

"She made me honest. I was already in there. She just—" he searched for it, "—she doesn't let you be lazy about it. You can't coast. You can't be good-enough-for-someone-who-won't-notice. She notices. The body notices. And the body doesn't lie."

## Chapter Twenty-Eight — Things Last

They go to Golconda at dusk, when the stone is still warm from the day's sun and the sound-and-light crew is rigging the evening show, and they stand on the high platform with the city turning orange-rose-violet below.

Priya has her notebook but isn't reading it. She is looking at the fort walls — the sheer fact of them, the weight of them against the sky.

"How old is this?"

"Five hundred years. Roughly. It's been many things. Kingdom. Fort. Prison. Diamond market."

"And now?"

"Now it's just a place people bring someone they want to show something to."

She looks at him. "What are you showing me?"

He takes a moment, because he has been carrying this sentence for a week, waiting for the place that deserved it.

"That things that last don't last because they were built to resist. They last because people kept choosing to come back."

A beat. The light deepens. The first star takes its post above the ramparts.

"Is this in my notes?" she asks.

"Not yet."

She opens the notebook and writes: GOLCONDA. THINGS LAST BECAUSE PEOPLE KEEP CHOOSING TO COME BACK. She reads it back. Looks at him.

"Am I understanding this correctly?"

"You understand it better than I do."

She holds the notebook. Her free hand finds his.

"Write something for me."

He takes the pen she offers and writes, in his slanted hand, in the blue section:

I HAVE CHOSEN TO COME BACK. I WILL KEEP CHOOSING.

She reads it. Reads it again.

"Every morning?"

"Every morning."

She leans her head against his shoulder, and behind them the fort lights bloom and the recorded narration begins, telling the stone's history in Telugu and then in English, kingdoms and sieges and diamonds, and she doesn't follow any of it, because she already has the only version that matters, in blue, in her own book:

Things last because people keep choosing to come back.

---

That night she stands before her wall — full now, the blue concentrated at the centre like the bright core of something seen through a telescope — and adds the evening's note before sleep:

GOLCONDA. HE WRITES "I WILL KEEP CHOOSING." BELIEVE HIM. THE BODY ALREADY DOES.

Then she reads the wall. The whole of it, slowly, the way she reads it every night before the long dark — her story, built by herself, post-it by post-it, morning by morning.

She is not someone to whom things happen. She is someone who constructs herself, daily, deliberately, from the only materials available: what she can observe, what her body retains, what the people who love her carry on her behalf.

She turns off the light. The wall glows faintly in the streetlight — the blue centre, the long history of her own handwriting — and somewhere across the city a man with three Post-its in his wallet is awake too, drawing a door with light underneath it, and on the other side of the door, in the drawing, it is always 6:47 in the morning.

## Chapter Twenty-Nine — Asking the Keeper

"He asked me," Kiran said.

Lakshmi didn't stop stirring the chai. "When?"

"Yesterday. At the studio. He showed me the poster he made — the one with both of them at the lake. He said: 'I want to be there for all of it. I want to be the person who stands at her railing. Not just mornings. Everything.'"

She poured the chai and set a cup in front of him.

"What did you say?"

"I said: she can't give you a long-form yes. She can't sit down and have the conversation that leads somewhere permanent."

"And?"

"He said: 'I know. So I'm asking you. Because you carry what she can't. And if you say yes, then every morning when she finds my name in her notes and it says safe — that's the yes. That's the one she gives me every single day without having to remember.'"

Lakshmi looked out the window at the rain, at the lake's grey shimmer in the distance behind the buildings.

"And you said?"

Kiran picked up his chai. "I said yes."

Lakshmi put her hand over her son's hand. Once. They drank their chai while the rain worked at the window, and neither of them said anything else, because the kitchen had heard everything it needed to hear, and some agreements are damaged by elaboration.

## Chapter Thirty — Seven Yeses

Winter came to Hyderabad the way it always did — mildly, almost apologetically, a season that visited rather than governed. The mornings turned sharp and clear. Priya's breath made small clouds at the railing, and Lakshmi had laid out the purple shawl with the Kalamkari border, and the lake had the clarity of cold water: everything precise, the Buddha distinct, the city behind it cut from tin.

Aditya arrived in his jacket. She held out the half-eaten Osmania biscuit; he took it. This was how every morning began now. This was the ritual the body had made.

"I dreamed last night," she said.

"What did you dream?"

"I don't know. But I woke up with the feeling that something very good had happened. And I checked my notes and they said nothing about yesterday, but the feeling—" she put her hand flat against her chest, "—was here. Real."

"Body memory."

"Body memory," she agreed.

He was quiet a moment. The lake held still, as if it knew.

"Priya."

She looked at him. He reached into his jacket pocket and brought out a ring — simple, gold, set with a small chip of emerald that was the exact colour of the lake in summer. He had spent four Sundays finding that colour. The jeweller in Abids had begun greeting him with visible dread.

She looked at it.

"I've been trying to figure out how to do this correctly," he said, "given that you won't remember this moment in seven seconds. And I thought: if I only have seven seconds—"

He closed his eyes once. Opened them on her.

"I want to be here. At this railing. With you. Every morning for as long as there are mornings. I want to be the person your notes say is safe. I want Kiran to trust me with every version of you that exists. I want to learn your geometry in every season. I want to carry what you can't so you never have to notice the weight." A pause, the whole lake in it. "Will you marry me?"

Seven seconds.

She looked at the ring. At his face. At the lake. Her hand went to her notebook, and he waited — he had promised himself he would wait every single time, however many times it took, without prompting, without pleading, letting her find her own way to it through her own system, because the system *was* her, and he was not marrying around it, he was marrying into it.

She read the inside cover: ADITYA SHARMA. LOVES YOU. KIRAN APPROVED. AMMA APPROVED. SAFE. CHOOSES TO COME BACK.

She closed the notebook. Looked at him.

"Yes."

Seven seconds passed. The page turned. She surfaced — and there was a ring in the air between them, held in the steady hand of a man whose face was open and waiting, and something in her chest moved faster than thought, the body voting before the mind could convene.

"Yes," she said.

Seven seconds.

"Yes."

He didn't move through any of it. He let her find her way to it every time, fresh, and every time she arrived at the same country by a new road. The same answer. Arrived at, never retrieved.

He slid the ring onto her finger. She held her hand up and the winter light went into the emerald and got comfortable.

"It looks like the lake in summer," she said.

"I know. I thought you'd say that."

"Do I say it every time?"

"First time you've said it," he said. "But you will. Because it's true."

---

She reset at the railing a minute later and found the ring on her finger like a small green dawn.

"This is new," she said.

"You said yes."

She read her notes. Her face did the quiet opening — the warmth before knowledge.

"The lake colour," she said, tilting her hand.

"Yes."

"Smart."

She went back to looking at the lake. He went back to looking at her looking at the lake. The Osmania biscuit sat between them on the railing, half-eaten, holding its office.

The loose stone repaired. The wrong choices owned and carried. The journalist gone. The brother persuaded. The mother had fed him dinner fourteen times.

Every morning. Every reset. Every first.

This was how they would live.

---

At the engagement dinner, Lakshmi made the special-occasion biryani, the one that took four hours, and the family sat around the table — and Aditya sat at it the way family sits: without announcement.

Priya read her notes every few minutes. Kiran no longer flinched when she did.

"Why is today special?" she asked, over the biryani steam.

"Because he finally asked," Kiran said, "and you finally said yes."

She read the new entry: ENGAGED. TO ADITYA. THE ONE YOUR BODY ALREADY KNEW. She looked up. Looked at Aditya. Looked at the ring.

"Oh," she said. And then, to Kiran: "Do you approve?"

"I approved months ago. I told him before he asked."

"You asked my brother first?" she said to Aditya.

"I asked your brother because he's the one who remembers. It seemed right that the person who carries your full story should be the one who gives the permission."

She sat with this. Under the table, her hand found his.

"That's why you're safe," she said, low. "You understand what the notes can't say."

"Eat," Lakshmi said. "The biryani is getting cold."

They ate. The rain worked at the window. The lake lay somewhere out in the dark, holding their mornings for them until they came back for more.

## Chapter Thirty-One — Seven Confessions

The crisis, when it finally arrived, did not look like a crisis. It looked like a man taking a biscuit.

Not a journalist. Not a party. Not a moment of cowardice caught on someone's phone. Something quieter — something Aditya had been carrying alone since the beginning, and which had not gotten lighter with the engagement, because the things we carry alone never get lighter; they only get older.

He came up the steps. Priya was at the railing. She read her notes, smiled, held out the biscuit. He took it.

And he stood beside her and thought: *she doesn't know about the party. She doesn't know about the three weeks. She doesn't know that Rohan's words landed somewhere in me and never fully left — that there was a moment, a real one, not a thought but a felt thing, when I understood that her forgetting was convenient for me. She doesn't know. She will never know. I carry it alone.*

He had been carrying it for eight months.

He looked at her. The ring on her finger. The lake.

He made his decision.

"I need to tell you something."

She looked at him.

"You have seven seconds. So I'll keep it to seven seconds. And then I'll tell you again."

"Okay," she said, in the tone of a woman who has agreed to stranger procedures than this.

"Eight months ago there was a party. Someone said something cruel about you. About how your forgetting made you easy to love. I didn't stop it fast enough. And for one moment — a real one — I felt the truth of it. I have never told you. You wouldn't remember if I did. But I needed to say it once while you could hear it."

She looked at him.

Seven seconds.

The page turned. She surfaced — lake, ring, the man beside her, her notes, his face still holding the same open, waiting, undefended expression — and he began again:

"Eight months ago there was a party. Someone said something cruel about you. I didn't stop it fast enough. I felt something I'm not proud of. And I needed you to know that I know it was wrong."

Reset.

He told her again.

And again.

Seven confessions, each one fitted to its window, each one complete in itself — no instalments, no remainder carried forward, because nothing could be carried forward; the whole debt had to be paid in full each time, in the present tense, the only currency she accepted. And her face, hearing it fresh each time: no accumulated wound, no history of the harm, no flinch trained in by repetition. Just the man in front of her, honest, the morning light between them, the words arriving for — as far as she knew — the first time, every time.

On the seventh:

"I forgive you," she said.

His eyes closed.

"What you just said—" she didn't know about the previous six; she would never know about the previous six; the six belonged to him and the lake and no one else, "—that took courage. Because you didn't have to. I would never know."

"That's exactly why I had to," he said.

She read his face the way she read everything — not the words, not the history. The body's testimony: a man who has confessed and been forgiven, standing lighter inside his own frame, the load-bearing walls of him visibly relieved.

Her hand found his jaw.

"Write this in my notebook," she said. "That you told me. And that I said: you've already been forgiven for every version of it."

He took her pen and wrote, beneath all the other entries that made up the ledger of them:

HE TOLD YOU THE HARD THING. YOU SAID: YOU WERE ALREADY FORGIVEN.

Blue note. Her instructions. Her grammar, his hand.

Around them the boardwalk refilled — joggers, chai-sellers, the early walkers, the world's ordinary commerce resuming as if nothing had happened, which, by the only clock she kept, was true.

"I keep finding this ring on my hand," she said, a few resets later, holding it up to the light.

"You said yes."

"I keep saying yes."

He smiled.

"That's not a complaint," she said.

"I know."

From his cart down the path, Raju Bhaiya watched them — the biscuit, the chai, the comfortable weight of two people who had learned each other's geometry — and said one word to himself, in Telugu, with the satisfaction of a man whose long-running forecast has finally been confirmed by the weather:

"Finally."

He made the chai. The day began.

## Chapter Thirty-Two — The Article

The article went up in early November, and Aditya read it at the railing at 6:30 in the morning, before she arrived, his thumb scrolling at the speed you read something you're afraid of.

THE WOMAN WHO LIVES IN SEVEN-SECOND WINDOWS. By Arnav Kapoor. An online magazine. Two hundred thousand shares.

It was — not cruel. That was the first surprise. Arnav had written what he had seen: the boardwalk, the notebook, the pre-dawn ritual, the blue Post-its, the man who arrives before her. He had changed her name. He had taken no photograph. He had described her from a distance, and the distance read as something Aditya had not expected from him: care.

But still. Her story. Unnamed but visible. Out in the world, shared and re-shared, annotated by strangers.

Aditya set the phone face-down on the railing. His chest rose once. The Buddha watched from the water, unimpressed by circulation figures.

Kiran called at 6:40.

"Did you see it?"

"Yes."

"He kept his word. No name. No photo. No location."

"The boardwalk is identifiable. Anyone who knows Hussain Sagar will know it."

Silence on the line.

"I'm going to read her the relevant section this morning," Kiran said. "Give her the choice about what the note says."

"I'll be there."

"She'll find out either way — someone she knows will mention it. Better she hears it from us first, even if she won't remember after seven seconds." A pause; then, reluctantly, like a man conceding a point to an opponent he'd prepared a whole case against: "The article is actually not bad. He wrote about her like she was a person."

"He did."

"I still don't like it."

"I know. Me neither. But it's done."

"So we make a note. We tell her what's true. And she decides how much she cares."

The three of them stood at the railing an hour later — Aditya, Kiran, Priya — and Kiran read her the relevant passage. She reset twice during the reading. He read the passage twice, beginning again without comment, his voice level both times.

She stood with it.

"He said I live in seven-second windows?"

"Yes," Kiran said.

"I don't think of it as windows. I think of it as light. Every window gets light."

Neither man said anything, because there was nothing either of them could add to that without subtracting from it.

"What do I want the note to say?" she asked — and the question was real; she was consulting the committee that carried her continuity. "Someone wrote about me. I'm not in the article by name. But I'm there. Do I want to be upset about this?" She looked at her notebook. "There's no existing note that tells me whether to care about this kind of thing."

She thought, her finger against her lower lip. The lake worked through its greys.

"If the article makes one person feel less alone in something difficult — is that bad?"

"No," Kiran said.

"But it's my story. Without my permission."

"Yes," Aditya said. "That part is wrong."

"But the story is kind?"

"The story is kind."

She nodded, slowly, a judge assembling a verdict from competing briefs.

"Then write this." She handed Aditya the pen. "'Someone wrote about your life without asking. The story was kind. You decided: let it be. Not everything needs a response. Some things just need to be released.'"

He wrote it. Blue note. Her voice, his hand.

She took it, read it, nodded once. "That sounds like me."

"It's exactly you," he said, low.

She put the note in the inside pocket of the notebook — not on the wall, not in the urgent section. Just held. Like a letter read, answered, and set aside.

## Chapter Thirty-Three — Warangal

He took her to Warangal in November, three weeks before the wedding, because a man should bring the woman he is marrying to the house he grew up in, and because his mother had issued the invitation in a form that admitted no appeal: "Bring her before the wedding. I want to meet her at my own table, not across a mandap with four hundred people watching."

The logistics took two weeks of planning between three households. A new travel section in the notebook, drafted by Lakshmi and ratified by Priya in a long window. The route loaded on the tablet. A laminated card for her wrist with the unfamiliar nouns: WARANGAL. SAVITRI — ADITYA'S MOTHER. YOU ARE A GUEST AND ALSO FAMILY. THE HOUSE HAS A WELL IN THE COURTYARD. THERE IS A DOG. THE DOG IS CALLED MOTI. MOTI IS SAFE.

The dog was the first triumph. Moti — old, golden, diplomatically fat — met them at the gate, and Priya knelt and took his face in both hands before any note could be consulted, because dogs live where she lives, in the eternal present, and the two of them recognised each other across it instantly, fellow citizens. Every reset for the rest of the visit, she rediscovered Moti with full joy, and Moti, who had never once been greeted that many times with that much enthusiasm by anyone, attached himself to her permanently and had to be physically prevented from boarding the car when they left.

"He's met his match," Savitri said, watching from the verandah. "Neither of them holds yesterday against anybody."

The house was old Telangana — thick walls, red oxide floors cool underfoot, the courtyard well with its rope-grooved stone lip, a kitchen where three generations of brass had been arranged by hands that all knew the same arrangement. Savitri ran it alone and completely, a woman who moved through any room as if she had always lived in it because, in this case, she had.

She had prepared for the visit in her own way. Aditya saw it the moment they entered: yellow Post-its, on the doorframes, in his mother's careful Telugu-English hand. THIS IS THE KITCHEN. THE BATHROOM IS THE BLUE DOOR. ADITYA'S CHILDHOOD ROOM — PHOTOS ON THE WALL. She had telephoned Lakshmi for the format. She had bought the correct brand.

"Amma," he said, and had to stop and look at the well for a while.

"What? I read about it. The system goes where she goes. Now the house is in the system." She took Priya's arm. "Come. I'll show you what he looked like before the hair gel."

The photographs were the afternoon's second triumph. Aditya at six, in school uniform, ears like jug handles. Aditya at eleven, holding a drawing prize certificate upside down. Aditya at sixteen, attempting a moustache the way a small army attempts a large border. Priya moved down the wall reading each one fresh, and at some point she took out her sketchbook and began copying the six-year-old's face, and reset, and found her own half-finished copy, and laughed, and finished it anyway — "whoever started this had the ears exactly right" — and Savitri stood in the doorway of her son's childhood room watching a woman with no memory take custody of his entire past, seven seconds at a time, with more attention than anyone had ever paid it.

At dinner, the two of them fell into Telugu — the Warangal register, older and rounder than the city's — and Aditya, whose Telugu still wore its Delhi accent like a guest's slippers, was left partly behind, which both women appeared to find correct.

"She asks good questions," Savitri reported to him afterwards, in the courtyard, while Priya sat on the verandah steps drawing the well by moonlight with Moti's head on her knee. "She asked me the same question four times. Each time I answered it differently, and the fourth answer was the true one. Do you understand? She asked until I told the truth. Nobody does that. Everybody accepts the first answer because they remember being given it."

"What was the question?"

His mother folded her hands and looked at the well.

"She asked whether I was sad. About — the way it is. The condition. Whether I had wanted something different for you."

He waited. The well held its black coin of sky.

"The first three answers were polite," Savitri said. "The fourth one was: I wanted you to be loved the way your father loved me. All day, like it was new. He died before you were old enough to see it properly, and I have spent twenty years watching you charm people instead of love them, and I had given up." She unfolded her hands. "And then you brought me a girl who cannot do anything but new. So no. I am not sad. I got the thing I wanted, by the strangest road God ever drew."

Aditya looked at the verandah, where Priya had reset and was greeting Moti again, both hands in his fur, joy without history, and the dog's tail thumped the old stone like a slow drum.

"The fourth answer," he said. "That's the one she wrote down."

"I know," Savitri said. "She showed me. Blue note." She turned to go in, and paused at the door with the smallest smile of a woman who had read the literature on her daughter-in-law's filing system. "I checked what blue means. Lakshmi told me." The smile widened by one degree, exactly. "Joy. She filed me under joy. Twenty years I've cooked for relatives who file me under obligation."

On the last morning, leaving, Priya consulted the laminated card and the notes and then, unprompted, hugged Savitri at the gate — the body voting first, as always — and said: "I don't remember arriving. But the whole house feels like him. Did you know that? Everything in it is arranged by someone who pays attention."

"That's the nicest thing anyone has ever said about my housekeeping," Savitri said.

"It wasn't about housekeeping," Priya said.

Savitri stood at the gate with Moti mourning at her feet until the car had turned out of sight, and then she went inside and did something she would tell no one, though Lakshmi, who knew everything eventually, knew it within the month and approved without comment: she took one of the spare yellow Post-its, and wrote on it, in her careful hand, and stuck it inside her own cupboard door, where housekeeping and prayer keep their private accounts —

SHE FILED YOU UNDER JOY. BEHAVE ACCORDINGLY.

## Chapter Thirty-Four — The Wedding Notebook

"Novotel or Taj Falaknuma?" Kiran asked, holding the venue folder.

"Falaknuma. Obviously," Lakshmi said.

"The logistics with her notes—"

"I've been thinking about it." She had been thinking about it, it would emerge, for the better part of a year. "We make a wedding notebook. The same system. Red, green, blue. She gets the same introduction card every morning of the wedding week. 'Today is your wedding. Aditya. You love him. The flowers are marigold and jasmine. It is good.'"

Kiran looked at his mother. "You've been planning this for a while."

"Since the first morning I saw her come home from the lake smiling at a blue note she'd written herself." She plated his breakfast, matter-of-fact. "Some children tell their mothers when they fall in love. Mine leaves herself instructions."

He sat. The morning light slanted across the folder — the palace on its cover, sandstone towers catching a printed sky.

"She's going to discover the wedding every morning that week," he said, "and think it's the first time."

"Yes."

"Is that — is that sad?"

Lakshmi considered the question with the seriousness it deserved, which was not much.

"She will be the only bride in history," she said, "to feel the joy of her wedding day seven times before lunch."

He stared at her. She served breakfast with complete serenity.

---

The invitation was Priya's own text, drafted in one of her long windows — six versions, and only one of them written in blue, which was how the family knew which one was true:

*Come to a wedding. Priya Anand and Aditya Sharma. December 14th. Taj Falaknuma. She won't remember you're coming. Come anyway. She'll be delighted to meet you.*

"You can't put that on the invitation," Meera said, reading it at the studio.

"Her idea. Kiran approved it. Lakshmi thought it was the funniest thing she'd ever read and then cried for fifteen minutes."

Meera read it again. "That's — actually perfect."

---

In late November, in the longest window anyone had recorded — ten full minutes, the cool-evening extension she had begun to document like a scientist of her own weather — Priya stood at her wall rearranging the blue cluster. Thirty, forty notes now, overlapping, a galaxy of things she had chosen to carry.

GOLCONDA FORT. THE SHAPE OF SAFE.
HE COUNTS TO SEVEN AND STAYS.
YOU SAID YOU LOVED. HE WROTE IT DOWN.
HE TOLD YOU THE HARD THING. YOU SAID: YOU WERE ALREADY FORGIVEN.
DECEMBER. THE WEDDING. YOU SAY: YES.
TODAY IS A GOOD DAY. TRUST THIS NOTE.

She pressed her palm flat against the cluster. Not reading anymore. Just touching — the way you rest a hand on a sleeping animal, to feel the warmth and the breathing.

"Amma," she said, without turning.

Lakshmi, in the doorway, startled. "How did you know?"

"The sound of your breathing changes when you're worried about me. It slows down, like you're trying not to disturb something."

Lakshmi came in and sat on the edge of the bed. "I'm not worried."

"I know. But you're feeling something you don't have a word for."

"My daughter is getting married in three weeks."

"Every morning I discover that," Priya said, "and feel very happy."

"Every morning?" Lakshmi was smiling through something difficult.

"Every single one. Seven times before lunch."

Lakshmi laughed — not politely; fully; a mother's laugh, surprised out of her like a bird flushed from cover.

Priya turned from the wall and crossed and sat beside her.

"I know it's hard to watch, Amma. I know you wonder if I'm really — here. Really choosing. Really present for my own life."

"Priya—"

"But I am here. More than most people. Because I can't afford to be anywhere else." A pause. "I'm sorry I can't give you the daughter who plans the wedding and remembers every detail for the rest of her life."

"I don't want that daughter," Lakshmi said. "I have the daughter who is genuinely happy every morning for the next three weeks."

Priya leaned against her mother's shoulder. Through the open window came the night air, cooler now, jasmine from the neighbour's fence, and from somewhere deep in the colony the sound of a dhol — another family's wedding, another procession, the city's permanent season of beginnings.

"I can hear a wedding," Priya said.

"That's not yours."

"Not yet."

## Chapter Thirty-Five — The Week of Mornings

The wedding notebook was bound in raw silk, marigold-coloured, and Lakshmi had built it the way she built everything — for the worst case, in hope of the best.

Seven introduction cards, one per morning of the wedding week, identical: TODAY IS [DAY]. YOUR WEDDING IS ON SUNDAY. ADITYA. YOU LOVE HIM. EVERYONE IS HAPPY. THERE IS NOTHING TO CATCH UP ON. The last sentence was Priya's own, dictated in a long window when the notebook was being designed: "Tell me there's nothing to catch up on," she had said. "That's the only thing that frightens me — the feeling that everyone else is carrying something I dropped. Tell me nobody's waiting for me to remember. Then I can just be there."

So the week went like this:

On Monday she discovered she was getting married, and was delighted, and helped Lakshmi choose between two shades of marigold for the entrance garlands by holding each one up against the kitchen light, and chose the deeper one, and wrote her reasoning on a green note so that Tuesday's Priya could ratify it.

On Tuesday she discovered she was getting married, and was delighted, and found Monday's note about the garlands, and wrote beneath it: AGREED. GOOD EYE.

On Wednesday she discovered she was getting married, and was delighted, and the mehendi artist came, and Priya sat for three hours of intricate work she experienced as several hundred first moments of watching beauty appear on her own hands — each reset arriving to find the pattern further along, magic with no visible magician — and the artist, who had been briefed, and who had wept twice quietly into her sleeve, said afterwards it was the best afternoon of her working life: a client astonished, on a loop, for three hours.

On Thursday, Savitri arrived from Warangal, and was met at the door by a daughter-in-law who consulted a silk notebook and then embraced her with total warmth, and Savitri — who had said *good, she'll always be happy to meet me* and been quoted on it for a year — discovered that being right about something and standing inside it were different sizes of experience. She sat in the kitchen with Lakshmi that evening, the two mothers, and said: "She met me three times today. Every time, her whole face. My own sister doesn't greet me with her whole face." And Lakshmi, who had spent three years learning what to do with sentences like this, poured the chai and said: "Now you know. It's not a smaller life, amma. It's a concentrated one."

On Friday she discovered she was getting married, and was delighted, and there was a small crisis about the shawl — the purple Kalamkari-border one had been packed for the palace and an anxious cousin had unpacked it for safekeeping into a place so safe it took four people two hours — and through all of it Priya sat serenely sketching the chaos, because for her each seven seconds contained only mildly animated relatives, and her notes said everything was fine, and her notes had never once lied to her. "She's the calmest bride in Telangana," the cousin said, half-hysterical, and it was simply true, and the family laughed in the way of people discovering that the thing they had braced against was, from one angle, the gift.

On Saturday night, Kiran placed the palace notebook on the pillow where her hand would land, measured the distance, adjusted it twice, and stood in the doorway a long time, unrecorded.

And on Sunday she woke under high marble arches to the smell of structural jasmine, and reached out, and found it.

TODAY IS YOUR WEDDING DAY.

Joy, arriving on time. Seven times before lunch — Lakshmi's arithmetic had been conservative; the cousins, who kept an affectionate private count, logged twenty-three discoveries before the pheras, each one complete, each one whole, the same sunrise rising again and again on the same astonished country.

## Chapter Thirty-Six — Twelve Steps

On the morning of December the fourteenth, Priya wakes under an unfamiliar ceiling — high arches, marble, the smell of jasmine arriving in quantities that can only be described as structural — and her hand goes out automatically and finds the notebook on the pillow, positioned exactly where her hand would reach, left there at midnight by her brother, who measured the distance.

She opens it. Inside cover, in large blue letters, in Kiran's aggressive capitals:

TODAY IS YOUR WEDDING DAY.
YOU ARE AT TAJ FALAKNUMA PALACE.
TODAY YOU MARRY ADITYA SHARMA.
THE MAN FROM THE LAKE.
YOUR NOTES SAY YOU LOVE HIM.
YOUR BODY WILL CONFIRM IT.
THE FLOWERS ARE MARIGOLD AND JASMINE.
AMMA IS DOWN THE HALL. KNOCK TWICE.
IT IS GOOD. IT IS VERY GOOD.
HAPPY WEDDING DAY, PRIYA.

She reads it. Reads it again. Her hands go to her face, both palms pressing in, and her shoulders shake once — not grief; something too full for one container. She lowers her hands and looks at the note again and smiles at her brother's handwriting, the underline beneath GOOD pressed so hard it has nearly torn the page.

She resets.

Eyes open. Unfamiliar ceiling. Jasmine. Notebook.

TODAY IS YOUR WEDDING DAY.

The joy comes fresh and complete, the whole sunrise of it, second-hand to no one.

She reaches for the door.

---

In the bride's room the women orbited her — Lakshmi, Meera, two cousins, an aunt — and the mehendi dried on her hands and feet, and the bridal silk was draped and pinned, and the gold went on at her throat and ears. Each time she reset, someone held up the notebook, open to the inside cover, and each time the same smile arrived: fresh, real, unperformed, joy happening to her the way sunrise happens to a window.

By mid-morning the cousins had learned to anticipate the reset; they watched for the small tidal pause in her breathing and were ready with the notebook before the question formed. No one was distressed. The room had adapted. It had become a rhythm, and then, imperceptibly, a ceremony of its own — the first rite of the day, repeated, the bride consenting to her own joy over and over.

"I've been to a hundred weddings," Meera said from the corner, under the room's noise. "This is the first one where the bride has been purely happy all morning."

"The notes do what memory usually does," Lakshmi said. "They hold the good thing so she doesn't have to."

"We should all have notes."

"We do," Lakshmi said. "We just don't write them down."

Priya held her hands out to the light — the mehendi dark, the ring visible through the pattern, the blue glass bangles from Laad Bazaar threaded among the gold bridal ones, because she had insisted, reading her own note about them, that *these ones I chose*.

"These are good hands," she said, to no one in particular. "I think they've done good things."

---

The hall was Nizami arches and chandelier light, marigold thick along the columns, jasmine in the air like a held chord. The guests had come — and they had come knowing, because the invitation had told them, and not one of them had stayed away.

Kiran stood with his sister at the entrance, her hand on his arm, his hand over hers.

She checked her notebook: KIRAN. BROTHER. YOUR PERSON.

"Is it far?" she whispered.

"Twelve steps. I'll count them with you."

"All twelve?"

"All twelve."

They began. His mouth moved beside her ear, barely audible — one, two, three — counting not for himself but for her, an anchor laid against the reset that could come at any step.

At step seven, it came.

His counting didn't stop. He squeezed her hand. She surfaced mid-stride — silk, gold, jasmine, a hall full of faces, her hand on her brother's arm, his voice saying *eight* — and she looked down at the notebook riding open in her free hand.

TODAY IS YOUR WEDDING DAY.

She looked up.

Aditya. Thirty feet away. At the fire.

Something crossed her face that was not recognition and was not memory. Something before both — the body making its oldest decision, the one it had been rehearsing every morning for a year at a lake railing.

Her feet continued without instruction.

Nine. Ten. Eleven. Twelve.

She stood beside him. He turned, and before anything else could be said, he said it — quietly, for her alone, the introduction perfected over four hundred mornings:

"I'm Aditya Sharma. You love me. I love you. Your notes will confirm it. I'm the one from the lake."

She looked at him. At her notebook. At him.

"I know," she said.

The pandit began. The fire took its first ghee and stood up bright.

She reset four times during the pheras. Each time she came back to: fire, him, the warmth of his hand guiding hers, smoke, Sanskrit rising through the palace air. Each time, her hand in his was already there — her body had never stopped walking the circle.

On the fourth reset, mid-phera, she surfaced into the heat of the fire on her face and the weight of the silk and his hand — and she did not look at her notebook.

She looked at his profile in the firelight.

And she walked the circle. Her choice, made without instruction, without note, without memory — made purely by the body that had learned, over months of mornings, what safe feels like, and walks toward it.

The pandit placed the sindoor.

Kiran watched his sister's face from the first row, and his jaw was set, and his eyes were wet, and he pressed his lips together once — not performing anything. A man watching his sister be happy in a way he had spent three years fearing she never could be, and being quietly, completely wrong, and grateful to be wrong, which is the rarest gratitude there is.

---

On the terrace that evening, with the city glittering below and the shehnai winding through the cold December air, she reset and opened her notebook and read TODAY IS YOUR WEDDING DAY and looked at the man beside her.

"We're married?"

"As of three hours ago."

"How was it?"

"You walked the pheras without looking at your notes. Not once."

She took this in, turning it in the light like a bangle.

"I trusted the body."

"The body knew."

"It always does."

Below them the wedding traffic dispersed into the city's rivers of light. Above, the winter sky had cleared to stars.

"Tell me something I won't remember," she said.

He considered.

"In seven seconds, you'll forget this. But right now, on the night of our wedding, you are the most extraordinary person I have ever stood next to. And I want to be standing next to you for every morning I have left."

She watched his face the whole way through it.

"I'm going to forget that."

"I know."

"You said it anyway."

"That's the point," he said.

She lifted her face to the sky — the cold, the stars, the sound of people who had come even though she would not remember them coming, and had been delighted to meet every one of them, some of them twice.

"Ask me something," she said. "Something only I would know."

He thought. "What does the boardwalk smell like at 6:47 in the morning?"

Without hesitation:

"Wet stone. Irani chai from down the path. If it's just rained: mud and marigold. Sometimes — if the wind comes from the south — something that smells like tangedu."

"You smell the tangedu?"

"Every third morning, roughly. It's yellow. You can't always see it from the boardwalk, but the wind carries it."

He looked at her. "I've been going there for a year and I've never noticed the tangedu."

"I notice it every third morning for the first time." A pause, and then she gave him the sentence like a wedding gift, the only kind she could give, the kind minted entirely in the present: "Some things are better fresh."

## Chapter Thirty-Seven — The House That Anchors

Their apartment in Jubilee Hills had the quality of a place designed for her — not clinically, but the way a person builds around what they love: warm walls, good light, and on every surface, small anchors.

A ring holder stood on the nightstand, shaped like a railing. Her idea — after a morning three weeks into the marriage when she had come to the boardwalk without the ring, and her hand had reached for the notebook in the old way, and her face had carried a brief confusion that lasted only one reset and that Aditya carried for a week. Now the ring lived on its railing every night, and every morning she found it and put it on: the daily rediscovery, the small green dawn.

The hall wall was not the Post-it wall — she had a version of that too, in the bedroom, growing its coral — but frames. Small frames, and inside each one, a blue Post-it. Not all of them. Just the ones she had chosen to keep permanently, promoted from paper to glass:

HE COUNTS TO SEVEN AND STAYS.
YOU SAID YES. YOU KEEP SAYING YES. THIS IS GOOD.
THE BODY KNOWS HIM. TRUST THE BODY.

And a new one, added in the second month, in her own hand:

YOU ARE HOME. THIS IS THE LIFE YOU BUILT.

She came into the kitchen one March morning with the ring already on and three pages of writing in her notebook, because she had been up since before him, in a long window, working.

"I wrote three pages this morning," she said.

He looked up from his sketchbook. "Long window?"

"Twenty minutes, maybe more. I think it was the weather. Something about cold mornings extends the window. I've noticed this before but never written it down until now."

"Write it down now. So future-you can test it."

"You knew I was already doing that," she said, writing.

"Yes."

"Then why did you say it?"

"I like watching you write."

She looked up at him, and a beat passed between them that came not from information but from recognition — the body saying *I know this*.

"My notes say I've been married to you for three months."

"Yes."

"Was it a good three months?"

"Yes."

"Tell me one thing from it."

He thought. "You decided last month that you wanted to paint. You bought a Kalamkari kit from Charminar. You've been working on something, but you won't let me see it until it's done."

She looked across the room. A covered canvas stood against the far wall, draped in an old cotton sheet.

"I'm painting something?"

"You've been at it for three weeks. You know when you're working on it because your hands have colour on them when you discover them in the morning."

She looked at her hands. There — faint, at the edge of her right thumb — a crescent of yellow ochre.

She crossed to the canvas. "Can I look at it?"

"It's yours. You can look at whatever you want."

She lifted the cloth.

A painting. Kalamkari in style, but her own dialect of it: the lake, the boardwalk railing, two figures with their backs to the viewer, looking at the water. And around the border, where the tradition put its flowering vines, she had painted Post-its — small blue squares, dozens of them, each containing a single word too small to read from across a room. You would have to lean in. The painting required you to lean in. That was the painting.

She stood in front of it. Her breath went out of her once, slowly.

"I made this?"

"You did."

She put her palm in the air just in front of the canvas — not touching the paint, feeling the warmth of the thing the way you hold a hand over something alive.

"The hand remembers the shape of it," she said.

"Yes," he said, barely above the lake.

"Even if I didn't know what I was making, my hand knew."

She came back to the table and slid him the pen.

"Write this for me. 'The hand makes things the mind cannot hold. Trust the hand.'"

He wrote it. Blue note, pressed to the page. Outside, Hyderabad in March: the last of the winter light, the first of the summer heat building at the sky's edges, the azaan from the Charminar direction, the auto horns, the crow on the balcony rail conducting its morning audit.

---

His mother came from Warangal for a week that spring — Savitri, fifties, a woman who moved through any room as if she had always lived in it. Aditya had told her everything before the wedding. Her reply had entered family legend by way of Lakshmi, who repeated it at intervals with reverence:

*Good. She'll always be happy to meet me.*

It was true. Priya liked her mother-in-law every time. "You've met her four times," Lakshmi told her once. "You like her every time."

"That seems efficient," Priya said.

## Chapter Thirty-Eight — The Fever

In the second year of the marriage, Aditya got dengue, and the system met its hardest test, and the test was not the one anybody expected.

The fever itself was ordinary misery — a week in bed, platelets counted like rupees in a bad month, Savitri arriving from Warangal with a thermos of something bitter and total command of the household. The problem was the boardwalk. For the first time since the three weeks — since the break, the empty steps, the photograph with the blue note — Aditya could not come to the lake.

He understood what that meant before the first morning ended. Somewhere across the city, a woman was standing at a railing checking an inside cover that said COMES EVERY MORNING, and the steps were empty, and a red note was being written. Her grief without an object. The space where something was. He lay in bed at 6:47 with his skin on fire and his chest worse, because he, unlike her, could remember the last time the steps were empty, and he knew exactly which muscle of hers would be hurting and that she would not know why.

"Call Kiran," he told Meera, who had appointed herself communications officer between the sickroom and the world. "He has to go. Every morning. It can't just be a note — the note says I come. If the note changes, the entry gets weaker. Someone has to *be* the morning."

So Kiran went to the lake.

He stood at the railing at 6:47 in his office clothes, holding a cup from Raju Bhaiya's cart, and when his sister came up the steps and consulted her notebook and looked at him with polite puzzlement, he said the sentence Aditya had dictated by phone through a 103-degree haze, and which Kiran had written on his own hand like a schoolboy:

"I'm Kiran. Your brother. Aditya sent me. He's sick — not badly, not dangerously — and he can't come for a few days, and he didn't want the steps to be empty. He said: tell her the morning still has someone in it."

Priya read her notes. Read his face. And then she did the thing that Kiran reported back to the sickroom that evening, and which made Aditya turn his face to the wall for a while:

She took out her pen, opened to the inside cover, and amended the constitution. Beneath ADITYA SHARMA. HUSBAND. COMES EVERY MORNING, she wrote:

WHEN HE CAN'T COME, HE SENDS THE MORNING ANYWAY.

Five mornings, Kiran stood at the railing. They talked — or rather, they had the same three conversations many times, and Kiran discovered what Aditya had discovered years before him: that the repetition was not the tax on talking to her but the revelation of it. By the third morning he had stopped varying his answers for his own entertainment and started listening to how her questions changed while his answers stayed still. *Is he really all right* on the first morning. *Does he have someone with him* on the second. And on the fourth morning, a question that arrived from somewhere below the notes, from the deep file system, in the small voice of the brackish hour:

"When someone is away — does missing them count, if you can't remember them?"

Kiran held his cup and gave it the time it deserved.

"You tell me," he said. "You're the expert."

She looked at the lake. Her thumb ran along the railing — that old path, the one her thumb had been keeping for years.

"I think it counts double," she said at last. "Other people miss with their minds. The mind exaggerates. The body doesn't exaggerate." She put her hand flat on her sternum, the auditor's gesture. "Something in here is set to him, and it can tell he's not where he should be, and it doesn't know any stories about it. It just aches accurately."

"Aches accurately," Kiran repeated.

"Put it in your phone," she said, "you'll forget it," and then laughed at her own joke half a beat before the reset took it, so that she surfaced from the page-turn already laughing, holding a joke she no longer had, and laughed harder at the strangeness of that, and Kiran stood there at the railing laughing with his sister at six in the morning over a joke that existed now only in his keeping — one more thing he carried for her, the lightest one yet.

On the sixth morning Aditya came back — pale, seven kilos down, against two doctors' advice and under Savitri's armed escort, who waited in the car. He climbed the steps slowly. Loose-stone spot, step left, weight forward, slower than ever before.

Priya turned before he reached the railing. Notebook unconsulted.

"You're the one," she said. "The one the ache is set to."

"Yes."

She studied him — the pallor, the careful walk, the week written all over him.

"You've been sick. And you came anyway."

"I missed the mornings."

She stepped close and put her hand flat on his chest, over the sternum, checking the calibration of something. Whatever she found there, it balanced.

"It counts double," she said, which made no sense to him at all and total sense to her brother, watching from the cart with the third cup, and she didn't explain, and Kiran never told him, and so there was, at last, one small thing in the marriage that she and Kiran carried together and Aditya did not know — a single grain of asymmetry, lodged in the system like a pearl's first irritant, except that what accreted around this one, over the years, was only ever the two of them smiling at each other across a railing for no reason Aditya could find in any notebook.

## Chapter Thirty-Nine — The Memory Series

The gallery in Banjara Hills hung eight paintings that autumn under a single printed title card: THE MEMORY SERIES — PRIYA SHARMA.

The boardwalk at dawn. The fort. The kitchen window with its brass vessels. The Post-it wall, abstracted into colour-field. The lake. The ring on its railing-shaped holder. Two figures at a railing, their backs to the viewer, the city before them.

And at the centre, the new one: a blue ground, and on it, rendered in the Kalamkari style — intricate, painstaking, bamboo-pen line over vegetable dye — a single Post-it note, enlarged to fill the canvas. The text small. Too small to read from most distances.

You had to lean in.

EVERY MORNING IS THE FIRST MORNING. THIS IS THE ONLY MORNING. IT IS ENOUGH.

The opening drew the Hyderabad art crowd — the Kalamkari revival people, the design community, two critics, a journalist who was not Arnav. Priya moved through it resetting and recalibrating: notebook, room, person in front of her, present. Fully present. The artist, here, now, with whoever stood before her, giving each of them the undivided attention that was her condition's strange tax refund.

A young woman — art student, by the tote bag and the hunger — leaned toward Meera.

"Is she—"

"Yes."

"How does she — how does she paint without being able to remember the work?"

"The same way she does everything," Meera said. "The hand knows. She just has to start."

The student looked at the central canvas, crossed to it, leaned in, read the small text. She straightened up with her face doing the thing that art does when it lands — and then she looked across the room at Priya, mid-conversation, resetting, recalibrating, present, and saw it: the living version of the text on the canvas.

All eight paintings sold. Aditya bought the boardwalk one before anyone else could and hung it in the studio beside the framed poster, and clients began to ask about it, and he learned to say "my wife's work" with a fullness that required no performance, and more than one client looked at his face while he said it and bought whatever he was selling.

On the gallery steps afterwards, in the Banjara Hills evening, she read her notes.

"My notes say I had an art opening today."

"All eight paintings sold."

"My hands made something people wanted."

"Desperately."

"Which one did they want most?"

"The one with the Post-it note."

"What did it say?"

He recited it — he had read it many times, and would read it many more, because it hung now in a stranger's house saying its one true thing to people neither of them would ever meet:

"'Every morning is the first morning. This is the only morning. It is enough.'"

She took it in.

"Did I know I was describing my own life?"

"I think your hand did."

She leaned into his shoulder. His arm came around her — the automatic, practised geometry of two people whose bodies had built a map. The city lit up below the steps.

"I want to go to the boardwalk tomorrow," she said.

"We go every day."

"Then it's already in my notes somewhere."

"It's in your notes as 'THE THING TO DO FIRST.'"

"Good," she said. "I'll discover it tomorrow and be glad."

## Chapter Forty — Eleven Students

The Saturday class had begun as Meera's idea, the way most structural changes in Aditya's life had begun as someone else's idea that he executed beautifully and then took mild credit for.

"The gallery keeps calling," she had said. "People don't just want the paintings, they want the method. Let her teach."

"She can't—" he had started, and stopped, because every sentence in his life that had begun *she can't* had eventually been dismantled by the woman herself, and he had learned to abandon them early and save the demolition costs.

The format took three weeks to find. Lecture was impossible — a thread of argument needs a speaker who can hold the thread. Demonstration, it turned out, needed no thread at all. Priya sat at the head of the long studio table with a stretched cotton panel and her bamboo pens, and worked, and talked while she worked, and the talk reset every seven seconds and the work never did. The eleven students — they had been six, then nine, then eleven, with a waiting list — arranged themselves around the table and learned kalamkari from a teacher who could not remember their names and noticed, every few minutes, freshly and aloud, the exact thing each of them most needed noticed.

"Your line is afraid of the corner," she told a retired bank officer, three separate times in one morning, each time as a brand-new discovery, and by the third time he stopped being afraid of the corner, because a thing said three times by a woman with no capacity for rehearsed criticism is simply, three times, true.

"How do you plan the whole tree," asked the art student — the one from the gallery opening, who had leaned in and read the canvas and signed up that night, "if you can't hold the whole tree in your head?"

Priya kept the pen moving. The branch she was drawing reached the place it was going, and turned, and became birds.

"I don't plan it. I make this stroke well. Then I find out what I've been making." She reset; surfaced; looked down at the panel with her usual collegial surprise; kept going from exactly where the hand was. "See — there. I just found out again. It's a tree. Good news."

The class laughed. She looked up, pleased, not knowing what the joke had been, knowing only that there was warmth in the room and she was somewhere near the middle of it.

Aditya watched from the back on the Saturdays he could, and what he watched was the students' faces in the moment they understood — each of them arriving at it on their own schedule, somewhere around the fourth week — that they had enrolled to learn a craft and were being taught, on the side, for no extra fee, an entire metaphysics: *make this stroke well, then find out what you've been making.* He watched the retired bank officer write it on the back page of his sketchbook, furtively, like a man pocketing a seed from a botanical garden.

The students said she was the only teacher who made them feel like the not-knowing was part of the work. They put it on the feedback forms. Priya read the forms each term as if for the first time, which it was, and each term she said the same thing, freshly:

"Of course it is. It's the biggest part."

## Interlude — Kiran, at the Railing

He came on the mornings he needed it, which was more mornings than he told anyone.

It had taken him six months of marriage-watching to admit what the boardwalk was for. He had told himself surveillance first — old habits, the brother's audit — and then exercise, and then company, and then he had stopped telling himself things, which was, his mother observed privately, the single largest development in her son since the diagnosis.

The truth was simpler and he arrived at it standing at the railing with the third cup Raju Bhaiya had begun pouring unasked: he came because it was the only place in his life where the question that had run him for three years finally went quiet.

The question had moved in the night of the hospital corridor, and it had many costumes but one body: *what will happen to her?* It had run under his code and his promotions and his interrogations of strangers; it had run so long it had stopped sounding like a question and started sounding like himself. He was twenty-four when it moved in. He could not remember his own interior weather before it, the way you can't remember a room before the furniture.

And then a designer with a Delhi accent had walked into the question and begun, morning by morning, dismantling its premise — not by answering it, but by demonstrating it had been wrongly framed. *What will happen to her* assumed she was a thing happenings happened to. Three years of evidence had been sitting in plain sight on a colour-coded wall: she was the one doing the happening. She had built the route, run the tests, chosen the man, written the constitution. The family had not been protecting a vulnerable woman. They had been staffing a sovereign one.

He drank his chai. Down the railing, his sister was showing his brother-in-law something in her notebook, and laughing, and the lake was doing its slow gold ignition for the ten-thousandth morning of its tenure.

"You didn't stop coming," Raju Bhaiya had said. Kiran had thought about that for weeks. His father had stood at this railing wanting an answer, and the lake had declined, and he had stopped coming, and eventually he had stopped coming to all of it. Kiran had wanted an answer too. The difference — he could see it now, in the cold clear December light — was that somewhere along the way he had traded the want. The lake didn't give answers. It gave mornings. You could stand at the railing demanding the first until you starved, or you could take delivery of the second, daily, the way his sister did, the way she had been trying to teach them all along without a single lecture, by the sheer patient spectacle of her joy.

He finished the cup and rinsed it at the cart, over Raju Bhaiya's objections, the way he did now. Then he walked down the boardwalk to where the two of them stood, and took his place on Priya's right — the right was his; the left was Aditya's; the geometry had absorbed him too — and his sister consulted her notebook to confirm what her body had already concluded from his footsteps, and gave him her whole face.

"Bhaiya. My notes say you come on Thursdays."

"It's Thursday," he said.

"Then everything," she said, with the satisfaction of an auditor closing a clean ledger, "is exactly where it should be."

## Chapter Forty-One — The Third Cup

On a December morning two years married, there were three figures on the boardwalk, and Raju Bhaiya's cart carried a third cup without being asked.

Kiran had been coming for six months. Not every day. Often enough. He stood slightly apart, hands in his pockets, looking at the lake with the expression of a man who has finally understood something and is renegotiating his relationship with the years he spent not understanding it.

"You look like your father today," Raju Bhaiya said, setting down the third cup.

Kiran looked at him.

"He used to stand exactly like that," the old man said, in Telugu, not unkindly. "Hands in pockets. Looking at the water like it owed him an answer."

"Did it give him one?"

"I don't know. He stopped coming." A pause. "You didn't."

Raju Bhaiya returned to his cart. Aditya came to stand beside Kiran, and for a while the two men watched the water in the way of men who have business to conduct and are letting the lake chair the meeting.

"I owe you an apology," Kiran said.

"You don't."

"I told you to stay away from her."

"You were right to protect her. I would have said the same thing."

"I was afraid she couldn't have a life." He paused; his jaw worked; the admission cost him, and the cost was not pride. It was grief — the grief of a man realising how long he had carried a fear that turned out to be wrong. "I thought this condition, this life — I thought it meant she couldn't have the full thing. Marriage. Partnership. Someone who stays."

He looked down the railing at his sister, a few feet away, reading her notebook, her face making the morning's small recalibration.

"I was wrong about what the full thing was."

Aditya followed his gaze. "She taught me that too."

Priya reset, opened her notebook, looked up — found her brother, found her husband. Smiled.

"Why does my notebook say I have two men on the boardwalk this morning?"

"I'm the taller one," Kiran said, recovering, voice level. "The better-looking one."

"Debatable," Aditya said, without pause.

"I'll put that in my notes." She was already writing. "'KIRAN THINKS HE'S THE BETTER-LOOKING ONE. CHECK THIS CLAIM.'"

She showed the note to Kiran. He read it. His mouth twitched, and his eyes did something complicated that he managed not to perform, and he took a long sip of chai to give the something somewhere to go.

---

In her mother's kitchen later that week — Lakshmi cooked for four most mornings now; the table had simply grown — Priya sat with her painting materials spread among the breakfast things in perfect domestic carelessness, sketching the window light on the brass vessels.

"You have paint on your notebook," Lakshmi said.

"I have paint on everything. My hands have a life of their own."

"This is apparently a family trait."

"You paint?"

"Your grandmother. She embroidered. She said her hands thought in thread."

Priya looked at her own hands. "My hands think in colour."

"And in notes."

"And in notes."

Lakshmi set the plate down — idli, and the coconut chutney Priya had been eating at this table since childhood, the taste that lived below language, in the place where memory and body are the same thing.

Priya ate. Her eyes closed once.

"This tastes like home," she said, eyes still closed.

"It should," her mother said, not stopping what she was doing, because some sacraments are best administered without ceremony.

## Interlude — What the Mother Keeps Now

There is a new note on the inside of Lakshmi's cupboard door.

The old one is still there — SHE IS NOT GONE. SHE IS HERE MORE THAN ANYONE. YOUR JOB IS TO MAKE HERE WORTH IT — softened at the corners now, the ink gone the brown of old letters. She has never taken it down and never will; you do not demolish the house you survived a war in. But beside it, written the week after the wedding, there is a second one, and the second one is the whole of what the years amount to, as far as she is concerned:

JOB DONE. NEW JOB: ENJOY HER.

She is sixty now, more silver than not. She cooks for four most mornings and for nine on Sundays, when the students sometimes come, and the kitchen has reached the state every kitchen aspires to, where the cooking does itself and the cook is free to watch the table.

This is what she watches:

Her daughter, reading the wall of her old bedroom on visits, left to right, liturgically, adding notes to a museum she no longer lives in but still curates. Her son, who interrogates no one now, whose jaw unlocked somewhere in his thirtieth year and never relocked, standing at a lake railing on Thursdays holding chai like a man holding a truce. Her son-in-law, who arrived as a stranger with the wrong flowers — no; with exactly the right flowers — and who has learned the family's one language so thoroughly that he now dreams, he admits, in Post-its. Her own mother's embroidery, reframed, hung over the painting her granddaughter made of a wall of blue squares — the hands of the family talking to each other across four generations in their shared dialect of thread and ink and dye, the dialect of hands, the only tongue in the house that has never once needed a reminder note.

People still say it to her, at weddings and funerals, the relatives with their theories retired now into wistfulness: *Such a hard life she has. She won't remember any of this tomorrow.*

Lakshmi has a reply now. She spent three years building systems and another three building this sentence, and she delivers it the way she delivers breakfast — without ceremony, and it feeds people more than they expect:

"None of us remember most of our days," she says. "Think — can you tell me anything about the fourteenth of last month? Gone. Almost all of it goes; we are all forgetting machines; she is only honest about it. The difference is that my daughter has never once lost the *taste* of a day. She drinks every one of them whole, while the rest of us are checking our phones." A pause; the chai poured; the rejoinder landed gently on its feet. "I stopped pitying her years ago. Some mornings, at the lake, watching her meet the water brand new — God forgive me, I have envied her."

## Chapter Forty-Two — What the Lake Is Doing

The third August of their marriage came in wet and undecided, the sky piled with cloud that would neither rain nor leave, the lake the colour of pewter at rest.

Priya walked the railing in the generous early window, her free hand trailing along the bars, each one a separate note in a melody she played by touch. She stopped. Looked at the water. Opened her notebook to the inside cover — two and a half years of evolution in the handwriting, the essential entry unchanged, only more decorated, more certain at its edges, bordered now with tiny Kalamkari vines she had inked in over the months.

She read it. Looked up.

The lake. The dawn. The railing.

Something in her face had gone past peace into the country beyond it — acceptance made so complete it stops being a feeling and becomes a fact.

"Here I am," she said. To herself. To the lake.

Footsteps on the stone. The particular weight of them, the step-over at the third stair where a stone used to be loose, years ago now. She didn't count. She didn't have to count anymore. The body had built a map that ran below counting, below knowing. The body simply recognised.

He sat beside her on the ledge. His shoulder against hers. The geometry.

"The lake is very quiet this morning," she said.

"The monsoon makes it think before it moves."

"My notes say I've been married almost three years."

"Four months to the anniversary."

"Do we come here every morning?"

"Every day you're in the city. Yes."

She considered this. "My feet know the railing."

"Every stone of it."

"Tell me one thing I wouldn't know."

He looked at the water and chose from the store he kept for exactly this question, which she asked most mornings, and which he had come to understand was not small talk but governance: the daily briefing of a head of state by the keeper of her archives.

"You've started teaching. A class at the studio — Kalamkari for beginners. Saturday mornings. You have eleven students."

She looked at her hands. "My hands teach?"

"Your hands demonstrate. You talk while you work. Your students say you're the only teacher who makes them feel like the not-knowing is part of the work."

"Because it is."

"Yes."

Raju Bhaiya arrived with the two cups, set them down with the economy of long practice and the small bow of one craftsman to another, and went back to his cart.

The cloud at the horizon shifted. A line of gold broke through, low — the specific colour of a sun that has had to fight its way to the announcement — and the lake went from pewter to bronze, and then, as the light widened, to the particular amber that belongs to that city and no other.

"There," she said, watching it.

"Yes."

"It does this every morning?"

"Different every morning. Same surprise."

She watched the light move across the water for a while.

"Do you know what I think the lake is doing?" she said.

"What?"

"Beginning. Every morning. The same lake, the same water, the same shore — but the light is new every time. And the light is the whole thing. Without the new light, it's just water." A pause. "Maybe that's what I am. The same water, every morning. And you're the new light."

He looked at the lake. He looked at her.

"That's going in your notebook."

"Already there," she said, writing.

I AM THE LAKE. YOU ARE THE NEW LIGHT.

She pressed the page flat. Blue ink. Her handwriting. Both of their words.

---

"Do you ever wish—" she began, and the page turned mid-sentence, and she surfaced, and found the thread again through the notebook, through his face, through the body's deep file system: anniversary, year, him. "Do you ever wish I was different? That I could remember?"

He held his cup in both hands and didn't answer immediately — not constructing an answer; checking what was true. She had taught him that distinction without ever knowing she was teaching.

"Sometimes I want you to remember something we had," he said. "A specific morning. A specific conversation. I think: I want to say 'remember when' and have you remember. But then I think — the reason you are the way you are, in any given moment, is exactly this. You're not somewhere else. Not in yesterday's argument. Not in next week's worry. You're completely here." He looked at the lake. "Most people spend their whole lives trying to be present and can't manage it. You have no choice. And it's the thing I love most about being near you."

She took it in.

"That's a very kind way to describe a difficult thing."

"It's true."

"Write it down anyway. Not for me — for you." She slid the pad across. "Write: 'I love being near her because she is always here.' So you remember too. Not just me."

He took a blue Post-it from her notebook and wrote it and folded it into his wallet, where it joined the others — the small wall he carried now, three notes, four, growing at the pace of years.

"There," he said. "My Post-it wall."

"How many do you have?"

"That's the fourth."

"Start small," she said.

## Chapter Forty-Three — The Long Window

The longest window of Priya's recorded life arrived on an ordinary Tuesday in her third married year, and lasted, by the clock Aditya kept with shaking hands, twenty-six minutes.

It was the cold, probably — the sharpest December morning in a decade, the boardwalk almost empty, their breath standing in the air. Or it was nothing, no cause at all, the brain's weather producing one anomalous day of summer. The neurologists had warned them years ago, gently, not to read hope into the long windows: the corridor was gone; the windows were eddies, not repairs.

She felt it herself around the fourth minute. He watched her notice — watched her go still, attentive to her own interior, like a woman in a house at night who realises a clock she has lived with has stopped ticking.

"Aditya."

"Yes."

"It's not turning."

"I know. I'm counting."

"Don't count." She took his hand. "Walk with me. I want to spend it, not measure it."

They walked the full length of the boardwalk, once, slowly — past Raju Bhaiya, who took one look and turned the cart's business over to his nephew and stood watching them go with his towel motionless on his shoulder; past the benches and the parliament of newspapers; past the place where a journalist had once waited and a stone had once been loose. She talked the whole way. Not urgently — that was what undid him most, that she didn't spend the miracle on urgency. She talked the way anyone talks on a morning walk with their husband: the light on the water, a student's progress, whether the biscuit ration should be revisited, an idea for a painting — "a wall of blue notes, but seen from behind, from inside the wall, the writing backwards, the way I must look to the notes" — and he said very little, and held her hand, and performed for twenty-six minutes the hardest discipline of his life, which was to treat the miracle as ordinary because she had asked him to.

At the far end she stopped at the railing and looked back down the full length of what they'd walked.

"I can see all of it," she said. "The whole walk. It's still here." She touched her own sternum, lightly, taking an inventory. "So this is what you carry. All the time. Everything, still attached to everything."

"Yes."

"It's heavy."

"Sometimes."

She nodded slowly, looking down the boardwalk, the whole unbroken morning of it lying there behind her like a sentence she could read end to end.

"It's beautiful," she said. "And I don't envy it."

He looked at her.

"I thought I would," she said. "I used to think — in the long windows, early on, I'd panic, I'd try to write everything down before it went, I'd grieve the closing while it was still open. This is the first one I've just lived in." She turned from the boardwalk to the lake. "It's a beautiful room, the long room. But the small room is mine. Everything I love comes to find me there. I've checked."

She came back into his arms and stood there, and somewhere around the twenty-sixth minute he felt it return — the small tidal pause in her breathing, the page preparing to turn — and she felt it too, and looked up at him, and what she said came out quick, but not in fear; in the tone of a woman calling something cheerful through a closing door:

"Thank you for the walk. I'll see you in seven seconds."

The page turned.

She surfaced. Lake, railing, ring, the man holding her, her notebook between them. She read his face — wet-eyed, smiling, undefended — and then the inside cover, and then his face again.

"Did something good just happen?" she asked. "You look like something good just happened."

"We went for a walk," he said.

"Was it nice?"

"It was the nicest walk of my life."

She considered him for a moment. Then she opened the notebook to a fresh page and put the pen in his hand.

"Write it down," she said. "You're the one who has to remember it."

## Chapter Forty-Four — Four Seasons at the Railing

A year at the railing, kept by the one who keeps things:

In winter the lake is glass and the Buddha stands so sharp against the cold blue that Priya, three mornings out of seven, says he looks newly washed, and is right every time. Her breath makes clouds. The purple shawl comes out. Raju Bhaiya's chai steams like a small local industry, and the long windows come more often, riding the cold, and Aditya has learned not to gather himself when one opens — to spend it, not measure it, walking.

In summer the city burns and the dawn is the only mercy, and they come at six sharp to take delivery of it. The lake goes low and bronze. The tangedu blazes all down the southern shore, and every third morning the wind brings it, and every third morning she discovers the smell for the first time and tells him some things are better fresh, and the sentence has stopped being a heartbreak and become a liturgy, the way all true sentences do if you keep them long enough.

In the monsoon the boardwalk empties and they have the rain to themselves. The lake erupts in its thousand simultaneous circles, every drop a small event. Her notebook lives in its oilcloth sleeve; the notes survive; the system was built for weather, all kinds. She lifts her face to the first rain of every shower with an astonishment that the years cannot dull because the years, for her, do not accumulate — and he has understood at last that he is not watching a deficit when she does this. He is watching the thing every poet who ever wrote about rain was trying to claw back. She never lost it. It is the one inheritance her condition refuses to spend.

And in October, when the heat finally breaks and the sky turns the amber that belongs to that city and no other, they stand at the railing in the year's best light, and the inside cover of a worn notebook says what it has said, in slowly evolving handwriting, through every season for years:

You are loved. The evidence is beside you.

She reads it most mornings. Most mornings, she doesn't need to. The evidence has learned to arrive before the note does — in the footsteps, in the chai's temperature, in the geometry, in the warmth before knowledge.

The seasons turn. The lake holds nothing and reflects everything.

They keep choosing to come back.

## Chapter Forty-Five — The Paper Boat

The notepad stayed in Arnav's pocket.

He had come back to the boardwalk one April morning — not hunting; passing; the story long dead, filed, two hundred thousand shares decayed into the internet's compost — and he saw them at the railing from the path below. The two figures. The chai. The morning.

He reached for the notepad out of professional reflex. Held it. And then his hand released it back into his jacket, unopened, because he was watching Priya say something and Aditya laugh — head back, brief, the way you laugh at something small and perfect — and he understood that he had already written everything he was entitled to, and that the rest of it belonged to the two people at the railing and to the lake, in roughly equal shares.

He turned and walked the other way.

Some things don't need to be written down.

---

The full morning came on. The sun cleared the buildings; the gold burned off into October blue; the Buddha stood vivid against it. The boardwalk filled with its daytime self — the walkers, the newspaper man, the children below the embankment arguing about something that didn't matter and mattered enormously.

Priya stood at the railing with her notebook open to a fresh page, writing in a long window, the hand moving steadily. Aditya sat beside her with his sketchbook, drawing what he always drew: the city and the lake and her in it, the figure at the railing who is always exactly here.

She finished. Read it back once.

Then she tore the page out — and did not hand it to him, and did not pin it anywhere. She folded it instead, her hands moving without instruction through a shape they knew: hull, prow, sail. A boat. Small, precise, the paper thinning at the folds.

She set it on the ledge and looked at it.

"The lake should have this one," she said.

She picked it up, leaned over the railing, and set it on the water.

The paper boat on Hussain Sagar: white, small, the ink of her handwriting just visible through the fold. The current took it — gently, the way the lake did everything — and drew it away from the stone, out toward the open water, toward the Buddha, toward the centre of things.

They watched it go.

Aditya didn't ask what it said. He had learned, over the years, which questions were his to ask, and this was not one of them. The boat moved. Got smaller. Passed through the zone where the morning light fragmented into gold on the surface, and went on, a white point, moving.

"I'll write it again," Priya said, watching it. "I always do."

Out on the water, the paper began its slow surrender — the ink releasing into the lake, the words becoming part of the water, the water becoming part of the city, the city becoming part of the morning that is always, for one woman at a railing, the first morning.

It is enough.

---

Her hand rested on the railing. The ring caught the light. Her fingers lay on the iron — familiar, easy, the body at home in its own weight.

She opened her notebook. Read the inside cover. Her lips moved slightly — not reading aloud; just the trace of the words entering her through all the ways she had learned to receive them.

She closed it. Looked at the lake. Smiled — the smile of someone who has just received the best news and is deciding what to do with it.

She already knew.

She would stand here. She would hold this. She would, in seven seconds, open the notebook and find it again, and it would be new, and she would smile, and she would stand here, and she would hold this.

The lake lay below her, copper and still and alive, the whole city reflected in it — the minarets, the glass towers, the skyline of a city that had been building itself for four hundred years and had not finished. A ripple moved out from somewhere, rings travelling outward, each one perfect and brief and gone. Then stillness again.

A surface that remembers nothing and reflects everything. Infinitely present. Infinitely new. Infinitely the same.

The city waking around it. The morning doing what the morning does.

And at the railing, two people, shoulders touching, with everything they need.

*

In the quiet, a single sound: paper, turning.
