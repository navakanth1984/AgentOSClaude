# Love Lasts for 7 Seconds — Character Bible

> A complete character reference for all principal characters. Based on documented case study of anterograde amnesia. All neurological and psychological details are clinically grounded.

---

## THE CONDITION — Understanding Priya's World

Before the characters, the science. Because everything flows from this.

**Anterograde amnesia** means the inability to form new declarative memories after the onset of illness. Priya can remember everything from before the viral encephalitis four years ago — her childhood, university, her PhD defence, her wedding, the smell of her mother's kitchen — with perfect, heartbreaking clarity. But anything that happens now, in the present, dissolves in approximately seven seconds. Sometimes faster. Occasionally a handful of seconds longer. But never more. Never enough.

The mechanism: **hippocampal degeneration**. The hippocampus — a small, curved structure deep in the medial temporal lobe — is the biological machinery that converts short-term experience into long-term memory. Priya's brain scans, taken at onset and again four years later, tell the story in two images. The first scan showed white areas: inflammation, active attack, the virus burning through tissue. The second scan, four years later, showed a dark void where her right temporal lobe had been — not injury but *absence*. The tissue didn't scar. It vanished.

**What she can still do:**
- Reason fluently and intelligently
- Feel emotions — including love, fear, joy, grief
- Speak, read, write with full literary competence
- Access all memories formed before the encephalitis
- Understand written notes as language (semantic processing)
- Recognize deeply familiar faces and spaces from before

**What she cannot do:**
- Form new declarative memories (episodic or semantic)
- Remember meeting someone, even seconds after the meeting ends
- Experience the feeling of *remembering* — reading a note feels like reading a stranger's diary, grammatically comprehensible but emotionally hollow of lived experience
- Know what day it is, what she ate, whether she has already cried today
- Accumulate a sense of time passing

**The crucial distinction that writers must understand:**
When Priya reads a note she wrote yesterday, she *understands* it semantically — she knows what the words mean, she can follow the logic, she recognizes her own handwriting. But she does not *relive* the moment. There is no flash of memory, no "oh, right, I remember." It is, always, like reading it for the first time. This is the difference between **semantic memory** (intact) and **episodic memory** (destroyed). Her notebooks are not a key that unlocks a locked room. They are a new room, built from scratch, every time she opens them.

## PRIYA SHARMA — Protagonist

**Age:** 34  
**Before:** Associate Professor of Literature, University of Hyderabad. Specialist in post-colonial fiction and narrative theory. She spent her career thinking about how stories construct identity.  
**Now:** A woman whose identity must be constructed by hand, every single morning.

### Physical Presence
Priya is not frail in the way people expect. She stands straight. She dresses carefully — each day's clothes laid out the night before, instructions written on an index card taped to her wardrobe mirror: *Tuesday. Blue salwar. The one that Nandita said makes you look like a professor.* She has learned that being well-dressed is armour. If she looks capable, people are less likely to treat her like she's broken.

Her hands are always slightly ink-stained — the red pen bleeds if you press too hard, and she always presses too hard.

### The Notebook System — Her External Hippocampus

Priya carries **three notebooks** at all times. This is non-negotiable. If she has to choose between her phone and her notebooks, she takes the notebooks.

**Notebook 1 — The Current Log (cream cover, A5 size):**  
This is her working memory, externalized. She writes in real-time — not notes, but a continuous log of present experience. Not "had breakfast" but the actual timestamp and content: *09:14 — ate idli with sambar at the small round table. Nandita said something funny but I can't — (note to self: it's okay, you don't need to remember. You have this.)* The entries are in black ink for regular observations. **Critical details are in red — always red.** Red means: *read this before you do anything else. This is the thing that matters right now.* A red entry might say: *THIS MAN KABIR HAS BEEN HERE THREE TIMES THIS WEEK. YOU LIKE HIM. HE KNOWS ABOUT YOUR CONDITION. HE COMES BACK ANYWAY.*

The notebook fills at a rate that astonishes strangers. Three, sometimes four pages per hour of active life. The handwriting is meticulous — she was always meticulous — but the entries become increasingly urgent as the day wears on and the cream pages fill with the texture of a life being held together by ink.

**Notebook 2 — Social Records (green cover, B6 size):**  
This is her database of people. Every person she encounters regularly gets a page. The format is standard: Name. How we know each other. Physical description (so she can recognize them before they speak). What they mean to me (written after reading the other entries, so she knows what she *should* feel even when she feels nothing specific). Recent interactions — dated, summarized. At the bottom of each person's page: **KEY PHRASE.** The thing this person always says to her first. Nandita's key phrase: *It's okay, Priya. It's all okay.* Her mother's: *You ate today, yes? Show me your hands.* Kabir's — newly added, barely established — *You don't have to explain anything.*

**Notebook 3 — The Night Summary (dark blue cover, hardback):**  
This is the most important notebook. She writes in it every night before sleeping, no matter what — even if she is exhausted, even if she has cried for an hour, even if the day has been so fractured she doesn't know where to start. One page. Always one page. She has developed a format over four years of practice: *Date. Weather. One thing I ate. One person I spoke to. One moment that felt good. One moment that felt hard. The condition of my notebooks (all present? damaged?). A note to tomorrow-Priya.* The note to tomorrow-Priya is always the last line. It is sometimes one word (*breathe*) and sometimes a paragraph. It is the most intimate thing Priya has ever written because it is addressed to someone who is, functionally, a different person — a person she will never remember being.

**The Archive:**  
In Priya's bedroom, stacked in a corner, are **twelve cardboard boxes** of completed notebooks, each dated on the spine in her mother's handwriting. These boxes are the externalized form of her hippocampus. They are, as Priya wrote in an early night summary, *my memory and my life itself.* When the encephalitis first progressed and she understood what was happening — in those terrible first months before the notebook system fully developed — she had a recurring nightmare: the boxes burning. Not a nightmare about dying. A nightmare about *not existing.* The fire doesn't kill her in the dream. It erases her. There is a Priya-shaped hole in the world and no record that she ever filled it.

She has written instructions on every box, in red: *DO NOT MOVE WITHOUT ASKING ME. IF THESE ARE LOST I WILL NOT KNOW WHAT I AM.*

### Psychology — What Drives Her

**Surface motivation:** To get through the day without humiliating herself or frightening someone who loves her.

**Deep motivation:** To maintain what neuropsychologists call **autobiographical continuity** — the sense that the self persisting through time is the same self, connected by a thread of remembered experience. Priya knows, intellectually, that this thread is now external. She knows she has to *read* her identity rather than *feel* it. What she fights for, every single day, is the dignity of that constructed self — the insistence that a self built from notebooks is still a real self. Not a diminished one. A different kind of real.

**Her great fear:** Not the amnesia itself — she has accommodated the amnesia. Her great fear is that people will love the *idea* of her condition — the poignancy of it, the tragedy of it — rather than *her.* She has been pitied. She has been studied. She has been featured in a medical journal without being named. What she wants is to be known — actually known — the way you know someone you have watched for years.

**Her great secret:** Some days she is at peace. Not resigned — genuinely at peace. The present moment, when it is good, is completely good. There is no shadow of the past dragging on it, no anxiety about forgetting the future. A cup of tea on a cool morning is just a cup of tea. She has started to understand why the Buddha talked about impermanence — not because she went looking for philosophy, but because the philosophy found her.

### Her Voice
Literary. Precise but warm. When Priya writes in her notebooks she sounds like herself — a woman who spent years teaching students to read carefully, who believes language is a form of attention, who knows that how you say something is as important as what you say. She is not sentimental. She doesn't like clichés. She will cross out a word and write a better one even in a real-time log that no one else will ever read.

## KABIR MEHTA — Love Interest

**Age:** 38  
**Before:** Senior architect, London practice. Spent twelve years building other people's homes and living in a series of flats that never quite felt like his.  
**Now:** Returned to Hyderabad after his father's death, ostensibly to settle the estate, actually because he realized, standing at his father's grave, that he had no idea what he was doing with his life.

### Who He Is
Kabir is the kind of man who listens to the *texture* of a conversation, not just the content. He notices things — the way light changes in a room, the silence after someone almost says something. He became an architect because he believes that space shapes emotional life, that the rooms you inhabit form you. He came back to Hyderabad because the room of his London life had formed him into someone he didn't recognize.

He met Priya at his sister's dinner party — a gathering he almost didn't attend. He saw her writing in a small cream notebook before she looked up at him, and was struck by the complete absorption of it — a woman more interested in capturing the moment than performing within it.

### How He Meets the Condition
He didn't know about the amnesia when they first spoke. She was sharp, funny, incisive — made an observation about Nabokov that stopped him mid-breath. When she disappeared mid-conversation to write something, he thought she was a journalist. When Nandita explained, quietly, in the kitchen, he didn't react with pity or horror. He stood very still for a long moment and then said: *So every time she sees me, it's the first time?*

*Yes.*

*And she keeps — she keeps choosing to come back anyway?*

*She doesn't choose. She doesn't remember meeting you. But look at her notes about you.* Nandita showed him Priya's social records page. Under KABIR MEHTA, in red ink: *THIS ONE. COME BACK.*

He fell in love with the red ink before he fell in love with her.

### His Role in the Story
Kabir is the narrative engine: what does love mean when the other person cannot remember you? He is not a saviour. He doesn't fix her. He is a man learning to be loved differently — learning that love can be an act of daily re-introduction, that the emotion persisting in Priya's body and the meaning preserved in her notebooks constitute a form of knowing that is, in its way, as complete as any other.

**His recurring question:** *Does she love me, or does she love what her notes tell her about me?*  
**The answer the story gives:** *There is no difference.* Love was always partly constructed from memory, impression, narrative. His love for her is also constructed — from the hours he's spent watching her, reading her old notes when she offers, learning what she was before. They are both working from records. The only difference is that her records are written down.

### His Wound
He was with someone in London for six years — a relationship that ended because, as she put it, he was *never fully present.* He was always somewhere else — in his head, in the architecture, in the future. The bitter irony of falling in love with a woman who lives only in the present is not lost on him.

## NANDITA "NANDI" KRISHNAN — Best Friend / Primary Caregiver

**Age:** 33  
**Occupation:** ICU nurse, Niloufer Hospital  
**Connection:** Best friend since Class 7. Has known Priya for twenty years.

### Who She Is
Nandita is the person who held Priya's hand in the hospital when the diagnosis came and did not cry until she got to the parking lot. She is the person who spent three months researching every published case of anterograde amnesia, who found case reports from Japan, Canada, the UK, who drove two hours to consult a neuropsychologist in Bengaluru and came home with three pages of notes on cognitive prosthetics. She is the person who had the idea for the notebooks.

She is not a martyr. She has a life — a demanding job, complicated family, her own desires and exhaustions. She has cried in the parking lot at work more times than she can count. She has her own grief for the Priya who remembered, who called her at midnight to argue about a novel, who remembered the names of all her nephews and their birthdays. That Priya is not gone — but she is overlaid, now, by this Priya, who must be reminded of the nephews every time.

### Her Practice — "It's Okay"
Nandita has a phrase she says to Priya at moments of distress or disorientation. *It's okay.* Simple words. But she has learned — from nursing, from research, from four years of watching — that the words are not the thing. The thing is the tone, the steadiness, the warm eye contact, the physical presence of her hand on Priya's arm. Priya's emotional memory (implicit memory, limbic system, unaffected by hippocampal damage) registers safety even when her declarative memory cannot hold the experience. This is why Nandita smiles the same smile every time — not because she's performing comfort, but because the smile has been encoded by repetition into Priya's emotional landscape. Priya's body knows Nandita is safe before Priya's mind can read her notebook to confirm it.

This is the most sophisticated thing Nandita does, and she does it instinctively because she loved Priya before she knew the neuropsychology.

### Her Secret
She has a recurring fantasy of telling Priya something she could never have told her before the illness — something about a year in their twenties when Nandita was in love with someone Priya disapproved of, when there was a year of distance between them. She fantasizes about confessing it because Priya won't remember — the confession would dissolve in seven seconds. She has never done it. Not because she doesn't trust Priya. Because she respects Priya too much to use her condition as a confessional booth.

## DR. ADITI RANGAN — Neurologist

**Age:** 48  
**Institution:** Nizam's Institute of Medical Sciences, Hyderabad  
**Specialty:** Behavioural neurology and memory disorders

### Who She Is
Dr. Rangan has treated Priya for four years. She is the person who looked at the second brain scan — the one with the dark void — and had to explain what darkness meant. She is technically excellent and emotionally present, which is a combination rarer than it should be in medicine.

She does not soften the science. She has found, over two decades of treating memory patients, that the softening insults people's intelligence and robs them of the chance to build their lives around accurate understanding. When she told Priya, early in the illness, that recovery was unlikely, she told her directly and then sat with her in the silence for a long time.

### What She Carries
She has looked at Priya's second brain scan hundreds of times. She keeps a copy in her desk, not because she needs it clinically, but because she thinks about it. The dark void where the right temporal lobe was. She has treated patients with partial amnesia, with traumatic amnesia that improved, with dementia that progressed. Priya's case is unusual — stable in its damage, progressive only in small increments, with intact cognition and personality. What this means is that Priya will be Priya, in full, while being unable to accumulate a self in the usual sense. Dr. Rangan finds this the most philosophically startling case of her career. She has not told Priya this.

### Her Function in the Story
Dr. Rangan is the voice of medical reality — not cold, but honest. She explains the neuroscience in language Priya can use. She is also a counterweight to the story's romantic warmth: a reminder that there is no cure, no improvement, no arc toward wholeness in the conventional sense. Whatever Priya builds, she builds despite the science, not because it relents.

## LEELA SHARMA — Priya's Mother

**Age:** 62  
**Background:** Retired schoolteacher. Has been a widow for eight years.

### Who She Is Now
Leela Sharma was destroyed by her daughter's diagnosis. She spent the first year in a state of sustained, private grief that she hid from Priya because she could see — through the bewilderment of love — that Priya needed her present and steady, not crumbling.

She learned, over four years, to exist in the present alongside her daughter. Not by suppressing grief — by moving it to a different room. She grieves in the mornings, before Priya wakes, when she sits with her tea and her own memories of a daughter who used to call from the university at 7pm to complain about departmental politics. Then she goes in to Priya with her tea, and she is just Leela, just Amma, just the woman who checks Priya's hands to see if she has eaten.

### What She Has Learned
She has learned to let go of the accumulated weight of the relationship. When Priya wakes and doesn't remember what they talked about yesterday, Leela doesn't grieve the lost conversation. She has started to understand — not intellectually, but in her body — that the love doesn't require the memory to be real. Priya's face lights up when Leela enters the room, every time, as if seeing someone beloved. *She doesn't remember me,* Leela thought in the beginning. *But she recognizes me.* That distinction, once devastating, has become something else. It is the thing Leela holds onto on the hardest days.

### Her Role in the Story
Leela is the long view. She has been with Priya through the whole four years. She has seen more of the condition than anyone. She is, therefore, the character who can speak to what love looks like at the end of four years of this — not the romantic, newly-confronted version that Kabir is experiencing, but the worn-in, habitual, morning-and-evening kind. She loves Priya in the way that you love a person when the love has been tested so many times it has become structural — built into the walls, not hanging on them.

## RELATIONSHIP DYNAMICS

### Priya ↔ Kabir
The central relationship. Every scene between them operates on two timelines simultaneously: *his* timeline (cumulative, remembered, building toward something) and *her* timeline (present-only, notebook-mediated, emotionally real but episodically discontinuous). The writer must hold both. The scenes that feel most true are the ones where these two timelines create unexpected harmony rather than tragedy — where what she can't remember turns out to matter less than what she can feel.

### Priya ↔ Nandita  
The oldest relationship and the most complex. Nandita knows things about Priya that are locked in the past — before the illness, a complete version of the friendship. She is also the primary caretaker of the *present* Priya. There is sometimes a tension: the Priya in Nandita's memory is her friend. The Priya in front of her is her responsibility. The story's job is to show that this tension is not a problem to solve but a condition to live inside.

### Priya ↔ Leela
Pure love with grief running underneath like a river under ice. They don't talk about the illness much anymore. They talk about tea, and weather, and what to cook, and which of Leela's former students she ran into at the market. This is not avoidance. It is the form that accommodation takes.

### Priya ↔ Dr. Rangan
Patient and doctor who have, over four years, developed a relationship that cannot quite be called friendship but is something close. Dr. Rangan is the only person who speaks to Priya entirely without tenderness. Not coldly — with respect. As an equal who happens to have a condition.

## THE WORLD — Physical Details That Matter

**The house:** Priya and Leela share a house in Banjara Hills — a modest, well-maintained place that Priya has helped organize for navigability. Sticky notes are forbidden. (Priya learned early that sticky notes are a trap — they come unstuck, get lost, create a blizzard of decontextualized information.) Everything is labelled but permanently — engraved tags, painted letters. The kitchen drawer labels are in Priya's handwriting, chosen with care: *the spoon drawer*, *the sharp things*, *the things for chai.*

**The notebooks on the shelf:** In Priya's bedroom, next to her bed, is a small wooden shelf. This holds her current notebooks, three spare notebooks (cream, green, dark blue), three red pens, two black pens, a pencil (backup), and a laminated card that she reads every morning before she opens any notebook. The card says: *Your name is Priya. You have a condition called anterograde amnesia. This means you cannot form new memories. This is not your fault. Your notebooks are on the shelf. Read the blue one first. Then the cream one. Then check: do you know where Nandita is? Do you know what day it is? The answers are in the notebooks. It's okay. You have everything you need.*

**The cardboard boxes:** Twelve boxes, stacked in the corner of Priya's room. Her mother writes the dates on them — it has become, without either of them naming it, Leela's contribution to the notebook system. She writes the dates in a careful, rounded hand that Priya has always associated with report cards, with gold stars, with the particular warmth of being known by someone who wants you to do well.
