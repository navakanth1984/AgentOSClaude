### A Novel

## Part One: The Morning Protocol

---

### Chapter One — What Priya Reads Before She Is Priya

The laminated card is always the first thing.

She has read it so many times that she could, theoretically, recite it without looking — except that the point of reciting it without looking would be to prove she remembered it, and she cannot remember it, and so she reads it. Every morning. The words, always, landing fresh.

*Your name is Priya. You have a condition called anterograde amnesia. This means you cannot form new memories. This is not your fault.*

Not your fault. She chose that phrase herself, in the early days, when she was still angry. She wrote it on the card and laminated it at the shop near the hospital and thought: if future-me wakes up in the middle of the night and is frightened and reaches for this card in the dark, I want her to know immediately that she didn't cause this. The viral encephalitis caused this. The white areas on the brain scan that became dark void caused this. The hippocampal degeneration that the neurologist described to her — that she presumably received the explanation of and cannot remember receiving — caused this.

Not her fault.

*Your notebooks are on the shelf. Read the blue one first.*

She reaches for the blue one. Dark cover, hardback, slightly worn at the corners because she presses her thumb into the corner when she's thinking. She opens to the last written page and begins.

---

**Entry: 13 June 2026**

*Today was — it was a Tuesday that felt like a Monday, or that's what Nandita said, and I laughed, though I don't know why Tuesdays feel different from Mondays to people who remember all of them. The light this afternoon was very good. I sat by the window for a while.*

*Amma made mirchi bajji in the evening. This is important: I ate. I know I ask about this, so: I ate. The mirchi was slightly too spicy but I ate two of them anyway and Amma was pleased.*

*Kabir called. This is the important thing — the most important thing, read carefully:*

She always reads carefully. The instruction is redundant but she gave it to herself anyway because past-Priya knew that future-Priya would have a moment, reading about Kabir, when the name might not immediately be certain, when she might search the green notebook for the page on KABIR MEHTA before reading on. The instruction *read carefully* is permission to slow down. To stay.

*Kabir called at 7:14pm. He asked if he could come on Thursday. I said yes. Nandita thinks I should — she didn't finish the sentence, but she was smiling. I have written his page in the green notebook again. He has been here four times.*

*I think I like him. I can't feel what that liking felt like in the moment because the moment is gone. But I wrote: THIS MAN. COME BACK. So past-me liked him. And past-me is the only other witness I have.*

*Note to Thursday-Priya: he's coming. Read the green notebook. Page 7. Be yourself — you are very good at being yourself.*

*Goodnight.*

She closes the blue notebook. Sets it on her lap. Outside the window, the Hyderabad morning is doing its elaborate theatrical thing — birds conducting an argument in the gulmohar tree, autorickshaws beginning their distant brass section, someone three houses away cooking something with mustard seeds.

She reaches for the green notebook. Page 7.

---

**KABIR MEHTA**

*First met: 4 May 2026, Nandita's sister's house. Dinner party.*

*Physical description: tall, broad-shouldered, skin the colour of strong tea, reads as warm but not immediately loud about it. Hair going slightly at the temples. Architect. Works with his hands, you can tell — he has the habit of turning objects over when he's looking at them, examining the underside, the join.*

*How we know each other: he started talking to me about Nabokov. I was at a dinner party (see daily log 4 May) and he just — started. As if continuing a conversation already in progress. I found this either very comfortable or very presumptuous and apparently settled on comfortable.*

*What he means to me: he knows about the condition. Nandita told him at the party. He didn't leave. He came back on 9 May, and 17 May, and 23 May. Each time I have no memory of the previous visit but the notes say: he comes back. He asks to come back. He does not seem to need me to remember him to want to return.*

*I don't understand this entirely but I find it — I find it —*

*I find it like standing in the sun.*

*Recent interactions:*
*4 May: First meeting. He said: "I've never met anyone who takes notes like that." I was writing in the cream notebook. He was watching. I said: "I've never met anyone who doesn't find it alarming." He said: "Why would it be alarming? You're paying attention." (Note: this man. These are good instincts.)*

*9 May: He brought mangoes. We ate them on the veranda. He did not ask me if I remembered him. He said: "I'm Kabir. I was here two weeks ago. We talked about Nabokov." As if it were a completely ordinary thing, to re-introduce yourself to someone. I have notes about this in the daily log. The logs say I laughed.*

*17 May: He showed me photographs from his work — buildings in London, a library he designed in Edinburgh. I asked if he missed London. He was quiet for a long time and then said: "I missed the feeling of being somewhere I was supposed to be. But London wasn't it." I wrote this down immediately. He watched me write it and didn't say anything.*

*23 May: He brought a book — a collection of short stories, a writer I had taught. He said he'd found it in a second-hand shop. He said: "I don't know if you have it already. I bought it anyway." I checked: I don't have it. Or, I had it and can no longer confirm this. Either way, the gesture.*

*KEY PHRASE: "I don't have to explain anything." Wait — he says this to me. He says: "You don't have to explain anything." Meaning: he already knows. You can stop managing him.*

*IN RED: THIS ONE. COME BACK.*

---

Priya closes the green notebook. She has read the Kabir page before — how many times, she cannot say. Each reading is, to her, new. But the entries accumulate toward something that feels, even reading from the outside, like tenderness. Like a portrait painted one sitting at a time by someone who doesn't remember the previous sessions but who keeps choosing the same subject.

She reaches for the cream notebook — the current log. She opens to a blank page and writes the date and time.

*14 June 2026. 07:22.*

*Good morning. You are Priya. The card was right.*

### Chapter Two — The Architecture of an Hour

Kabir came on a Thursday.

He knew, by the fourth visit, the protocol. You knock. Leela Sharma or Nandita opens the door — one of them is always there, not hovering but available. You go in. Priya is at the table, reading. You don't rush to her. You let her finish the page, finish the entry, let her bring herself fully present first. Then you say your name.

"I'm Kabir," he said, on this fourth Thursday. "We've met. Several times. I was here on the twenty-third of May. We're — we're getting to know each other."

Priya looked up. Something in her face that wasn't recognition but was close to it — recognition's cousin, the response to a voice that the body has registered even when the mind hasn't filed it.

"The architect," she said.

"You have notes."

"I have excellent notes." She patted the green notebook. "You're the one who says *you don't have to explain anything.* Which is —" she paused. "Which is the correct thing to say to me, apparently. According to my notes. Which I trust."

"I'm glad your notes approve of me."

"They're cautiously optimistic." She said it with dry amusement, and he thought: that's the thing that didn't require any notes. That quality — the precision of her humour, the slight angle on everything. That was always there, every time. Some things, he had come to understand, you don't need a hippocampus for.

He sat across from her. Between them: tea, which Leela had appeared with at precisely the right moment, and the green notebook, open on the table between them like a referee.

"Read me the entry," he said. "From the first time."

"You want me to read you what I wrote about you?"

"I want to hear how you described me." He paused. "The first time we talked."

She found the entry. Read it aloud. *I've never met anyone who doesn't find it alarming.* He said: *Why would it be alarming? You're paying attention.*

"I said that?" He sounded slightly surprised at his own recorded self.

"You said that." She looked up. "Was it true?"

"I thought so then." He turned his cup in his hands — the architectural habit, she'd noted it. "Now I think — I think I was also just trying not to be someone who found it alarming. There's a difference."

"There is." She wrote something in the cream notebook. He didn't ask what. He had stopped asking, after the second visit. Whatever she was writing — it was for her, for the her who would exist later and need to know what this moment had been. He had learned to be comfortable in the knowledge that he was always, for her, being observed and recorded. It was, he thought, not unlike being loved.

"Can I ask you something?" he said.

"You're going to ask anyway."

"The notes about me —" he gestured at the green notebook. "Do they — when you read them, does it feel like memory? Like you're remembering the visits?"

She considered this with the seriousness she brought to everything. "No," she said. "I understand the notes. I know what the words mean. But there's no — the reading doesn't come with a feeling of having lived it. It's more like reading someone else's diary, except the handwriting is mine." She paused. "In the beginning, this was devastating. The gap between understanding and remembering. Now it's just — the way it is."

"But you feel something," he said. "When you read the notes about me. You're feeling something right now."

"I'm feeling something about you right now," she said carefully. "In this present moment. The past notes and this present feeling are separate. But they point in the same direction." She looked at him for a long moment. "I don't know what to make of that."

"Neither do I," he said.

And somehow — neither of them knowing the architecture of how they'd arrived at this — that was enough to continue.

### Chapter Three — What the Boxes Contain

On a Wednesday in June, Nandita watched Priya organize the twelfth box.

It was a ritual. Every few months, when a box filled, Priya spent an afternoon on the floor of her room, surrounded by completed notebooks, numbering them in sequence and packing them in order. The boxes held a careful record of years: each notebook dated on its first page, each sequence numbered so that any entry could be found by date.

Nandita sat in the doorway. Priya didn't know she was watching.

What Nandita was watching was this: Priya holding an old notebook — the number on the spine said 3, which meant it was from the first year — open to a random page. Reading it. Not with recognition. With the same careful attention she brought to anything written — the attention of a former literature professor encountering a text. Her eyes moved across the page, unhurried. She turned a page. Another. Then she closed it, held it for a moment with her hands flat against the cover, and placed it gently in the box.

"That one's from the first year," Nandita said.

Priya looked up. "You're watching me."

"For a while. Sorry."

"Don't apologize." Priya held the box. "What was I like? In the first year."

"The same," Nandita said immediately. Then: "No. Not the same. You were — the notes were more frantic. More cross-outs. More words that were crossed out and written in red. A lot of entries that said: *IMPORTANT — YOU ARE PRIYA SHARMA. YOU ARE NOT LOSING YOUR MIND. YOU HAVE A CONDITION.*" She paused. "You used to write it in capitals. Like you were shouting at yourself."

"Was I shouting at myself?"

"I think you were — I think you were trying to hold yourself together with very loud instructions." Nandita moved from the doorway and sat on the floor next to her, cross-legged. "You don't write in capitals much now."

"Because I believe it now," Priya said. "I think." She set the notebook in the box. "The boxes —" she touched the side of the cardboard. "My mother's handwriting on the spine." 

"She started doing that in October of the first year. You couldn't always read your own handwriting when you were distressed."

Priya traced the dates in Leela's rounded hand. October 2022. November 2022. The careful loops of a woman who spent thirty years teaching children to form their letters correctly.

"If these were lost," Priya said quietly. Not alarmed. Just stating the arithmetic of it. "If these were lost, I would lose four years. Not the feelings — the context. The names. The architecture of what I've built."

"I know."

"You would remember for me."

"I would," Nandita said. "But it wouldn't be the same. The boxes are yours. I can describe what happened — but I can't give you back your own observation of it. Your own words." She picked up notebook 3, which Priya had just placed in the box, and looked at the spine. "You have a voice in these. A very specific voice. When I read them it sounds like — like the best version of you. The you who notices everything."

"I can't lose them," Priya said. "It's written on the boxes. *If these are lost I will not know what I am.* I wrote that in — I must have written that in the beginning."

"You wrote it on all of them," Nandita said. "You taped a piece of paper to the inside lid of each box with instructions on what to do if the box is found. Where to bring it. Who to call."

Priya was quiet for a moment. Then: "That was very organized of me."

"You were always organized. That's the thing the illness couldn't touch." Nandita looked at her. "When the encephalitis was at its worst — when you were in the hospital and we didn't know the extent of the damage — you asked the nurse for a notepad. Before you knew what was wrong. Before anyone had explained what was happening to your memory. You asked for a notepad and you started writing."

"Because something was wrong and I needed to record it."

"Because you were still you." Nandita's voice did something complicated. She steadied it. "You were always going to figure out a way to stay you."

Priya looked at the twelve boxes stacked in the corner of her room — Leela's handwriting marching down the years, notebook 1 through whatever number she was on now. A life in cardboard.

"Kabir is coming Thursday," she said. Not as a change of subject. As a continuation of the same thought. *I am figuring out how to stay me. And somehow, that involves this.*

"I know," Nandita said. "Are you —" she paused. "How do you feel about it? Right now, in this moment?"

"Right now," Priya said carefully, "I feel like a woman who has built an entire external memory system out of nothing but determination and red ink, and who has therefore proven, empirically, that she can do extremely difficult things." She placed the last notebook in the box and closed the flaps. "I think I can manage a Thursday."

Nandita laughed — the specific, unguarded laugh that Priya's notebook described as *the one that sounds like she's surprised to be happy.* 

Priya didn't remember the description. But she smiled at the laugh anyway, by instinct, because some things the body holds long after the mind lets go.

### Chapter Four — First Time, Every Time

He asked her, on the fifth Thursday, what she felt when he left.

"I mean — after I'm gone," he said. "Do you — what is it like?"

"I don't know what it's like," she said. "I don't remember the after."

"But right now — thinking about me leaving —"

She looked at him steadily. The cream notebook was open on the table. "I don't want you to leave," she said. "That's a present-tense thing. I can tell you that clearly."

"And tomorrow, when you read the notes —"

"Tomorrow-Priya will read that she didn't want me to leave and she will — understand that. She will know it as information. Whether she will feel it —" Priya paused. "She will feel something about you when she reads about you. She always does. Past-me was always — present-me is always —" she stopped, slightly frustrated. "The tenses are a problem."

"They are," he agreed.

"The point is," she said, "the accumulation in the notebook moves in one direction. Toward warmth. The entries about you have become warmer. That's not something I planned. It's something that happened, across the visits, and I can see it in retrospect because the record shows it. The feelings I have in each present moment are apparently — consistent." She looked at him. "Whatever I feel for you, I feel it every time I'm with you. From scratch, without precedent, without the accumulated weight of every other visit. Every time is the first time. But the first time is always the same."

He was very still.

"That terrifies me," he said. "And I don't think it should."

"It should be both things," she said. "It's both things for me." She picked up the red pen. "Every time I read your page, there's a moment where I don't know yet — where the words haven't resolved into meaning — and I'm just looking at a name. *Kabir.* And for a fraction of a second I am a person meeting you for the first time, with no prior knowledge, no expectation." She clicked the pen. "And then I read, and I know, and —" 

She wrote something in the margin of the cream notebook.

She turned it so he could see. In red ink, slightly smudged because she pressed too hard:

*First time, every time.*

"It's not only a loss," she said. "That's what I want you to understand."

He looked at the red ink for a long time. Then he looked at her — the way he looked at buildings: the whole of it, the proportions, what it was and what it was reaching toward.

"Okay," he said.

"Okay?"

"I'll come back on Tuesday."

"Write it down," she said. "Write it down so I can write it down. I'll put it in red."

He took the pen she offered. On the margin of the page she'd turned toward him, next to *First time, every time,* he wrote: *Kabir. Tuesday. He'll be here.*

She looked at his handwriting next to hers on the page and wrote, below it: *Two kinds of memory. Same page.*

Then she closed the notebook carefully, the way you close something you intend to return to.

### Chapter Five — What Nightly Summaries Accumulate

The night summary is the last thing. Always.

Priya has a specific ritual. She makes chai — she knows how, the muscle memory of it is intact, the particular ordering of water and milk and cardamom and the way to hold the strainer. She takes the chai to her desk. She opens the dark blue notebook to the next blank page and she writes the date.

Then she sits for a moment before writing. This is the part she has no instructions for. This is the five minutes — sometimes ten — when she inhabits the day she cannot remember in the only way available to her: by reading back through the cream notebook's log and composing, from that reading, a summation. She is, essentially, a journalist writing an article about a day she didn't witness. The journalist happens to have excellent primary sources.

*14 June 2026.*

*The weather was something — the gulmohar tree was doing its noisy morning thing. I ate at 09:14 (idli and sambar, the small round table). I sat in the window. The morning light was — (I wrote: very good).*

*Kabir came in the afternoon.*

She pauses here. Every time she writes his name in the night summary — and she has written it now, she knows, many times, though the count is not knowable to her — she pauses. Because the name, in the daily log, is followed by entries that are dense and careful and in some places have small cross-outs where she found a more accurate word. The entries about Kabir are the most carefully written entries in the current log. She has noticed this herself. *You pay attention to what matters.* She wrote that once, to herself, as a note.

*He asked me what it was like when he left. I told him I didn't want him to leave. (Present tense. True when said.)*

*He stayed for two hours and forty-three minutes. My log says I didn't notice the time passing.*

*The log says: he asked to come back on Tuesday. He wrote it in the margin. His handwriting is —* 

She opens the cream notebook to check. Finds the margin note.

*His handwriting is architectural. Very precise, slightly slanted. It looks like a blueprint annotation.*

*I wrote: Two kinds of memory. Same page.*

She sits with this. Reads it again. *Two kinds of memory. Same page.* She doesn't remember writing it — she never does. But it sounds like her. It sounds like the version of herself she would most like to be.

*The condition of my notebooks: all present. Green, cream, blue. The archive boxes: twelve, all present, all with Amma's writing on the spine.*

*The thing that felt good today: the margin note. His handwriting next to mine.*

*The thing that felt hard today: explaining the tenses. That the tenses are a problem. That I can't speak in continuous time the way everyone else does.*

*Note to tomorrow-Priya:*

She writes this part last. Always. It is the hardest part because the person she is addressing is herself — a self she knows is coming but has never met. A self who will wake with this notebook in reach and read these words without any sense of having lived today, without knowing how the chai tasted, without carrying forward any of the texture of this evening. Tomorrow-Priya will be new. Tomorrow-Priya will have this note.

*Note to tomorrow-Priya:*

*He's coming on Tuesday. He wrote it in the margin in his own handwriting. You can see it on page 44 of the cream notebook. The handwriting is precise and slightly slanted.*

*You are not a woman who cannot love. You are a woman who loves differently. The notebooks are proof: whatever you feel for Kabir, you feel it consistently, across every visit, without reference to the previous one. That kind of love — arising from the present moment, every time, without baggage — is not lesser. It is just more visible.*

*The boxes are all in the corner. Amma's handwriting is on the spines.*

*It was a good day.*

*Goodnight.*

She closes the blue notebook. Drinks the last of the chai — cold now, but she doesn't mind. She sits for a moment in the silence of the room, the cardboard boxes in the corner, the little wooden shelf with its three notebooks and its three red pens and its laminated card.

Tomorrow she will wake and read the card and not remember this.

But she will have this.

### Chapter Six — Dr. Rangan Explains the Dark Void

She showed Priya the scans in the fourth year of treatment.

Not for any medical reason — the progression had largely stabilized, there was nothing new to see. She showed them because Priya had asked, months ago, and then forgotten asking, and then asked again, and then forgotten. Dr. Rangan had noted the repeated request in her file and decided: she keeps asking. She should see.

"The white areas," Dr. Rangan said, setting the first scan on the light box. "This is from November 2022. The month after the encephalitis."

Priya looked at the white areas. Irregular, scattered through the medial temporal region. They looked like — she thought, with the literary mind she still had — like snow falling in a dark room. Random. Accumulating.

"That's inflammation," Dr. Rangan said. "The virus was still active. The immune system was fighting. The white shows activity — but the wrong kind of activity."

She placed the second scan beside the first. Three years later. Same patient. Same brain. 

The right temporal lobe was dark.

Not damaged, not injured — absent. A void where tissue had been. Where the hippocampus and surrounding structures had degraded to the point that the scan could no longer distinguish them from surrounding fluid.

"That's what happened," Dr. Rangan said. Simply. Not gently — directly. "The degeneration progressed from inflammation to atrophy. The hippocampus and parts of the surrounding cortex." She pointed. "This is why you can't form new declarative memories. The machinery is gone."

Priya looked at the dark void for a long time.

"It doesn't look like anything," she said finally. "In the first scan, the white looks like — it looks active, at least. This one just looks like absence."

"Yes."

"Will it progress further?"

"Slowly. The major damage is done." Dr. Rangan paused. "The areas most critical to declarative memory formation are gone. Further degeneration will be at the margins — it may affect other things, eventually. We watch and we adjust."

Priya was still looking at the second scan. "I find this — I find this easier to look at than I expected," she said. "The void." She paused. "I think I expected it to be frightening. But it's just — it's just what is. The absence is what I live in. The void is where the notebooks happen instead."

Dr. Rangan said nothing. She had learned, in four years, when to let the silence work.

"Can I take a photograph of it?" Priya asked.

"The scan?"

"I want to put a photograph of it in my notebook. So I know — so future-me knows — that the thing I'm working around is this visible. This real. Not a deficit in character. Not carelessness. This." She gestured at the dark void. "A specific, documented, visible absence."

Dr. Rangan photographed the second scan with Priya's phone and watched her write the caption in the cream notebook, in red:

*This is the inside of my head. The dark part is the part that should hold your memories. It is gone. The notebooks are what live there instead. They are doing the job the dark part cannot. They are doing it well.*

### Chapter Seven — Nandita at Three in the Morning

Nandita knows the one thing Priya doesn't know about herself.

She knows it because she was there — in the hospital, in the first weeks, before the notebook system, when Priya was waking in the night and panicking and there was no protocol yet, no laminated card, no shelf with three notebooks in three colours. Just a woman waking up not knowing where or when or who, and a best friend sleeping on a chair in the corner.

Nandita was the first one to say: *It's okay.* 

Not as a phrase. Not as a protocol. As a response — instinctive, immediate, from the gut — to a person in terror. She said it and reached out and held Priya's hand and let her own steadiness conduct itself through the grip, and she watched Priya's breathing slow, and she thought: *She can't remember that I'm here. But she can feel that someone is.*

That was the beginning of the methodology.

What Nandita knows — what Priya doesn't know — is that in those early weeks, Priya cried about Kabir before Kabir existed. Not Kabir specifically. About the idea of Kabir. About the category of person he would turn out to represent. She cried, in the third week, at three in the morning, and said: *I will never be loved now. No one will want to know someone they have to introduce themselves to every time. No one will do that.*

Nandita said: *You don't know that.* 

Priya said: *I know it. It's the kind of thing you know without needing to remember.*

Nandita held her hand. Said nothing else. Put this conversation in a private place inside herself that has no notebook equivalent, a place that Nandita's own intact hippocampus stores in exactly the way Priya's cannot: as a lived, felt, embodied memory. The warmth of the grip. The specific quality of crying that is also, underneath, the assertion of grief — proof that something real has been lost.

Now, three years later, at three in the morning on a night when she can't sleep, Nandita takes out her phone and looks at a photograph. It's from two Thursdays ago. Kabir and Priya, side by side on the veranda, Priya with the cream notebook open, Kabir reading something she's written. Both of them leaning slightly inward without knowing it.

Nandita thinks about Priya in the hospital saying: *No one will do that.*

She puts her phone down. She lies in the dark.

She thinks: *Someone did. You just don't know it was the thing you feared wouldn't happen.*

She thinks: *That's the part that would most delight you. If you could know. If the knowing could cross the gap.*

She thinks: *You don't need to know. The thing happened anyway. You don't have to know a good thing happened for it to have happened.*

Then she sleeps.

### Chapter Eight — What Love Is When Memory Isn't

On a Tuesday afternoon in July, Kabir sat with Priya in the Sharma kitchen while rain dismantled the afternoon outside.

Leela was somewhere in the house — they could hear her, occasionally, the soft domestic sounds of a person existing nearby without intruding. Nandita was at work.

Priya had been writing in the cream notebook when the power cut came — something the monsoon does to Hyderabad, a reminder that modernity is a suggestion. The house went dim. Priya put her pen down. The dim light gathered on the kitchen table between them.

"In the dark," Priya said, "I can't write."

"No."

"I don't like it." Stated matter-of-factly. "Not the dark. The not-writing."

"I know."

"My notes from the next few minutes will be very thin," she said. "When the lights come back. I'll write: *there was a power cut, we sat in the kitchen, Kabir was there.* And that will be the entirety of — of whatever this is."

She gestured at the rain, the kitchen, the space between them.

"Whatever this is," he repeated.

She looked at him in the low monsoon light — the grey of it, diffused. "The notes will say you were there. They won't have the rain. Or this." She put her hand on the table, not reaching — just present. "The texture of moments is the thing that doesn't survive the writing."

"Nothing survives the writing fully," he said. "For anyone. Memory does that too. The texture bleeds out. You're left with — the outline."

"You remember in outlines?"

"Sometimes. The really important things." He turned his cup in his hands — the habit. "My father's death. I remember the hospital room and his hands. I don't remember what it sounded like."

"The notebooks are outlines too," she said slowly. "That's what I'm realizing, sitting here in the dark. I thought they were a substitute for memory. But they're an outline, the same as your memory is an outline." She paused. "We're all working from outlines."

He looked at her. The rain made its enormous sound against the window.

"Priya," he said.

"Yes."

"I want to come here every Tuesday. And Thursday. And — eventually — every day. That's what I want." He said it plainly, the way he said things that he'd decided. "I'm telling you now. I'll write it down. I'll write it in the margin again if you want. But I'm also saying it now, in the dark where you can't write it, because I want this to be the kind of thing you know in your body before your notebook has to explain it to you."

She was very still.

"In my body," she repeated.

"Your body knows things," he said. "Your body knows Nandita is safe. Before you read the page, before you check the notes. That smile you give her — it's there before you read. I've watched it."

"The emotional memory," she said. "The limbic system. Dr. Rangan explained it."

"Whatever explains it," he said. "I want to be — I want to be in that." He paused. "Not just in the notebook. In the part of you that the notebook can't hold."

The power came back. The light returned.

Priya picked up her pen. She wrote in the cream notebook, in red ink, quickly, while the feeling was still in the room:

*He said he wants to be in the part of me that the notebook can't hold. Write this carefully. Don't lose this one. This is what he said in the dark, in the rain, in the kitchen.*

*I think I am already there.*

She underlined it. Then she looked up at him.

"I wrote it down," she said.

"Good."

"But I also —" she put the pen flat on the notebook. "I also know it right now. Without the notebook. This is what knowing feels like when it's happening."

"Is it different?" he asked.

"Yes," she said. "It's — it's warm."

Outside, the rain continued its enormous patient work, and in the kitchen, two people sat with the kind of warmth that doesn't require a witness or a record, that exists, complete, in the moment of its happening, which is the only place anything has ever truly existed.

Priya would not remember this.

But tonight, in the dark blue notebook, she would write the best summary of her life.
